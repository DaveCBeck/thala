# When oceans turned toxic

# When Oceans Turned Toxic

The biomarker isorenieratene shouldn't be there. This organic compound, a molecular fossil from photosynthetic green sulfur bacteria, has a very specific requirement for existence: it needs both sunlight and hydrogen sulfide. In modern oceans, those two things almost never coexist—hydrogen sulfide is the toxic byproduct of bacterial respiration in oxygen-free waters, and it typically accumulates only in the deep darkness of the ocean floor. Yet across multiple continental shelf sites during the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum, roughly 56 million years ago, scientists have found isorenieratene preserved in the sediment record [@C5A4BTTB].

What this tells us is remarkable and unsettling: during the PETM, toxic, sulfide-rich waters rose high enough in the water column to reach sunlit surface layers. The photic zone—where marine photosynthesis occurs, where the base of the food web lives—became poisonous.

## The Geography of Suffocation

To understand why this happened, and why it matters, we need to think about oxygen in the ocean not as a uniform property but as a spatially complex landscape shaped by circulation, temperature, productivity, and basin geometry.

During the PETM, global temperatures rose approximately 5-8°C within a few thousand years, driven by massive carbon release into the atmosphere [@TBYRHSCY]. This warming had direct physical consequences for ocean oxygen. Warm water holds less dissolved oxygen than cold water—a straightforward physical constraint. A 5°C temperature increase would reduce oxygen solubility by roughly 15-20%, pushing already hypoxic regions toward complete anoxia [@C5A4BTTB]. But the warming did more than just reduce oxygen's carrying capacity. It intensified stratification of the water column, slowing the ventilation of deep waters. It accelerated metabolic rates of bacteria decomposing organic matter, increasing oxygen consumption. And it amplified the hydrological cycle, increasing nutrient runoff from continents and fueling productivity blooms that further drew down oxygen as the excess organic matter sank and decayed.

The result was not a uniformly anoxic ocean—such a thing has rarely if ever existed in Earth history—but rather a dramatic expansion and intensification of oxygen minimum zones, particularly along continental margins [@7U76VLJY]. What makes the PETM distinctive is the sheer variety of settings where deoxygenation reached extreme states, and the progression in some locations from merely low-oxygen conditions to fully euxinic ones, where hydrogen sulfide accumulated in the water column.

## Reading the Redox Record

How do we know sulfidic conditions invaded surface waters? The evidence comes from multiple independent geochemical systems, each telling part of the story.

In the North Sea, high-resolution records from well E-8X capture the transition with remarkable precision [@YFSXQJH4]. Pyrite framboid diameters—tiny spherical iron sulfide crystals that form in the water column under specific redox conditions—shift from larger sizes typical of suboxic conditions to mean diameters below 5 micrometers, characteristic of euxinic waters. Molybdenum concentrations spike at the carbon isotope excursion onset, as this element gets rapidly sequestered into sediments under sulfidic conditions. And molybdenum isotope ratios (δ⁹⁸/⁹⁵Mo) shift from light values around -0.8‰ to heavy values approaching 2‰, indicating a fundamental change in the geochemical pathway by which molybdenum enters the sediment [@YFSXQJH4].

The molybdenum isotope story is particularly revealing. In oxic marine settings, molybdenum gets slowly absorbed onto manganese oxide particles, producing a large isotopic fractionation that leaves sediments with light values. Under strongly euxinic conditions with dissolved sulfide exceeding about 11 micromoles per liter, molybdenum is converted to thiomolybdate and rapidly removed from seawater with minimal fractionation, preserving something close to the seawater isotope signature [@YFSXQJH4]. The heavy values seen in PETM sediments indicate quantitative removal of molybdenum under strongly sulfidic conditions.

Along the Gulf Coastal Plain of North America, the story takes an even more extreme turn. Here, the presence of isorenieratene in PETM sediments from the Harrell Core indicates that euxinia extended into the photic zone—the upper ~100 meters where light penetrates [@C5A4BTTB]. The green sulfur bacteria that produce isorenieratene are obligate anaerobes requiring hydrogen sulfide as an electron donor for photosynthesis. Finding their biomarkers means finding waters where sulfide and sunlight coexisted.

The peculiar thing is that the same sediments containing isorenieratene also contain benthic foraminiferal linings and in situ-formed glauconite—both requiring at least periodic oxygenation at the seafloor [@C5A4BTTB]. This paradox suggests intermittent, probably seasonal, development of photic zone euxinia. The PETM wasn't a uniform toxic state but a dynamic oscillation between conditions, with sulfidic waters periodically expanding into surface layers then retreating.

## Why Some Regions Suffered More

The spatial heterogeneity of PETM deoxygenation reflects the complex interplay of factors that determine where and when oxygen disappears from seawater. Not all ocean regions experienced equal stress.

Basin restriction played a crucial role. In the North Sea and Norwegian-Greenland Seaway, geological evidence indicates that the North Atlantic Igneous Province was actively uplifting, partially isolating these basins from open ocean circulation [@YFSXQJH4]. Restricted basins are particularly vulnerable to deoxygenation because they lack the ventilation that open ocean connection provides. The Mo/TOC ratio—the amount of molybdenum preserved per unit of organic carbon—provides a proxy for basin restriction. High ratios indicate access to the large marine molybdenum reservoir; declining ratios suggest progressive depletion of a limited pool as euxinic conditions persist without renewal [@YFSXQJH4].

In the North Sea, Mo/TOC values peak at the carbon isotope excursion onset, then progressively decline through the main phase of the PETM, consistent with drawdown of aqueous molybdenum from a restricted, incompletely renewed basin [@YFSXQJH4]. This basin reservoir effect tells us that the North Sea was effectively cut off from the open Atlantic, transforming into something resembling a supersized Black Sea analogue—a semi-enclosed basin with limited deep water renewal and a propensity for sulfide accumulation.

Productivity also shaped the oxygen landscape. Enhanced continental weathering during the PETM, driven by the accelerated hydrological cycle, delivered more nutrients to coastal waters. Evidence from barium records suggests elevated productivity in many shelf settings [@C5A4BTTB]. More primary production means more organic matter sinking to depth, more bacterial respiration, and more oxygen consumption. Under normal circumstances, this productivity-driven oxygen demand would be balanced by ventilation. But in a warmer, more stratified, and more restricted ocean, the balance tips toward deoxygenation.

The timing of different environmental changes varied by location. In some settings, redox changes preceded the full development of the carbon isotope excursion, suggesting local factors like basin restriction created preconditions for anoxia that then worsened as global warming intensified [@YFSXQJH4]. In others, productivity-driven deoxygenation followed the initial warming as the hydrological cycle spun up. This temporal heterogeneity complicates any simple narrative about PETM ocean chemistry and highlights the importance of high-resolution, multi-proxy reconstructions from different basin settings.

## The Phosphorus Connection

One of the most consequential aspects of widespread anoxia involves nutrient cycling, particularly phosphorus. On geological timescales, phosphorus is likely the ultimate limiting nutrient for marine productivity—unlike nitrogen, which can be fixed from the atmosphere by specialized microbes, phosphorus must be weathered from rocks and delivered to the ocean.

Under oxic conditions, sediments effectively sequester phosphorus, binding it to iron oxide particles and burying it. But when bottom waters lose oxygen, this iron-oxide trap breaks down, and phosphorus regenerates back into the water column [@C5A4BTTB]. The result is a positive feedback: anoxia releases phosphorus, phosphorus fuels productivity, productivity drives oxygen consumption, which intensifies anoxia, releasing more phosphorus. This phosphorus regeneration feedback helps explain the persistence of eutrophic, oxygen-depleted conditions in many PETM shelf settings for tens of thousands of years.

The residence time of phosphorus in the modern ocean is estimated at 10,000-40,000 years [@C5A4BTTB]. If widespread anoxia substantially reduced the efficiency of phosphorus burial during the PETM, the elevated nutrient availability could have persisted for a significant fraction of the event's duration. This timescale matches the observed persistence of euxinic indicators in some records, suggesting that nutrient feedbacks, not just direct temperature effects, maintained the anomalous redox state.

Nitrogen cycling also shifted. Anoxic waters support denitrification and anaerobic ammonium oxidation (anammox), which convert fixed nitrogen back to atmospheric N₂. Evidence from some PETM settings suggests enhanced nitrogen fixation compensated for these losses, maintaining high productivity despite nitrogen loss pathways [@C5A4BTTB]. The combination of enhanced phosphorus regeneration and compensatory nitrogen fixation may have sustained the eutrophic conditions that contributed to oxygen depletion.

## Beyond the Sea: Freshwater Catastrophe

Marine systems, with their large volume and chemical buffering capacity, experienced severe but survivable stress during the PETM. Freshwater ecosystems tell a more extreme story.

Lacustrine records from the Jianghan Basin in eastern China capture temperature increases of approximately 11°C—far exceeding the global average warming—coincident with the characteristic carbon isotope excursion [@NVB36TDB]. This amplified continental warming pushed freshwater systems toward their thermal limits and beyond. Lakes have far less buffering capacity than oceans and are more susceptible to rapid stratification and deoxygenation.

The ecological consequences were catastrophic. Mass mortalities of both terrestrial and aquatic animals are recorded in PETM lacustrine sediments, with taphonomic and geochemical evidence pointing toward toxigenic cyanobacterial blooms as the proximate cause [@NVB36TDB]. These blooms, fueled by warming and nutrient delivery, produced toxins lethal to vertebrates while simultaneously driving down oxygen levels. The pattern echoes modern eutrophication crises, where warming and nutrient loading combine to create dead zones and harmful algal events.

Freshwater ecosystems thus provide a window onto the most extreme environmental stresses generated by the PETM. Their sensitivity to temperature change and their vulnerability to eutrophication-driven toxicity made them early casualties of the hyperthermal. The mass mortality events documented in China suggest that the PETM was not merely an episode of ecosystem reorganization but one of genuine ecological catastrophe in vulnerable settings.

## What the Data Don't Tell Us

Despite the wealth of evidence for ocean deoxygenation during the PETM, important uncertainties remain.

First, the absolute depth and lateral extent of photic zone euxinia are difficult to constrain. Finding isorenieratene tells us that sulfide reached the photic zone, but it doesn't specify at what depth or over what geographic area. Green sulfur bacteria can live at relatively low light levels, so their presence indicates euxinia within roughly the upper 100-150 meters, but the precise boundary between oxic and euxinic waters remains uncertain.

Second, the contribution of different forcing mechanisms—thermal stratification, nutrient loading, basin restriction, circulation changes—to deoxygenation likely varied among settings, but quantifying their relative importance is challenging. Models can simulate individual mechanisms, but the observational constraints for validating such models are sparse, particularly for open ocean settings where sedimentation rates are lower and records less complete.

Third, the relationship between deoxygenation and biotic impacts remains incompletely understood. The PETM is associated with the largest benthic foraminiferal extinction of the Cenozoic, with approximately 50% of species disappearing [@C5A4BTTB]. But disentangling the roles of temperature, oxygen, carbonate saturation, and habitat loss in driving this extinction is difficult. Different organisms have different tolerances, and the spatial heterogeneity of environmental change means that refugia probably existed even during the worst of the event.

## The Modern Parallel

The PETM deoxygenation narrative carries uncomfortable resonance for the present. Modern ocean oxygen content has declined by roughly 2% since 1960, driven by warming and changing circulation [@C5A4BTTB]. Coastal dead zones associated with nutrient pollution and warming have expanded dramatically—from fewer than 50 documented sites in the 1960s to over 400 today. And projections under continued warming suggest substantial further expansion of oxygen minimum zones and increased frequency of coastal hypoxia.

The PETM shows what can happen when warming, nutrient delivery, and stratification combine over thousands of years. The timescales differ from modern change, but the underlying processes—reduced oxygen solubility, enhanced stratification, productivity-driven oxygen consumption, phosphorus regeneration feedbacks—are the same. The PETM reached states, including photic zone euxinia, that are unknown in the modern ocean. Whether anthropogenic change could push modern systems toward similar extremes depends on the rate and magnitude of future warming and the resilience of ocean circulation patterns that currently ventilate oxygen minimum zones.

The geochemical proxies that reveal PETM conditions—molybdenum isotopes, pyrite framboid sizes, isorenieratene biomarkers—provide a template for what to look for if modern deoxygenation intensifies. They also remind us that ocean chemistry can cross thresholds, transitioning rapidly from low-oxygen to sulfidic states once critical conditions are met. The PETM ocean didn't gradually shade from blue to purple; in many settings, the transition to euxinia was geologically abrupt, occurring within the resolution limits of the sedimentary record.

## Questions That Remain

Several fundamental questions about PETM deoxygenation remain open for future investigation.

How far into the open ocean did euxinic conditions extend? Most evidence for photic zone euxinia comes from shelf and marginal settings, where restricted circulation and productivity both favor deoxygenation. Whether the open ocean experienced similar conditions, or remained a refuge of better oxygenation, has implications for both ecosystem survival and carbon cycling.

What was the feedback between deoxygenation and carbon burial? Anoxic and euxinic conditions generally favor organic carbon preservation, meaning that expanded oxygen-depleted zones could have enhanced carbon burial, acting as a negative feedback on atmospheric CO₂. Quantifying this feedback requires better constraints on both the spatial extent of deoxygenation and the organic carbon burial rates in affected settings.

How quickly did oxygenation recover after the peak of the PETM, and what controlled that recovery? Records from the North Sea suggest that bottom waters never fully recovered to pre-PETM oxic conditions for at least 140,000 years [@YFSXQJH4]. Understanding why recovery was slow—whether due to persistent basin restriction, continued nutrient regeneration, or other factors—matters for predicting recovery timescales from any future deoxygenation event.

The PETM's toxic waters represent one of the most severe biogeochemical responses to a rapid warming event in the geological record. The evidence shows that ocean chemistry can reorganize dramatically and rapidly under greenhouse forcing, with consequences that cascade through ecosystems from benthic communities to surface waters to freshwater lakes. The event demonstrates both the vulnerability of marine systems to warming-related stresses and the spatial complexity of environmental response. Perhaps most importantly, it reveals that some environmental changes, once initiated, can persist for tens of thousands of years through internal feedbacks—a sobering reminder that the consequences of rapid carbon release extend far beyond the initial perturbation.
