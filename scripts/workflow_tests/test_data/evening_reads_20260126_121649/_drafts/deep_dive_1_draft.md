# The drilling ship and the smoking gun

# The Drilling Ship and the Smoking Gun

In the summer of 2021, the drilling vessel *JOIDES Resolution* positioned itself over an unremarkable patch of seafloor in the Norwegian Sea, roughly 350 kilometers from the coast. The target lay buried beneath 1,660 meters of sediment: a funnel-shaped crater, 80 meters deep and perhaps 200 meters wide, that had been identified years earlier on seismic surveys. To the geologists aboard, this was no ordinary hole in the ancient seafloor. It was a crime scene, frozen in time for 56 million years.

The crater was one of thousands of hydrothermal vent complexes scattered across the North Atlantic margins—the explosive exhaust pipes of a volcanic province so vast it would eventually split apart a supercontinent. And according to a growing body of evidence, these vents had belched greenhouse gases into the atmosphere at precisely the moment when Earth experienced one of the most dramatic warming events in the geological record. The question was whether the drilling team could prove it.

What they found, after punching five boreholes through the crater and its infill, would transform a decades-old hypothesis from a plausible speculation into something approaching a smoking gun. The sediments filling that ancient crater contained the unmistakable chemical fingerprints of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum—including the diagnostic dinoflagellate cyst *Apectodinium augustum*, which appears globally at the onset of the warming event. The vent, they concluded, had formed and begun filling just millennia before the planet tipped into hyperthermal conditions [@SFHXHQXJ]. Here, at last, was direct physical evidence linking volcanic activity to climate catastrophe.

## The Stakes of the Volcanic Hypothesis

The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum has long represented both a scientific puzzle and a cautionary tale. Around 56 million years ago, global temperatures spiked by 5-6°C in what appears to have been geological fast-forward—perhaps just a few thousand years. The warming persisted for roughly 150,000 to 200,000 years, leaving its signature in sediments worldwide as a sharp negative excursion in carbon isotopes that signals the injection of vast quantities of isotopically light carbon into the ocean-atmosphere system [@47YBCEFM].

The question of where that carbon came from has spawned competing hypotheses for decades. Methane hydrates destabilizing on continental margins. Permafrost releasing ancient organic carbon. Even a comet impact. But one candidate has always loomed particularly large: the North Atlantic Igneous Province, a volcanic system that would eventually produce 6-10 million cubic kilometers of magma as it tore apart the landmass that would become Greenland and Europe [@LVFPSGAY].

The temporal coincidence between peak NAIP activity and the PETM was recognized early on. High-precision dating of East Greenland flood basalts places massive volcanism at 56.1 ± 0.4 million years ago, essentially synchronous with the warming event [@INWM2JEG]. But temporal coincidence alone proves nothing—the geological record is littered with events that happened at roughly the same time without being causally connected. What investigators needed was a direct mechanistic link, ideally preserved in the physical evidence of volcanic activity itself.

That link has now been established through multiple independent lines of evidence, converging on a picture that is both more complex and more compelling than the original volcanic hypothesis suggested. The smoking gun, it turns out, is not a single weapon but an arsenal—and the Modgunn Vent drilled by IODP Expedition 396 is just one piece of the evidence.

## Mercury Anomalies: The Volcanic Fingerprint

Before the drilling ships arrived, geochemists had already begun building the circumstantial case through sedimentary mercury. Volcanic eruptions are among the dominant natural sources of mercury to the atmosphere; during prolonged periods of enhanced volcanism, this elevated mercury flux should leave an imprint in marine sediments where organic matter preferentially complexes with the metal. Normalizing mercury concentrations to total organic carbon (Hg/TOC) helps control for variations in organic carbon burial, making anomalously high ratios a potential proxy for volcanic activity [@LVFPSGAY].

The results from PETM sections paint a striking geographic pattern. In the North Sea, at the Grane field located less than 700 kilometers from known NAIP source volcanoes, mercury concentrations reached astonishing levels—up to 90,100 ppb, with Hg/TOC ratios of 95,700 ppb wt%⁻¹ [@LVFPSGAY]. For context, the extreme mercury anomalies measured across the Permian-Triassic boundary in Svalbard—associated with the devastating Siberian Traps eruptions—peaked at just 680 ppb. The Grane values are more than 100 times higher.

Moving outward from the NAIP, the anomalies diminish but remain detectable. Danish sections show Hg/TOC anomalies up to 324 ppb wt%⁻¹, while Svalbard records reach 257 ppb wt%⁻¹ [@LVFPSGAY]. Crucially, these enrichments are not randomly distributed through the stratigraphy. They cluster at specific intervals: just prior to the PETM onset, and during the recovery phase—precisely the intervals where ash layers are most abundant in the geological record [@47YBCEFM].

This temporal and spatial pattern carries important mechanistic implications. Mercury released during subaerial volcanic eruptions disperses broadly through the atmosphere, potentially leaving a global signature. But mercury released into seawater—whether from submarine eruptions or hydrothermal venting—would be captured more locally, given the relatively short residence time of mercury in ocean waters compared to the mixing time of the global ocean [@LVFPSGAY]. The pronounced proximity effect suggests that submarine emissions, potentially from the very hydrothermal vent complexes later drilled by Expedition 396, dominated the NAIP mercury flux.

## Osmium Isotopes and the Mantle Signature

Mercury anomalies reveal the intensity of volcanic activity, but osmium isotopes provide information about its character. The ratio of ¹⁸⁷Os to ¹⁸⁸Os in seawater reflects the mixing of two end-members: radiogenic osmium from continental weathering (¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os ≈ 1.40) and unradiogenic osmium from mantle sources (¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os ≈ 0.13), including basalt weathering and submarine hydrothermal activity [@47YBCEFM]. A shift toward unradiogenic values in the sedimentary record can signal enhanced input from volcanic sources.

The osmium record across the PETM reveals a remarkable pre-event signal. In Svalbard, Denmark, and North Atlantic localities, ¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os ratios drop sharply in sediments deposited just before the carbon isotope excursion begins, reaching values as low as 0.27—well below the late Paleocene baseline of approximately 0.4 [@47YBCEFM; @LVFPSGAY]. This "pre-CIE" osmium anomaly, occurring roughly 18,000-23,000 years before the main warming event based on sedimentation rate estimates, suggests that NAIP activity ramped up significantly before the climate system reached its tipping point.

Throughout the body of the PETM itself, osmium isotopes remain remarkably stable in Danish sections, with ¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os(i) values between 0.30 and 0.39 persisting across approximately 24 meters of stratigraphy [@47YBCEFM]. This sustained unradiogenic signature indicates continuous volcanic input throughout the hyperthermal interval—not a single pulse of eruptions followed by quiescence, but ongoing magmatic activity that persisted for the duration of the carbon isotope excursion.

The combination of mercury and osmium data points to a specific scenario: NAIP volcanic activity intensified in the latest Paleocene, reached a peak during the PETM body, and then declined considerably during the recovery phase [@47YBCEFM]. The acme of magmatic activity, dominated by flood basalt volcanism and thermogenic degassing from contact metamorphism, was likely confined to just roughly 200,000 years spanning approximately 56.0 to 55.8 million years ago.

## The Drilling Evidence: Anatomy of a Vent

The Modgunn Vent drilled during IODP Expedition 396 provides the most direct window into the mechanics of NAIP carbon release. The structure itself is diagnostic: a funnel-shaped crater approximately 80 meters deep relative to the surrounding paleoseafloor, connected to a feeder system extending 400 meters down to a sill complex at depth [@SFHXHQXJ]. The shape and aspect ratio closely resemble both maar-diatreme volcanoes formed by phreatomagmatic explosions and modern blowout craters from drilling accidents—indicating rapid initial formation through catastrophic fluid overpressure release rather than gradual seepage [@SFHXHQXJ].

The infill tells the story of what happened next. Laminated diatomite and graded ash beds suggest rapid, largely undisturbed sedimentation following the initial explosion—the crater was filling quickly, not experiencing ongoing venting. But the timing of this infill is what makes the discovery so significant. The PETM-marker dinoflagellate *Apectodinium augustum* appears in the crater fill, along with a characteristic negative carbon isotope excursion of approximately 2‰ compared to underlying strata [@SFHXHQXJ]. The vent formed and began receiving sediment at essentially the same moment the global climate system was crossing into hyperthermal conditions.

High-resolution sampling allows for more precise constraints. The carbon isotope excursion onset occurs 11-15 meters above the vent base in boreholes on the crater margin, suggesting the vent formed "probably only a few millennia before the PETM onset" based on the exceptionally high sediment accumulation rates [@SFHXHQXJ]. Intriguingly, this early infill is missing near the crater center, which could indicate that sediments could not accumulate there until around millennia after crater formation due to ongoing hydrothermal activity.

The palynological assemblages in the vent fill provide another crucial piece of evidence: the setting was shallow marine, likely less than 30 meters depth below wave base but demonstrating coastal conditions [@SFHXHQXJ]. This shallow-water setting has profound implications for climate forcing. When volcanic or thermogenic gases vent into deep water, the overlying water column acts as a filter—methane is efficiently oxidized to CO₂ before reaching the atmosphere. But shallow-water venting allows gases to bypass this oxidation pathway, delivering methane directly to the atmosphere where it exerts a warming effect roughly 25 times greater than CO₂ on decadal timescales [@SFHXHQXJ].

## The Contact Metamorphism Amplifier

The vent complexes were not simply volcanic chimneys. Their connection to sill intrusions at depth points to a second, potentially more important carbon source: thermogenic methane generated through contact metamorphism of organic-rich sediments.

When magma intrudes into sedimentary rock, the heat drives rapid reactions in the surrounding material. Organic carbon that accumulated over millions of years can be converted to methane and CO₂ within centuries or millennia as the thermal aureole expands around the intrusion [@LVFPSGAY]. The gases generated by this "cooking" process build up pressure in the sediment until they find a release pathway—typically the explosive hydrothermal vents that terminate at the edges of sill complexes. Modeling suggests that individual sills produce thermogenic gases over timescales of hundreds to tens of thousands of years, with vent structures serving as conduits for multiple ejections during their active lifetimes [@LVFPSGAY].

Seismic surveys have identified over 700 potential hydrothermal vent complexes on the Norwegian margin alone [@SFHXHQXJ], with additional systems documented in the Faroe-Shetland basin and on the Northeast Greenland margin. Approximately 95% of these vents terminate at the horizon between Paleocene and Eocene strata, directly implicating them in events at the PETM [@LVFPSGAY]. Estimates for total carbon release from Norwegian margin vents alone range from 225 to 2,250 Gt C, with potential degassing across the entire NAIP-affected area reaching perhaps 13,000 Gt C [@47YBCEFM].

The thermogenic pathway offers a solution to a persistent puzzle about the PETM: the carbon isotope excursion is too large to explain with volcanic CO₂ alone unless implausibly large magmatic volumes are invoked, yet methane sources must be isotopically very light (strongly ¹²C-enriched) to match the observed excursion with realistic carbon masses. Thermogenic methane from kerogen maturation fits this requirement—it carries the depleted isotopic signature of organic carbon while being released through mechanisms directly tied to magmatic activity. The volcanic trigger did not need to supply all the carbon directly; it may have mobilized carbon that had accumulated in sediments over tens of millions of years.

## Quantifying the Volcanic Carbon Flux

How much carbon did NAIP activity actually contribute to the PETM? The question demands integration of multiple constraints, and recent work has attempted exactly that synthesis.

Mercury accumulation rates provide one metric. During the PETM body in Danish sections, Hg(a) values reached 1.76 ng cm⁻² yr⁻¹—an order of magnitude higher than rates during the onset interval and well above Holocene background [@47YBCEFM]. If this rate applied across the entire North Sea basin, approximately 8.8 tonnes of mercury would have been deposited annually, representing 1.3-12% of modern global volcanic mercury emissions despite the basin covering just 0.1% of Earth's surface area. Scaled appropriately, these numbers suggest sustained, voluminous volcanic emissions throughout the hyperthermal interval.

The character of NAIP magmas themselves offers additional constraints. Trace element-gas correlations indicate that CO₂ concentrations in Large Igneous Province tholeiitic magmas vary substantially, ranging from roughly 1,080 to 7,471 ppm depending on the specific province and its mantle source characteristics [@ASJD2TES]. Critically, CO₂ concentrations and CO₂/S ratios vary by one to two orders of magnitude even within individual LIP magma suites, reflecting both mantle source heterogeneity and temporal evolution as magma compositions change through an eruptive episode [@ASJD2TES].

A comprehensive assessment integrating geochemical and paleoenvironmental proxies concludes that volcanic carbon likely constituted the dominant source during the PETM [@JUNM4JH9]. But this does not mean direct volcanic CO₂ emissions alone drove the warming. The evidence increasingly points to a more nuanced scenario: volcanic activity provided both the direct carbon emissions and the heat source for thermogenic carbon mobilization, with the shallow-water setting of many vents maximizing the atmospheric forcing by allowing methane to bypass water-column oxidation.

## The Complicated Aftermath

If NAIP volcanism triggered the PETM, why didn't the event end when volcanic activity waned? This question has troubled investigators since the volcanic hypothesis was first proposed, because tephra layers and mercury anomalies suggest continued NAIP activity well into the post-PETM interval—long after the carbon isotope excursion had recovered.

The Danish sections are particularly instructive here. The Fur Formation contains over 170 distinct tephra horizons representing explosive NAIP volcanism, with the main phase of 140 ash layers beginning roughly 27 meters above the PETM recovery horizon [@LVFPSGAY]. These ash layers are found more than 1,200 kilometers from the nearest known source volcano, indicating eruptions of extraordinary violence—individual events potentially involving hundreds to thousands of cubic kilometers of magma [@LVFPSGAY]. Yet this intense explosive volcanism occurred after global temperatures had begun returning to pre-PETM levels.

The resolution to this paradox may lie in the changing style of NAIP emplacement. The proxy evidence suggests that volcanic activity shifted from effusive eruptions and thermogenic degassing during the PETM itself to more explosive phreatomagmatic activity in the early Eocene as the developing rift system allowed greater magma-water interactions [@47YBCEFM]. Explosive eruptions produce impressive ash falls but may release volatiles less efficiently to the atmosphere than effusive eruptions or hydrothermal venting, particularly if eruption columns inject material into the stratosphere where it remains for shorter periods than tropospheric emissions.

There may also have been a fundamental change in the carbon budget. Contact metamorphism is a one-way process: once the organic carbon in a sedimentary basin has been "cooked" by sill intrusions, it cannot be cooked again. The earliest sill emplacements would have accessed the richest organic deposits; later intrusions would encounter already-depleted sediments. The explosive volcanism of the early Eocene may have represented impressive fireworks without the carbon payload of the earlier thermogenic phase.

## The Weathering Counterweight

The volcanic trigger hypothesis also illuminates a key negative feedback that may have ultimately terminated the warming. Flood basalt eruptions and widespread ash deposition dramatically increased the availability of fresh reactive silicate material at Earth's surface [@47YBCEFM]. Volcanic ash, with its extremely high surface-area-to-volume ratio, is particularly susceptible to chemical weathering—and silicate weathering is the primary long-term sink for atmospheric CO₂.

Lithium isotope data from Danish sections show large excursions that correlate with volcanic activity indicators, suggesting that ash deposits were indeed undergoing rapid weathering [@47YBCEFM]. Clay formation in volcanic ash can begin within months of deposition under modern conditions, and would likely have proceeded even faster in the warmer Eocene climate. The gradual negative δ⁷Li excursion observed in post-PETM strata coincides with both increased mercury content and the onset of the main tephra-rich interval, consistent with enhanced weathering of accumulating ash deposits.

In this view, the NAIP emplacement contained the seeds of its own climate stabilization. The same volcanic activity that released warming gases also spread reactive material across northern hemisphere landscapes, accelerating the silicate weathering thermostat that draws down atmospheric CO₂. The PETM may have persisted for 150,000 years not because carbon release continued unabated, but because that is roughly how long it takes for enhanced weathering to compensate for a major carbon injection.

## What We Still Don't Know

The drilling of hydrothermal vent complexes and the accumulation of geochemical proxy data have transformed the volcanic trigger hypothesis from speculation to well-supported theory. But significant uncertainties persist.

First, the relative contributions of direct volcanic CO₂, thermogenic methane, and potential feedback reservoirs like methane hydrates remain poorly constrained. The evidence establishes that NAIP activity was occurring at the right time and in the right places to drive warming, but cannot yet partition the total carbon release among different sources. The isotopic signature of the CIE suggests involvement of very light carbon—more consistent with thermogenic methane or hydrates than with volcanic CO₂—but the mixing proportions are difficult to determine.

Second, the geographic coverage of proxy data remains limited. Most high-resolution records come from North Atlantic and European sections; the global pattern of mercury anomalies and other volcanic tracers is still being mapped. The apparent absence of clear Hg/TOC anomalies at Lomonosov Ridge and Bass River localities [@LVFPSGAY] could reflect genuinely regional Hg deposition, or could result from the specific characteristics of those sections (incomplete recovery at Lomonosov, low TOC content at Bass River) that obscure the volcanic signal.

Third, the precise timing of different carbon release mechanisms remains uncertain at the millennial scale relevant to climate forcing. Did thermogenic methane release precede, coincide with, or follow the main phase of flood basalt eruption? The drilling evidence places vent formation just millennia before the CIE onset [@SFHXHQXJ], but "millennia" spans a wide range when climate feedbacks can be activated over centuries.

Finally, the relationship between NAIP-driven carbon release and potential feedback amplification through other reservoirs—particularly marine methane hydrates—remains to be fully elucidated. The emerging picture suggests that warming may have begun before the main carbon isotope excursion (see the companion article on pre-onset warming), which could indicate either that volcanic CO₂ drove initial warming that then destabilized hydrates, or that thermogenic methane release from contact metamorphism began before the main phase of carbon injection reached the ocean-atmosphere system.

What the drilling ship found in that buried crater was not simply confirmation of a hypothesis, but an invitation to more sophisticated questions. The smoking gun has been identified. Now the work of understanding exactly how it was fired—and whether there were accomplices—continues.
