# The drilling ship and the smoking gun

# The Drilling Ship and the Smoking Gun

In the summer of 2021, the research vessel *JOIDES Resolution* positioned itself over a peculiar depression on the seafloor of the Norwegian continental margin. The target was small—a crater roughly 80 meters deep and 200 meters across, now buried under millions of years of sediment. To the scientists aboard, this feature represented something extraordinary: the fossilized throat of a volcanic vent that had erupted 56 million years ago, at precisely the moment Earth's climate lurched into its most dramatic warming event of the past 66 million years.

What they found when the drill bits returned to the surface would provide the first physical evidence linking submarine volcanic venting to the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum—the geological event that has become our most instructive analog for understanding rapid climate change.

The problem of proving causation in Earth history is notoriously difficult. We can observe that two things happened at roughly the same time—massive volcanism in the North Atlantic and catastrophic global warming—but temporal coincidence is not causation. The challenge has always been finding the smoking gun: direct physical evidence that volcanic activity actually released greenhouse gases into the atmosphere at the right time, in the right way, to drive the climate system over a tipping point.

## The Vent Complex Problem

The North Atlantic Igneous Province is one of the largest volcanic events in Earth history. Between 63 and 54 million years ago, magma erupted across what would become Greenland, the British Isles, and the Norwegian margin, eventually emplacing somewhere between 6 and 10 million cubic kilometers of material [@47YBCEFM]. But the story of the PETM isn't simply about lava flows. Much of the magma never reached the surface. Instead, it spread horizontally through sedimentary basins as vast sheets called sills, intruding into organic-rich mudstones that had accumulated over millions of years.

When hot magma meets organic-rich sediment, chemistry happens. The heat cooks the organic matter, liberating methane and carbon dioxide in a process called contact metamorphism. This thermogenic carbon—isotopically light, enriched in carbon-12 relative to carbon-13—becomes a potent greenhouse forcing if it can escape to the atmosphere. The question has always been: did it actually escape, and if so, when?

Seismic surveys of the Norwegian continental margin had revealed tantalizing hints. More than 700 structures identified as potential hydrothermal vent complexes dot the mid-Norwegian margin alone [@SFHXHQXJ]. These features appear in seismic data as vertically oriented pipes connecting deep sill intrusions to crater-like depressions at what was once the seafloor. The geometry suggests explosive release—gas building beneath impermeable rock until overpressure caused catastrophic failure, blasting sediment and volatiles upward through hundreds of meters of overlying material.

But seismic images can only tell you so much. To establish timing, you need to get your hands on the rocks themselves.

## Drilling into the Past

The Modgunn Vent, as the target structure came to be known, sits on the Modgunn Arch offshore Norway, less than 10 kilometers from what was the rift axis during continental breakup. IODP Expedition 396 drilled five boreholes along a 500-meter transect from the crater rim toward its center, recovering over 540 meters of core with exceptional preservation rates exceeding 80% [@SFHXHQXJ].

What emerged from those cores was a remarkably complete record of the vent's birth and death. The crater infill consists of very dark grey to black diatomite—sediment rich in the silica shells of microscopic algae—interspersed with ubiquitous ash layers from ongoing volcanic eruptions in the region. The presence of these delicate, laminated sediments told the scientists something important: after its initial explosive formation, the crater filled relatively quietly, with limited fluid migration or disturbance. The sedimentary record was intact.

Here's where the detective work begins. To date the vent's formation, the team needed to identify exactly where in the geological timescale these sediments belonged. They turned to two independent methods: carbon isotope stratigraphy and dinoflagellate cyst biostratigraphy.

The PETM is marked globally by a sharp negative excursion in carbon isotope ratios—a 2 to 7 per mille drop in δ¹³C values reflecting the massive injection of isotopically light carbon into the ocean-atmosphere system. This carbon isotope excursion (CIE) provides a chemical fingerprint that can be recognized worldwide, from deep-sea sediments to terrestrial fossil soils. If the vent sediments recorded this excursion, they could be precisely tied to the PETM interval.

The second dating tool was more biological. Dinoflagellates are single-celled marine organisms that produce distinctive resting cysts preserved in sediments. Different species dominated at different times, and one in particular—*Apectodinium augustum*—is found exclusively during the PETM itself. Its first appearance marks the onset of the hyperthermal event with remarkable consistency across global sections [@SFHXHQXJ].

## The Smoking Gun Emerges

The results from the Modgunn cores were striking. Samples from the vent infill yielded abundant *Apectodinium augustum*, firmly placing deposition during the PETM. But more revealing was the vertical structure of the record. In the outer boreholes, 10 to 15 meters above the vent base, the team observed a sharp 6 per mille drop in organic carbon isotope values—the characteristic signal of the CIE onset [@SFHXHQXJ].

This stratigraphic relationship carries profound implications. The vent base lies below the CIE onset, meaning the crater formed before the carbon isotope excursion began. The overlying sediments, which contain the PETM marker species and the characteristic isotope signal, accumulated after formation. The vent erupted first, then the world warmed.

The timing appears remarkably tight. Using the exceptional sedimentation rates recorded in the crater—rapid enough to preserve millimeter-scale laminations—the team estimated that vent formation occurred only a few millennia before the PETM onset [@SFHXHQXJ]. In geological terms, this is essentially synchronous. The lag is shorter than the mixing time of the ocean, shorter than the resolution of most paleoclimate records, short enough that cause and effect become difficult to distinguish.

Crucially, the stratigraphic record near the center of the vent tells a slightly different story. At Site U1568A, closer to the dome structure marking the crater's heart, the earliest infill sediments are missing. The team interprets this absence as evidence that sediment could not accumulate in the central crater until thousands of years after formation, either because ongoing hydrothermal activity prevented deposition or because material slumped into the still-active vent center [@SFHXHQXJ]. The volcano, in other words, continued to breathe.

## The Shallow-Water Revelation

Perhaps the most significant finding from Expedition 396 concerns water depth. The drilling revealed that the Modgunn Vent erupted in very shallow water—possibly less than 30 meters, based on the coastal marine character of the fossil assemblages and the presence of an erosional unconformity indicating emergence [@SFHXHQXJ].

This matters enormously for climate forcing. When methane enters the deep ocean, most of it oxidizes to carbon dioxide before reaching the atmosphere. The water column acts as a filter, converting the more potent greenhouse gas to a less potent one. But in shallow water, or subaerial conditions, methane can reach the atmosphere directly. Given that methane has roughly 25 times the warming potential of CO₂ over decadal timescales, direct atmospheric injection produces far greater initial warming than an equivalent carbon release to deep water [@SFHXHQXJ].

The palynological evidence reinforces this interpretation. The organic microfossils recovered from the vent infill are overwhelmingly terrestrial—pollen, spores, and transported land plant material—mixed with dinoflagellate cysts showing coastal to restricted marine ecological affinities [@SFHXHQXJ]. The diatom assemblage tells the same story: dominantly coastal and neritic species, many associated with benthic or floating macroalgae typical of nearshore environments.

The Modgunn Arch sits close to the ocean-continent boundary of the nascent North Atlantic rift, suggesting that many other vent complexes were in equally shallow water or even above sea level. This interpretation is supported by palynological analysis of the only other drilled hydrothermal vent complex on the Norwegian margin, which shows similarly shallow, near-coastal conditions [@SFHXHQXJ; @LVFPSGAY]. The implication is that volatile emissions from these vents—including methane, carbon dioxide, and other gases generated by contact metamorphism—entered the atmosphere with minimal attenuation.

## Putting the Pieces Together

The Modgunn drilling provides the clearest evidence yet for the volcanic trigger hypothesis, but it doesn't stand alone. Multiple independent lines of evidence converge on the same conclusion: that North Atlantic volcanism preceded and accompanied the PETM in ways consistent with a causal relationship.

Mercury anomalies provide one such line of evidence. Volcanic eruptions are among the dominant natural sources of mercury to the environment, and elevated Hg concentrations in sediments can serve as a tracer for volcanic activity. Studies of PETM sections from around the North Atlantic reveal strong spatial variation in mercury enrichment, with the highest concentrations—reaching up to 90,100 ppb in the Grane field of the North Sea—occurring closest to the volcanic province [@LVFPSGAY]. The temporal distribution is equally telling: Hg/TOC anomalies cluster before the CIE onset and during the recovery phase, precisely when tephra layers indicate explosive volcanic activity [@47YBCEFM; @LVFPSGAY].

Mercury accumulation rates during the PETM body reached 1.76 ng cm⁻² yr⁻¹ in the eastern North Sea—an order of magnitude greater than during the onset phase and far exceeding modern volcanic contributions [@47YBCEFM]. This sustained elevation indicates continuous volcanic input throughout the hyperthermal interval, not merely a singular pulse.

Osmium isotope records tell a complementary story. Before the PETM onset, ocean waters shifted toward unradiogenic ¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os values—a signature of enhanced basalt weathering or submarine volcanism [@47YBCEFM; @LVFPSGAY]. In Svalbard, this osmium shift coincides with mercury anomalies predating the CIE by 18 to 23 thousand years, suggesting that volcanic activity was already influencing ocean chemistry before the main warming event [@LVFPSGAY].

## The Complexity of Causation

The Modgunn evidence is compelling, but the relationship between volcanism and climate during the PETM resists simple interpretation. One persistent puzzle is the mismatch between the timing of volcanic proxies and the carbon isotope excursion itself.

Studies from Denmark reveal that mercury anomalies and tephra layers are most abundant before the CIE onset and during its recovery, with remarkably stable Hg/TOC ratios through the body of the excursion when the most intense warming occurred [@47YBCEFM]. If volcanic activity drove the PETM, why does the volcanic signal appear to diminish during peak warmth?

Several explanations have been proposed. The style of NAIP emplacement may have shifted during the CIE, transitioning from explosive volcanism—which produces tephras and disperses mercury widely—to effusive eruptions and submarine sill intrusions that release carbon without the same atmospheric signatures [@47YBCEFM]. Alternatively, the extreme sedimentation rates and organic carbon burial during the PETM body may have diluted volcanic signals that were actually continuous.

A related complexity concerns the duration of warming compared to volcanic activity. The PETM lasted roughly 150,000 to 200,000 years, far longer than typical volcanic events. Recent work suggests that the acme of NAIP activity was concentrated in approximately 200,000 years between 56.0 and 55.8 Ma [@47YBCEFM], broadly coincident with the hyperthermal. But sustained warming requires either continuous forcing or slow-acting feedbacks, and disentangling these contributions remains challenging.

## What the Vents Tell Us

The Modgunn Vent drilling represents a methodological breakthrough as much as a scientific one. For decades, the volcanic trigger hypothesis relied on inference—matching ages between distant flood basalts and marine climate records, reconstructing gas generation from sill intrusion models, extrapolating from seismic images to degassing rates. Expedition 396 demonstrated that we can actually sample the volcanic features themselves, recovering datable sedimentary records that tie vent activity directly to paleoclimate events.

The crater shape and stratified infill of the Modgunn Vent suggest large-scale explosive gas release during initial formation, followed by rapid but undisturbed sedimentation [@SFHXHQXJ]. This pattern is consistent with models predicting that thermogenic gas generation occurs rapidly after sill intrusion, building to catastrophic release over hundreds to tens of thousands of years [@LVFPSGAY]. The physical evidence matches the theoretical predictions.

More broadly, the Modgunn results demonstrate that hydrothermal vent complexes were indeed active conduits for carbon release during the PETM. The shallow-water setting allowed efficient atmospheric forcing, and the tight temporal relationship between vent formation and CIE onset supports a causal link rather than mere correlation. While no single vent could drive global warming—the Modgunn crater is too small—the hundreds of similar features identified across the NAIP could collectively represent a massive carbon source.

## Open Questions

Despite these advances, fundamental uncertainties remain. We still lack precise constraints on the total carbon released through hydrothermal venting. Estimates for the Norwegian margin alone range from 225 to 2,250 gigatons of carbon [@LVFPSGAY], with potential emissions from the entire NAIP intrusive province reaching 13,000 gigatons [@SFHXHQXJ]. The wide range reflects genuine uncertainty about sill volumes, organic carbon content of intruded sediments, and the efficiency of gas escape through vent structures.

Equally uncertain is the relative contribution of thermogenic methane versus direct volcanic CO₂. The isotopic composition of PETM carbon—reconstructed from the magnitude of the CIE—suggests a highly ¹²C-enriched source consistent with organic matter, but could also be explained by mixed volcanic and biogenic sources [@JUNM4JH9]. Drilling additional vent complexes, particularly those with different host rock compositions, might help resolve this question.

Perhaps most fundamentally, we don't fully understand why this particular volcanic episode triggered such extreme warming when other large igneous provinces did not. The NAIP was enormous, but so was the Deccan Traps eruption at the Cretaceous-Paleogene boundary, which produced less sustained warming. The answer may lie in the specific configuration of the NAIP—the abundance of organic-rich sediments available for thermogenic conversion, the shallow-water setting enabling efficient atmospheric forcing, or the coincidence with boundary conditions that made the climate system particularly sensitive to perturbation.

The *JOIDES Resolution* has since moved on to other targets, but the cores from Expedition 396 continue yielding insights. Each analysis adds detail to our understanding of how the North Atlantic opened, how volatile emissions reached the atmosphere, and how Earth's climate responded to one of the most dramatic carbon injections in recent geological history. The smoking gun, it turns out, was a vent complex buried under Norwegian seas—waiting 56 million years for someone to drill into it and read the story preserved in its infill.
