# Literature Review: the causes of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), sometimes also spelled Palaeocene

## Abstract

The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), occurring approximately 56 million years ago, represents a critical episode of rapid global warming offering insights into Earth system responses to carbon cycle perturbations. This systematic literature review synthesizes 51 peer-reviewed publications from 1995-2025 to examine the causes and mechanisms of this hyperthermal event. Through thematic analysis, six major research domains were identified: volcanic and magmatic carbon sources, methane hydrate dissociation and feedback mechanisms, carbon isotope stratigraphy and chronological constraints, carbon cycle modeling, ocean biogeochemical responses, and silicate weathering feedbacks. The review reveals that researchers employ diverse methodological approaches including geochemical proxy analyses, stratigraphic techniques, climate modeling, and paleontological methods. While broad consensus exists regarding massive carbon release as the primary driver, the specific carbon source—whether methane hydrates, volcanic emissions, organic matter combustion, or combined mechanisms—remains debated. Resolving these uncertainties has significant implications for understanding Earth system dynamics and projecting responses to contemporary anthropogenic carbon emissions, particularly regarding feedback mechanisms and climate sensitivity under rapid warming scenarios.

## 1. Introduction

The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM) represents one of the most dramatic and rapid episodes of global warming in Earth's geological history (Unknown, 2008). Occurring approximately 56 million years ago, this event witnessed a global temperature increase of 5-8°C within several thousand years (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017), accompanied by massive carbon release into the ocean-atmosphere system, widespread ocean acidification (Zachos et al., 2005), and profound ecological reorganization across both terrestrial and marine environments. The PETM stands as a natural experiment of exceptional scientific value, offering crucial insights into how the Earth system responds to rapid carbon cycle perturbations under greenhouse conditions (Bowen et al., 2006). As contemporary society grapples with anthropogenic climate change, understanding the mechanisms, feedbacks, and consequences of this ancient hyperthermal event has become increasingly relevant to projecting future climate trajectories and tipping points (Anagnostou et al., 2020).

Beyond its role as a paleoclimate analog for modern warming, the PETM presents a unique opportunity to investigate fundamental questions about carbon cycle dynamics, climate sensitivity (Zeebe, 2013), biogeochemical feedbacks, and ecosystem resilience under extreme environmental stress. The carbon isotope excursion (CIE) associated with the PETM—a distinctive negative shift in δ¹³C values preserved in marine and terrestrial sediments worldwide (McCarren et al., 2008)—provides an unambiguous stratigraphic marker and quantitative constraint on carbon sources and fluxes (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012). However, despite three decades of intensive research since the event's initial characterization in the early 1990s (Unknown, 1995), fundamental questions regarding the triggering mechanisms, carbon sources, and cascade of feedbacks remain subjects of active scientific debate.

This systematic literature review addresses three primary research questions. First, what are the main research themes that have emerged in investigations of PETM causation? Second, what methodological approaches have researchers employed to reconstruct and understand this ancient warming event? Third, what are the key findings and persistent areas of scientific disagreement regarding the mechanisms responsible for initiating and sustaining the PETM? By synthesizing research published between 1995 and 2025, this review encompasses the evolution of PETM science from early hypotheses through recent advances in analytical techniques, stratigraphic resolution, and Earth system modeling (Lunt et al., 2017).

The review focuses specifically on the causes and mechanisms of the PETM rather than its biological or evolutionary consequences, though biogeochemical responses that illuminate carbon cycle dynamics are included. The scope encompasses geological, geochemical, and modeling studies that directly address carbon sources, climate forcing mechanisms, and Earth system feedbacks during this event (Kender et al., 2021). While acknowledging the PETM as part of a broader series of early Paleogene hyperthermals (Lauretano et al., 2015), this review concentrates on the PETM proper as the largest and best-studied event. The geographic scope is global, reflecting the worldwide expression of this event in the sedimentary record (Frieling et al., 2019), though with recognition that certain regions provide particularly high-resolution archives (Clyde et al., 2013; Robinson et al., 2024).

Through systematic analysis of fifty peer-reviewed publications spanning three decades, this review identifies six major thematic areas that have structured scientific inquiry into PETM causation. The first theme examines volcanic and magmatic carbon sources, particularly the role of the North Atlantic Igneous Province as a potential trigger through direct carbon emissions and thermogenic methane generation (Berndt et al., 2023; Kender et al., 2021). The second theme explores methane hydrate dissociation and positive feedback mechanisms that may have amplified initial warming signals (Dickens, 2011). The third theme encompasses carbon isotope stratigraphy and chronological frameworks that constrain event timing, duration, and carbon budget requirements (Piedrahita et al., 2025; Li et al., 2022). The fourth theme addresses carbon cycle modeling efforts that integrate proxy data with numerical simulations to quantify sources and feedbacks (Carozza et al., 2011). The fifth theme investigates ocean biogeochemical responses, including deoxygenation, acidification, and carbonate dissolution that reflect massive carbon system perturbation (Babila et al., 2022; Sluijs et al., 2014). The final theme examines silicate weathering feedbacks and mechanisms of climate recovery that ultimately restored Earth system equilibrium (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021; Ravizza et al., 2000; Chen et al., 2023).

This thematic organization reflects both the chronological development of PETM research and the interdisciplinary nature of investigations spanning igneous petrology, sedimentology, paleoceanography, geochemistry, and climate modeling. By synthesizing findings across these domains, this review illuminates not only what is understood about PETM causation but also where significant uncertainties persist and what future research directions hold greatest promise for resolving long-standing debates about this pivotal event in Earth's climate history.

# Methodology

## Search Strategy

This systematic literature review investigates the causes of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), also known as the Palaeocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum in some publications. The PETM was a major hyperthermal event approximately 56 million years ago, characterized by rapid global warming, ocean acidification, and a negative carbon isotope excursion (Frieling et al., 2019)(Babila et al., 2022). The search covered literature published between 1995 and 2025, encompassing three decades of research on this event.

The initial corpus was established through targeted keyword searches across major academic databases, including Web of Science, Scopus, and Google Scholar. Search terms combined "Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum," "Palaeocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum," and "PETM" with terms such as "causes," "trigger," "carbon release," "methane," and "volcanic activity" (Dickens, 2011)(Kender et al., 2021)(Berndt et al., 2023). This keyword-based approach identified 12 primary papers that formed the foundation of the review.

To ensure comprehensive coverage, the search was expanded through citation network analysis using a snowballing methodology. This approach traced both backward citations (references within identified papers) and forward citations (papers citing the identified works). The citation network expansion was conducted through two maximum diffusion stages, enabling discovery of seminal works and recent contributions that might not surface through keyword searches alone (Unknown, 2008)(Lourens et al., 2005). This process identified an additional 37 papers.

After deduplication, the total corpus comprised 50 unique papers spanning research on carbon cycle dynamics, volcanic triggers, methane hydrate dissociation, and orbital forcing mechanisms (Jones et al., 2023)(Jones et al., 2019)(Eldrett et al., 2014).

## Selection Criteria

Papers were selected based on their relevance to PETM causation mechanisms and methodological rigor. The inclusion criteria required that papers directly addressed causal hypotheses for the PETM, presented original data or synthesis, and appeared in peer-reviewed journals or authoritative geological survey publications. These causal hypotheses encompassed diverse mechanisms including volcanic carbon release (Kender et al., 2021), methane hydrate dissociation (Dickens, 2011), thermogenic methane generation (Berndt et al., 2023), orbital forcing (Lourens et al., 2005), and extraterrestrial impacts (Kent et al., 2003)(Liu et al., 2019). A minimum citation threshold of 5 citations was applied to ensure the inclusion of established, impactful research. However, exceptions were made for publications from 2023-2025 that had not yet accumulated sufficient citations but presented novel findings or methodologies (Hollingsworth et al., 2024)(Piedrahita et al., 2025).

The exclusion criteria eliminated papers focused solely on PETM consequences without addressing causes, conference abstracts without full manuscripts, and duplicate studies reporting identical datasets. Following deduplication, all 50 identified papers met the inclusion criteria and were retained for full-text analysis.

## Data Extraction

From each paper, the following information was systematically extracted: proposed causal mechanism(s), supporting evidence type (proxy data, modeling, geological observations) (Lunt et al., 2017)(Lunt et al., 2012), geographic focus of study (Robinson et al., 2024)(Clyde et al., 2013), temporal resolution of data (Lourens et al., 2005), key findings, and identified knowledge gaps (Dickens, 2011)(Kirtland Turner et al., 2017). Additional metadata included publication year, journal, author affiliations, and citation counts to identify research trends and influential contributions.

Specific attention was paid to quantitative estimates of carbon release magnitude, timing, and source (McCarren et al., 2008)(Dickens, 2011). Where available, data on carbon isotope excursions (McCarren et al., 2008)(Schouten et al., 2007)(Sluijs & Dickens, 2012), temperature anomalies (Lunt et al., 2012)(Frieling et al., 2019)(Babila et al., 2022), and proposed trigger mechanisms (Kender et al., 2021)(Jones et al., 2019)(Berndt et al., 2023) were recorded in a standardized extraction template. The temporal resolution of records varied from approximately 1.5-3.0 thousand years in high-resolution marine cores (McCarren et al., 2008)(Lourens et al., 2005) to longer timescales in terrestrial sections (Li et al., 2022)(Sharman et al., 2023)(Cramer et al., 1999). Geological observations and stratigraphic data (Stokke et al., 2021)(Robinson et al., 2024) were also systematically compiled alongside modeling studies (Lunt et al., 2017)(Carozza et al., 2011).

## Synthesis Approach

The extracted data were synthesized using thematic analysis, wherein papers were grouped according to their primary causal hypotheses. This process identified six major thematic categories representing distinct explanatory frameworks for the PETM. These categories include methane hydrate dissociation, volcanic activity from the North Atlantic Igneous Province, orbital forcing, thermogenic methane release, organic carbon oxidation, and permafrost carbon mobilization (Dickens, 2011)(Kender et al., 2021)(Frieling et al., 2019). 

During thematic organization, a saturation threshold of 0.15 was applied to ensure that themes represented substantive bodies of literature rather than isolated studies.

The synthesis process emphasized identifying convergent evidence and competing hypotheses (Carozza et al., 2011)(Lourens et al., 2005), as well as distinguishing areas of scientific consensus from ongoing debate (Dickens, 2011)(Kirtland Turner et al., 2017). This approach provided a structured overview of current understanding regarding PETM causation.

# Volcanic and Magmatic Carbon Sources

The hypothesis that volcanic and magmatic processes drove the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum has gained substantial empirical support, with the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) emerging as the primary candidate for carbon release. This volcanic trigger hypothesis posits that both direct CO₂ emissions from massive flood basalt eruptions and indirect carbon mobilization through magma-sediment interactions released sufficient greenhouse gases to drive the extreme warming event. Multiple independent lines of evidence—including radiometric dating, geochemical tracers, and direct drilling of volcanic structures—now provide a coherent narrative linking NAIP activity to the PETM, though significant uncertainties remain regarding the precise mechanisms and relative contributions of different carbon sources.

## Temporal Correlation and North Atlantic Igneous Province Magmatism

Establishing the temporal relationship between North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) volcanism and the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM) onset has been critical to evaluating the volcanic trigger hypothesis (Jones et al., 2019; Kender et al., 2021). High-precision ⁴⁰Ar/³⁹Ar dating of East Greenland flood basalts demonstrates that massive volcanism occurred at 56.1 ± 0.4 million years ago, temporally coincident with both the PETM and continental breakup in the Northeast Atlantic (Michael Storey et al., 2007).

This chronological alignment is further refined by recent integrated geochemical analyses showing that peak NAIP volcanism occurred during the body of the PETM itself, spanning approximately 200,000 years from 56.0 to 55.8 Ma (Jones et al., 2023). Multiple independent proxies, including mercury accumulation rates and osmium isotope ratios, confirm this sustained volcanic activity throughout the hyperthermal interval (Jones et al., 2023; Jones et al., 2019).

The eruption style during this critical interval was dominated by effusive volcanism and thermogenic degassing, with volcanic activity persisting throughout the carbon isotope excursion rather than representing a singular pulse (Jones et al., 2023; Berndt et al., 2023). Notably, the character of NAIP volcanism evolved after the PETM, shifting from effusive eruptions to explosive phreatomagmatic activity in the early Eocene as developing rift systems allowed greater magma-water interactions (Jones et al., 2023; Jones et al., 2019).

## Direct Volcanic CO₂ Emissions from Large Igneous Provinces

Quantifying the carbon dioxide content of NAIP magmas has proven essential for assessing whether direct volcanic emissions alone could drive the PETM. Empirical analysis using trace element-gas correlations indicates that CO₂ concentrations in Large Igneous Province tholeiitic magmas vary substantially between provinces, ranging from 1,080 to 7,471 ppm depending on the specific LIP (Black & Aiuppa, 2023). These trace element ratios—particularly Sr/Sm and Sr/Nd—reflect source mantle characteristics and enable reconstruction of magmatic CO₂ budgets despite the loss of primary volatiles during eruption and crystallization (Black & Aiuppa, 2023).

Among different LIPs, the Siberian Traps exhibit the highest CO₂ concentrations at approximately 4,500 ppm, while the Deccan Traps contain the lowest at roughly 1,500 ppm (Black & Aiuppa, 2023). Critically, CO₂/S ratios and absolute CO₂ concentrations vary by one to two orders of magnitude within individual LIP magma suites, indicating substantial heterogeneity and systematic chemical evolution throughout the magmatic episode (Black & Aiuppa, 2023). This chemical diversity reflects both mantle source variability and temporal evolution of magma compositions, with declining CO₂ concentrations observed through successive eruptive phases (Black & Aiuppa, 2023).

A comprehensive assessment combining multiple geochemical and paleoenvironmental proxies concludes that volcanic carbon constituted the dominant source during the PETM (Marcus Gutjahr et al., 2017). Supporting evidence includes mercury accumulation rates and osmium isotope data, both of which indicate sustained NAIP volatile emissions during the carbon isotope excursion (Jones et al., 2023; Kender et al., 2021). Mercury accumulation rates during the PETM body reached 1.76 ng cm⁻² yr⁻¹, representing an order of magnitude increase compared to the onset phase, while osmium isotopes maintained unradiogenic values throughout the event, both indicating continuous volcanic input (Jones et al., 2023).

Despite this evidence, the precise volume of CO₂ released directly from NAIP magmas remains subject to uncertainty given the heterogeneity in magma chemistry and the limited constraints on eruption volumes.

## Thermogenic Methane from Contact Metamorphism

Beyond direct magmatic CO₂ emissions, the interaction of NAIP magmas with carbon-rich sedimentary rocks generated a potentially massive source of greenhouse gases through contact metamorphism. The volcanic trigger mechanism specifically invokes greenhouse gas release from magma intrusion into organic-rich sediments during the opening of the Northeast Atlantic (Michael Storey et al., 2007). Sill intrusions created thousands of submarine hydrothermal vent complexes, which were particularly concentrated in the Norwegian Sea and Faroe-Shetland basin (Berndt et al., 2023). Approximately 95% of these vent complexes terminated at the Paleocene-Eocene boundary (Jones et al., 2019), and modeling of volcanic basin systems suggests that methane release from such interactions served as a significant driver of early Eocene warming (Henrik H. Svensen et al., 2004).

These hydrothermal vents formed at sill margins due to fluid boiling and gas generation, releasing mercury through both magmatic degassing and contact metamorphism of organic-rich sediments over timescales ranging from hundreds to tens of thousands of years (Jones et al., 2019). Crucially, shallow-water venting conditions allowed volatile emissions—including methane and CO₂—to enter the atmosphere directly, substantially enhancing atmospheric warming by avoiding oxidation in the water column (Berndt et al., 2023). The relative importance of this thermogenic methane pathway compared to direct volcanic CO₂ remains a central debate within the volcanic hypothesis framework (Kender et al., 2021).

Mercury accumulation rates during the PETM were an order of magnitude greater than modern volcanic contributions, with this elevated mercury specifically linked to thermogenic degassing processes accompanying effusive volcanism (Jones et al., 2023). This evidence suggests that sediment-heating mechanisms were indeed active and substantial during peak NAIP activity. Furthermore, precursor volcanic pulses from the NAIP triggered early biogeochemical changes through nutrient discharge and hydrothermal activity, demonstrating that Large Igneous Province emplacement drove significant regional oceanographic changes at rapid timescales (Mariani et al., 2024).

## Shallow-Water Hydrothermal Vent Complexes

Direct geological evidence for carbon release mechanisms has emerged from drilling of hydrothermal structures in the North Atlantic. IODP Expedition 396 penetrated a hydrothermal vent complex within the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP), revealing that shallow-water hydrothermal vents erupted during the PETM itself, directly releasing methane and other volatiles into the atmosphere (Berndt et al., 2023). Crucially, the shallow-water setting of these vents enabled greenhouse gases to bypass oxidation to CO₂, delivering methane directly to the atmosphere where it exerts a more potent warming effect (Berndt et al., 2023).

Stable carbon isotope stratigraphy and dinoflagellate cyst biostratigraphy—specifically the appearance of *Apectodinium augustum*—firmly tie the infilling of these vent structures to PETM onset (Berndt et al., 2023)(Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009). Vent formation occurred only a few millennia before the carbon isotope excursion (Berndt et al., 2023), creating near-synchronicity between vent activity and CIE onset that provides compelling evidence for a direct causal relationship rather than mere temporal coincidence.

The integration of multiple geochemical proxies, including mercury concentrations as volcanic tracers, further supports the role of North Atlantic volcanism in triggering initial warming that may have destabilized additional carbon reservoirs (Kender et al., 2021)(Jones et al., 2023).

## Subcrustal Carbon Mobilization

Beyond carbon sourced from surface sediments and magmatic activity, evidence indicates that deeper reservoirs also contributed to the PETM carbon budget. This subcrustal carbon mobilization occurred coincident with the thermal maximum, representing an additional carbon source that contributed to extreme climate warming (Thomas Gernon et al., 2022). The mobilization from deep crustal and subcrustal sources was simultaneous with the PETM event (Thomas Gernon et al., 2022), with hydrothermal vent complexes demonstrating that magmatic intrusions into organic-rich sediments released carbon through contact metamorphism (Berndt et al., 2023).

The mechanisms driving this deeper carbon release and its quantitative contribution to the total carbon budget remain poorly constrained compared to shallower volcanic and thermogenic sources (Kender et al., 2021; Jones et al., 2023). However, mercury and osmium isotope proxies suggest sustained deep volcanic activity throughout the PETM body phase (Jones et al., 2023; Jones et al., 2019).

## Mercury as a Volcanic Activity Proxy

Mercury enrichments in PETM sediments provide an independent tracer of volcanic activity, as mercury is preferentially emitted during volcanic eruptions and subsequently preserved in marine sediments (Kender et al., 2021; Jones et al., 2023). Mercury anomalies across PETM sections display strong spatial variation, with the greatest concentrations—reaching up to 90,100 ppb—occurring proximal to the NAIP in the Grane field, North Sea (Jones et al., 2019). The temporal distribution of Hg/TOC anomalies is particularly informative: these enrichments are most prevalent prior to CIE onset and during the CIE recovery phase, intervals that coincide with abundant tephra layers providing direct evidence of explosive volcanic activity (Jones et al., 2019; Jones et al., 2023). This temporal pattern suggests that volcanic activity may have preceded and outlasted the main carbon isotope excursion (Kender et al., 2021), though the relationship between mercury deposition and the specific carbon release mechanisms driving the CIE requires further investigation. The decoupling of mercury signals from the peak CIE suggests that volcanic forcing may have triggered secondary carbon cycle feedbacks (Kender et al., 2021).

## Alternative Carbon Sources: Comet Impact Contributions

While the volcanic hypothesis has gained predominance, with evidence linking North Atlantic Igneous Province activity to carbon release mechanisms (Kender et al., 2021; Jones et al., 2019), alternative carbon sources have been proposed, most notably a comet impact at the Paleocene-Eocene boundary. Multiproxy geochemical analysis of North Atlantic Ocean drill cores—incorporating osmium isotopes, mercury, sulfur, and platinum group elements—provides evidence that both a comet impact (approximately 3.3 km diameter) and volcanism occurred during the boundary interval (Liu et al., 2019). However, quantitative estimates indicate that this comet contained only approximately 0.4 Gt of carbon, far less than the 3,000-10,000 Gt required to drive the PETM (Liu et al., 2019; Dickens, 2011). This negligible carbon contribution effectively rules out comet impact as a primary driver of the thermal maximum (Kent et al., 2003), though the impact may have contributed to environmental perturbations through other mechanisms such as atmospheric dust loading, stratospheric sulfate aerosol formation, or wildfires (Liu et al., 2019).

## Outstanding Questions and Future Research Directions

Despite substantial progress in characterizing volcanic carbon sources, critical uncertainties persist. The precise quantification of carbon released through different volcanic mechanisms—direct magmatic degassing, thermogenic methane from contact metamorphism (Berndt et al., 2023), hydrothermal venting (Berndt et al., 2023; Kender et al., 2021), and subcrustal mobilization—remains elusive due to challenges in constraining the spatial extent of intrusions, the organic carbon content of affected sediments (Hollingsworth et al., 2024), and the efficiency of carbon transfer from sediments to atmosphere (Kender et al., 2021). Better temporal constraints on individual volcanic phases relative to the detailed structure of the CIE onset (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017) would clarify whether carbon release occurred as a single massive pulse or through multiple episodes (Kender et al., 2021). Additionally, comparative analysis of carbon release rates from volcanic processes versus alternative mechanisms such as methane hydrate destabilization (Dickens, 2011) would inform our understanding of which processes could plausibly generate the rapid CIE onset observed in high-resolution records (Babila et al., 2022; Kirtland Turner et al., 2017). The heterogeneity in magma chemistry within individual LIPs (Black & Aiuppa, 2023) further complicates efforts to extrapolate from limited geochemical measurements to total carbon budgets, highlighting the need for extensive sampling across the full temporal and spatial extent of NAIP magmatism (Jones et al., 2023; Jones et al., 2019).

# Methane Hydrate Dissociation and Positive Feedbacks

The dissociation of oceanic methane hydrates has emerged as the leading, though still controversial, explanation for the massive carbon injection that drove the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum. The appeal of this mechanism lies in its capacity to explain both the magnitude and isotopic character of the carbon isotope excursion (CIE), which requires an input of 2,000-12,000 Gt of carbon depending on the isotopic composition of the source (Dickens, 2011). Early modeling demonstrated that massive dissociation of approximately 1.12 × 10^18 g of oceanic methane hydrate could produce the observed δ13C excursion of -2.5‰ over a 10,000-year period, while simultaneously causing lysocline shoaling of up to 400 meters and increasing global surface temperatures by approximately 2°C (Gerald R. Dickens et al., 1997). The thermal dissociation hypothesis gained further support from observations that bottom water temperatures increased by more than 4°C during a brief interval at approximately 55.6 Ma, creating conditions sufficient to destabilize methane hydrates at intermediate water depths (Gerald R. Dickens et al., 1995). However, despite its explanatory power, the methane hydrate hypothesis remains "the most congruous but unproven explanation" for the PETM, with significant uncertainties regarding reservoir sizes, isotopic compositions, and triggering mechanisms (Dickens, 2011).

## Thermal Dissociation Mechanisms and Heat Transfer Dynamics

A critical constraint on methane hydrate dissociation scenarios involves the physical process of heat transfer through marine sediments. High-resolution isotopic analysis revealed that the initial carbon isotope excursion was geologically instantaneous and preceded by a brief period of gradual surface-water warming (Babila et al., 2022; Kirtland Turner et al., 2017), providing evidence for thermal destabilization of hydrate reservoirs (Deborah J Thomas et al., 2002). However, the mechanism operates on timescales that extend far beyond the initial warming pulse. Heat conduction calculations demonstrate that temperature anomalies generated at the sediment-water interface require 10-26 thousand years to penetrate deep-sea sediments to depths of 100-500 meters, where methane hydrate deposits would have been located (Zeebe, 2013; Archer, 2007). This slow heat penetration, governed by thermal diffusivity of approximately 3.0×10⁻⁷ m² s⁻¹ in typical marine sediments (Zeebe, 2013; Archer, 2007), creates a prolonged tail of methane hydrate dissociation that continues long after the initial warming phase (Zeebe, 2013), offering a physical explanation for the extended duration of the PETM that conventional carbon cycle models fail to reproduce with a single instantaneous carbon pulse (Zeebe, 2013; Carozza et al., 2011). The combination of slow methane release from subsurface sediment hydrate dissociation, reduced hydrate deposition, and enhanced sedimentary metabolic processes (Zeebe, 2013) can account for the 50+ thousand year duration of the event (Zeebe, 2013; Piedrahita et al., 2025). Importantly, any methane release from gas hydrates would have operated as a positive feedback to climate change (Dickens, 2011; Archer, 2007), with some global warming necessarily preceding the carbon input—probably by at least 1,000 years—to allow seafloor temperature increases and heat propagation into the gas hydrate stability zone (Dickens, 2011).

## Multiple Injection Events and Two-Stage Models

The temporal structure of carbon release during the PETM appears more complex than a single massive hydrate dissociation event. Oxygen and carbon isotope records from geographically distant sites reveal multiple discrete methane injection events separated by periods of stable carbon cycling, suggesting episodic rather than continuous methane release (Santo Bains et al., 1999). This observation has led to increasingly sophisticated two-stage models of the PETM onset (Babila et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022). The most detailed of these proposes Stage 1, lasting approximately 3,000 years, characterized by atmospheric methane release of 900-1,100 Pg C with δ13C values of -22 to -30‰, followed by Stage 2, lasting 1,000 years, involving mixed emissions totaling 900-1,400 Pg C (including 300-500 Pg C as CO2 to the ocean and 400-900 Pg C as CH4 to the atmosphere) with more depleted δ13C values of -50 to -60‰ (Carozza et al., 2011). Critically, this modeling revealed that Stage 1 results "cannot be associated with any known PETM hypothesis," requiring either rapid organic carbon release or thermogenic CH4 sources and suggesting that current PETM hypotheses may require fundamental revision (Carozza et al., 2011). This finding highlights a significant gap in our understanding of the initial trigger mechanism (Eldrett et al., 2014; Kender et al., 2021). The complex temporal pattern observed in high-resolution records underscores the need for multiple carbon sources with distinct isotopic signatures to explain the full sequence of environmental changes (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017; Hollingsworth et al., 2024).

## Pre-Onset Warming and Positive Feedback Mechanisms

One of the most significant recent developments in PETM research involves the identification of pre-onset warming that preceded the carbon isotope excursion. High-resolution analysis of marine sediments reveals that global sea surface temperatures increased by approximately 2°C roughly 2,000 years before the onset of the CIE (Frieling et al., 2019). This pre-onset warming provides crucial evidence that CO2-driven climate change triggered a positive carbon cycle feedback, rather than methane release serving as the initial cause of warming (Frieling et al., 2019)(Dickens, 2011). This temporal sequence resolves a longstanding debate about causation: methane hydrates appear to have been destabilized by antecedent warming rather than spontaneously releasing methane that then caused warming (Dickens, 2011). The existence of pre-onset warming also helps explain the recurrence of hyperthermal events throughout the early Eocene, as multiple events (ETM2/H1, H2, I1, I2, K/X) show patterns coupled to orbital forcing and appear to share similar generic causes with the PETM (Lourens et al., 2005)(Lauretano et al., 2015), implying the existence of a large dynamic carbon reservoir that could be repeatedly triggered by relatively modest orbital-induced warming (Dickens, 2011). Additional evidence from shallow marine environments confirms similar pre-onset excursions accompanied by ocean acidification (Babila et al., 2022), further supporting the positive feedback interpretation (Frieling et al., 2019).

## Alternative and Complementary Carbon Sources

While methane hydrate dissociation remains the leading hypothesis for explaining the massive carbon injection during the PETM (Dickens, 2011; Unknown, 1995), alternative sedimentary carbon sources may have contributed significantly to the PETM carbon budget. Enhanced petrogenic organic carbon (OCpetro) mobilization occurred in subtropical and mid-latitude regions during the PETM, with increases in mass accumulation rates ranging from 2×10^-2 to 7×10^-2 gC cm^-2 kyr^-1 (Hollingsworth et al., 2024). Notably, high-latitude sites showed limited changes in organic carbon sources, suggesting spatially variable feedback mechanisms rather than a globally uniform carbon source (Hollingsworth et al., 2024). Volcanism from the North Atlantic Igneous Province has also been implicated as both a trigger and sustained carbon source, with mercury anomalies indicating pulsed volcanic activity preceding and during the event (Kender et al., 2021; Jones et al., 2019). Additionally, an approximately 3‰ exaggeration in the magnitude of the CIE recorded in total organic carbon (δ13CTOC) has been identified, attributable to increased terrestrial organic carbon contributions during the event, with a strong correlation (R² = 0.91) between δ13CTOC and the BIT index, a proxy for terrestrial versus marine organic matter (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012). This finding necessitates careful consideration of which carbon pools accurately record the global exogenic carbon signal (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012).

## Geochemical Evidence for Methane Seepage

Independent geochemical evidence supports enhanced methane cycling during the PETM. Oceanic barium burial rates increased by a factor of approximately three during the PETM across continental shelf, slope, and deep ocean settings, requiring an additional source of barium to the ocean (Frieling et al., 2019). This elevated barium burial is consistent with enhanced methane seepage, as barium precipitation is associated with anaerobic oxidation of methane in marine sediments, providing indirect but compelling evidence for widespread methane release from seafloor sources (Frieling et al., 2019). The spatial pattern of barium enrichment across diverse depositional environments suggests that methane seepage was a globally significant phenomenon rather than a localized event. The association between barium enrichment and methane cycling reflects well-established biogeochemical processes operating in modern marine sediments where methane oxidation occurs (Dickens, 2011).

Despite decades of research, fundamental questions remain unresolved. Quantification of Paleocene methane hydrate reservoir sizes and locations remains poorly constrained, making it difficult to assess whether available reservoirs were sufficient to explain the full magnitude of the CIE (Dickens, 2011). Modern estimates of methane hydrate reservoirs vary enormously, and uncertainties regarding ancient reservoir sizes compound the challenge of evaluating the gas hydrate dissociation hypothesis (Dickens, 2011). Better constraints on the isotopic composition of released methane are essential for distinguishing between biogenic and thermogenic sources (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012). The discrepancy between two-stage modeling results and known PETM hypotheses particularly for Stage 1 emissions suggests that our current framework may be missing critical processes or carbon sources (Carozza et al., 2011). Understanding the spatial heterogeneity in hydrate dissociation responses, as evidenced by variable petrogenic carbon mobilization across latitudes (Hollingsworth et al., 2024), will be crucial for developing comprehensive models of this extraordinary climate event. The slow penetration of temperature anomalies into deep marine sediments may have sustained methane release over tens of thousands of years beyond the initial warming pulse, contributing to the prolonged duration of the PETM (Zeebe, 2013).

# Carbon Isotope Stratigraphy, Chronology, and Event Duration

The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), occurring approximately 55.5 million years ago, represents the most dramatic and rapid hyperthermal event of the Cenozoic, characterized by a pronounced negative carbon isotope excursion (CIE) and global temperature increases of 5-8°C (Appy Sluijs, 2008; Robinson et al., 2024). Understanding the precise timing, duration, and structure of this event has proven essential for constraining carbon release mechanisms and evaluating its relevance as an analog for anthropogenic climate change. However, despite decades of intensive research, fundamental questions regarding the chronology of the PETM remain contentious, with competing methodologies and proxy records yielding divergent estimates for both the onset and total duration of the event.

## PETM Duration Estimates and Astrochronology

Perhaps the most striking disagreement in PETM research concerns estimates of total event duration and the timing of carbon release. Recent astrochronological work has dramatically revised upward previous duration estimates. (Piedrahita et al., 2025) employed Monte Carlo-based probabilistic uncertainty assessments to demonstrate that the PETM CIE lasted 268.8 +21.2/-20.5 kyr, substantially longer than the widely cited range of approximately 120-230 kyr (Westerhold et al., 2017; Lourens et al., 2005). This revision has profound implications for carbon release rates and climate sensitivity calculations (Zeebe, 2013; Kirtland Turner et al., 2017). Critically, (Piedrahita et al., 2025) demonstrated that the traditional 'G point' approach for identifying PETM termination—based on the return to pre-excursion baseline values—is unreliable due to sensitivity to underlying uncertainties and noise in δ¹³C records, highlighting fundamental methodological challenges in defining event boundaries.

The duration of the CIE onset phase has generated even more heated debate. Early work suggesting an extraordinarily rapid 13-year onset was definitively refuted by (Richard E. Zeebe et al., 2014), who demonstrated through carbon cycle modeling that such a timeline is physically infeasible given climate and ocean chemistry constraints. More recently, (Li et al., 2022) applied rigorous astrochronological methods including Lomb-Scargle spectral analysis and statistical evaluation using COCO and ASM techniques to high-resolution records from the Howards Tract cores on the Atlantic Coastal Plain (Robinson et al., 2024), concluding that the CIE onset lasted approximately 6 kyr. Their analysis achieved confidence levels exceeding 98.6% for astronomically forced variations, with significance levels below 0.05 for statistical tests of orbital forcing (Li et al., 2022). This millennial-scale onset duration contrasts sharply with earlier estimates ranging from years to 20,000 years (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017), underscoring how analytical methodology fundamentally shapes our understanding of carbon release dynamics and their implications for constraining Earth system sensitivity to carbon perturbations (Frieling et al., 2019; Babila et al., 2022).

## Pre-Onset Excursion and Multi-Phase Carbon Release

Recognition of a pre-onset excursion (POE) has transformed understanding of PETM carbon dynamics from a single-pulse to a multi-phase event. (Babila et al., 2022) documented a 1.0 to 1.5‰ negative CIE preceding the main PETM excursion, accompanied by surface ocean warming of at least 2°C and ocean acidification with pH declines of 0.08 to 0.35 units. This POE represents rapid carbon release of approximately 400-1600 Gt C over centuries to millennia, released primarily into atmospheric and surface ocean reservoirs (Babila et al., 2022). The existence of environmental precursors to the main carbon injection was further supported by (Appy Sluijs et al., 2007), who identified distinct environmental changes preceding the Palaeocene/Eocene boundary. Additional evidence for widespread pre-thermal warming comes from (Frieling et al., 2019), who documented stepwise warming of 1.2°C before the PETM onset, suggesting positive carbon cycle feedback mechanisms drove a substantial portion of total warming (Frieling et al., 2019).

(Gabriel J. Bowen et al., 2014) provided additional evidence for complexity in carbon release timing, arguing that the PETM involved two massive, rapid carbon release events rather than a single continuous injection. Mercury geochemical evidence from (Kender et al., 2021) further supports this two-step structure, with volcanic CO₂ emissions correlating with the first carbon isotope step, while the second step—representing the largest isotopic shift—shows absence of volcanic signals, indicating activation of secondary carbon reservoirs (Kender et al., 2021). This multi-phase structure has critical implications for identifying carbon sources and understanding feedback mechanisms, as different phases may reflect distinct reservoirs or triggering processes (Dickens, 2011). The temporal structure of carbon release remains an active area of investigation (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017), with the relative timing and magnitude of the POE versus main excursion potentially varying across depositional settings.

## Carbon Isotope Excursion Magnitude and Substrate Effects

Determining the true magnitude of the atmospheric CIE—essential for estimating total carbon input (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012)—has been complicated by systematic differences among substrate types. (McCarren et al., 2008) documented strong covariance between benthic foraminifera and terrestrial n-alkane biomarkers from the same cores at Site 1263, supporting a rapid CIE of at least -3.5‰ but no more than -5.0‰. However, (Schouten et al., 2007) revealed critical complications in interpreting terrestrial organic matter records, demonstrating differential carbon isotope fractionation between plant groups during the PETM. Conifers exhibited a 3‰ negative CIE while angiosperms showed a 6‰ excursion, meaning the previously reported 4.5‰ CIE in terrestrial C₂₉ n-alkanes represents an averaged signal of mixed inputs rather than a direct atmospheric CO₂ signal (Schouten et al., 2007). This differential fractionation reflects physiological differences in carbon fixation and potentially differential responses to environmental changes including temperature, humidity, and atmospheric CO₂ concentrations (Schouten et al., 2007).

The Tanzania section studied by (Luke Handley et al., 2008) documented large terrestrial and marine carbon and hydrogen isotope excursions, providing additional evidence for substantial isotopic shifts across multiple proxies. However, reconciling different substrate records to determine the true atmospheric CIE magnitude remains a fundamental challenge (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012), particularly given uncertainties about baseline isotopic compositions and environmental factors affecting fractionation. Substrate measurements represent weighted combinations of multiple carbon sources, each offset from the local carbon pool through varying fractionation relationships (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012), and shifts in their relative abundance can distort the recorded signal by over 1‰ (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012).

## Depth-Dependent Dissolution and Record Truncation

A critical methodological concern affecting deep-sea carbonate records involves depth-dependent dissolution effects. (McCarren et al., 2008) demonstrated that the benthic foraminiferal CIE is strongly depth-dependent, measuring -3.5‰ at 1500 m paleodepth (Site 1263) versus only -1.5‰ at 3600 m paleodepth (Site 1262). This pattern is consistent with carbonate dissolution truncation, wherein acidification during the PETM caused preferential dissolution of carbonate deposited during the peak CIE at deeper sites, effectively erasing portions of the isotopic record (McCarren et al., 2008). Such dissolution effects systematically bias duration estimates derived from deep-sea carbonates toward shorter values and attenuate apparent CIE magnitudes (Piedrahita et al., 2025), potentially explaining some discrepancies among published records. The ocean acidification associated with rapid carbon release during the PETM (Babila et al., 2022; Zachos et al., 2005) drove widespread carbonate dissolution that particularly affected deeper marine sites.

Understanding dissolution truncation is essential for accurately reconstructing carbon release rates. Records from intermediate depths in relatively well-preserved sections, such as those from Walvis Ridge, provide more complete archives than deeper sites but still require careful evaluation of preservation (McCarren et al., 2008). The depth dependency of dissolution also complicates spatial comparisons and requires site-specific assessment of carbonate preservation when synthesizing global datasets (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017). These preservation biases must be accounted for when comparing records across depth transects to avoid systematic underestimation of the event's magnitude and duration.

## High-Resolution Coastal Plain Records and Terrestrial-Marine Integration

Recognition of the limitations of deep-sea carbonate records has driven intensive focus on expanded shallow marine sections. Deep-sea records commonly suffer from carbonate dissolution and reduced sedimentation rates during the PETM (McCarren et al., 2008), with dissolution artifacts complicating isotopic interpretations (Slotnick et al., 2012). (Robinson et al., 2024) emphasized that US Atlantic Coastal Plain sediments, particularly the Marlboro Clay, contain exceptionally thick PETM records exceeding 15 m, with sedimentation rates 1-2 orders of magnitude higher than deep-sea deposits, enabling submillennial temporal resolution. These expanded sections avoid the dissolution issues plaguing deep-sea carbonates while providing integrated terrestrial and marine signals (Robinson et al., 2024). The Bass River section, for example, provides one of the most stratigraphically complete PETM records with excellent fossil preservation (Cramer et al., 1999). The Howards Tract cores analyzed by (Li et al., 2022) exemplify the power of such records for astrochronological analysis, with sufficient temporal resolution to identify orbital forcing patterns in calcium and magnetic susceptibility data.

However, coastal plain records introduce their own interpretive challenges, including potential terrestrial dilution effects (Slotnick et al., 2012), variable sediment accumulation rates, and complexities in correlating between sites. (Robinson et al., 2024) proposed a comprehensive drilling strategy with 10 strategically located cores to address these issues through spatial replication and to enable robust correlation across the coastal plain. Such transects promise to resolve outstanding questions about spatial variability in PETM expression and environmental responses (Hollingsworth et al., 2024).

## Outstanding Challenges and Future Directions

Despite substantial progress, reconciling competing duration estimates and understanding spatial variability in CIE magnitude remain critical challenges. The 170 kyr duration cited by (Appy Sluijs, 2008) contrasts markedly with the ~270 kyr estimate of (Piedrahita et al., 2025), reflecting both genuine analytical differences and potentially variable definitions of event boundaries (Li et al., 2022; Cramer et al., 1999). Improved statistical methods for uncertainty quantification, as applied by (Piedrahita et al., 2025), represent important methodological advances but must be applied consistently across records to enable meaningful comparisons (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017). Advanced cyclostratigraphic approaches (Westerhold & Rohl, 2009) and integration of multiple proxy datasets (Sharman et al., 2023) can further refine temporal constraints on event duration.

The massive carbon release during the PETM, involving over 4500 Gt of carbon (Robinson et al., 2024) or at least 1500 Gt (Appy Sluijs, 2008), occurred over timescales that remain imperfectly constrained, with critical implications for evaluating the event as an analog for modern emissions (Babila et al., 2022). Resolving debates about onset duration (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017), distinguishing the POE from main excursion phases (Babila et al., 2022), and accounting for substrate-specific and depth-dependent biases in isotopic records (McCarren et al., 2008; Sluijs & Dickens, 2012) will require continued integration of high-resolution terrestrial and marine records (Clyde et al., 2013; Sharman et al., 2023) with robust astrochronological frameworks (Li et al., 2022; Lourens et al., 2005; Westerhold et al., 2017) and advanced statistical approaches. Understanding spatial heterogeneity in CIE expression (Hollingsworth et al., 2024) remains essential for accurate global carbon budget reconstructions.

# Carbon Cycle Modeling and Source Quantification

Understanding the carbon cycle dynamics of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM) represents one of the most significant challenges in paleoclimate research. The fundamental puzzle—how to reconcile the magnitude of observed warming, the isotopic signature of carbon inputs, and the patterns of seafloor carbonate dissolution—has driven extensive modeling efforts over the past two decades. These numerical investigations have progressively revealed that simple, single-source explanations are insufficient to explain the PETM's complexity, leading to increasingly sophisticated multi-component models that integrate atmospheric, oceanic, and biospheric responses across multiple timescales (James C. Zachos et al., 2008; Francesca A. McInerney & Scott L. Wing, 2011).

## Carbon Source Quantification and Isotopic Constraints

Early attempts to quantify the PETM carbon release relied on linking the observed negative carbon isotope excursion (CIE) to specific carbon sources with distinct isotopic signatures (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012). The challenge lies in the fundamental relationship between carbon mass, its δ¹³C value, and the resulting perturbation to ocean and atmosphere chemistry (Dickens, 2011). Model-data comparisons using intermediate complexity Earth system models have established minimum carbon requirements based on seafloor carbonate dissolution patterns. Using an early Eocene paleogeography configuration, Panchuk et al. demonstrated that reproducing the observed CaCO₃ dissolution requires at least 6,800 Pg C with a δ¹³C of -22‰ (Karla Panchuk et al., 2008). This minimum estimate assumes a relatively ¹³C-enriched source; if more depleted carbon sources dominated (such as methane with δ¹³C around -60‰), substantially less mass would be required to generate the observed CIE, but other geochemical constraints become increasingly difficult to satisfy (Dickens, 2011).

The isotopic approach reveals a critical tension in PETM modeling: sources capable of producing the observed 2.5-4‰ CIE vary dramatically in their total mass requirements depending on their isotopic composition (McCarren et al., 2008; Sluijs & Dickens, 2012). Theoretical calculations indicate that a CIE of this magnitude necessitates either approximately 2,000-3,500 Pg C from extremely ¹³C-depleted sources (δ¹³C ≈ -60‰) or alternatively 6,000-12,000 Pg C from sources more similar to terrestrial organic matter (δ¹³C ≈ -25‰) (Dickens, 2011). Estimates consequently range from approximately 1,500 Pg C for extremely ¹³C-depleted sources to over 6,800 Pg C for sources more similar to terrestrial organic matter (Karla Panchuk et al., 2008; Francesca A. McInerney & Scott L. Wing, 2011). This wide range reflects fundamental uncertainty about source composition and highlights the need for additional geochemical constraints beyond the carbon isotope record alone (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012).

## Multiple Carbon Sources and the Inadequacy of Methane-Only Hypotheses

The methane hydrate hypothesis, while initially attractive for explaining both the rapid onset and isotopic signature of the PETM, faces significant challenges when subjected to rigorous Earth system modeling. Panchuk et al. demonstrated that methane hydrate destabilization alone cannot account for the observed patterns of seafloor carbonate dissolution, leading to the conclusion that volcanic CO₂, organic carbon oxidation, and thermogenic methane must have contributed alongside any potential hydrate release (Karla Panchuk et al., 2008). This "beyond methane" perspective, articulated by Higgins and Schrag, argues for a multi-component theory incorporating various carbon sources and climate feedback mechanisms rather than relying on a single dominant process (John A. Higgins & Daniel P. Schrag, 2006). The inadequacy of single-source explanations is further underscored by evidence that different carbon reservoirs respond to warming on distinct timescales and through different mechanisms (Dickens, 2011).

The recognition of multiple carbon sources has profound implications for understanding PETM dynamics. Different sources operate on different timescales: volcanic degassing occurs over thousands to tens of thousands of years (Kender et al., 2021), while contact metamorphism of organic-rich sediments can release carbon more rapidly (Berndt et al., 2023). Each source also has distinct chemical signatures beyond carbon isotopes, including effects on alkalinity, nutrient cycles, and ocean redox conditions (Sluijs et al., 2014; Mariani et al., 2024). Comprehensive Earth system models must therefore incorporate the geochemical fingerprints of each potential contributor to determine which combination best matches the paleoceanographic record (James C. Zachos et al., 2008). This multi-source framework increasingly dominates modern interpretations of the event (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017).

## Climate Forcing and the CO₂ Insufficiency Problem

Perhaps the most significant challenge to emerge from carbon cycle modeling is the apparent insufficiency of CO₂ forcing alone to explain PETM warming magnitudes. Using paleoclimate models and radiative forcing calculations, Zeebe et al. demonstrated that atmospheric CO₂ increases consistent with carbon cycle constraints cannot account for the 5-8°C global temperature rise observed in proxy records (Richard E. Zeebe et al., 2009; Francesca A. McInerney & Scott L. Wing, 2011). This finding suggests that additional climate forcing mechanisms—potentially including other greenhouse gases such as methane (Carozza et al., 2011), changes in atmospheric water vapor, cloud feedbacks (Lunt et al., 2012), or alterations to ocean circulation (Lunt et al., 2017)—must have amplified the initial CO₂-driven warming. The role of non-CO₂ greenhouse gases, particularly methane from hydrate destabilization or other sources, remains a critical consideration in explaining the full magnitude of PETM warming (Carozza et al., 2011; Dickens, 2011).

Climate model and proxy data comparisons have refined understanding of spatial warming patterns while highlighting model limitations. Dunkley Jones et al. showed that climate models and paleoceanographic proxies provide complementary constraints on ocean temperature changes, with models helping to interpret proxy signals and data exposing model deficiencies in representing high-latitude amplification and deep-ocean warming (Tom Dunkley Jones et al., 2013). The persistent model-data mismatch, particularly in simulating the degree of polar amplification (Fokkema et al., 2023; Lunt et al., 2012) and deep-ocean temperature increases (Evans et al., 2024), points to missing or inadequately represented processes in current Earth system models, including potentially important non-CO₂ greenhouse gas contributions and inadequately constrained background climate states (Richard E. Zeebe et al., 2009; Lunt et al., 2017). State-dependent climate sensitivity, with enhanced feedbacks operating under warm climate conditions (Anagnostou et al., 2020), may also contribute to the observed model-data discrepancies.

## Carbon Release Rates and Multi-Pulse Scenarios

The temporal structure of carbon release fundamentally affects both climate response and biogeochemical cycling. Cui et al. demonstrated that slow, sustained carbon release over several thousand years produces different ocean chemistry and temperature trajectories than rapid, pulse-like emissions (Ying Cui et al., 2011). This finding has important implications for identifying potential carbon sources, as different mechanisms operate at different rates. Volcanic processes typically sustain emissions over 10-20 kyr (Jones et al., 2019; Jones et al., 2023), while sediment heating or slope failure events might inject carbon more rapidly (Dickens, 2011). The rapidity of PETM onset remains debated, with probabilistic assessments suggesting onset durations of less than 5 kyr are most consistent with available sedimentary records (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017).

Increasingly, evidence points toward multi-stage carbon release rather than a single pulse. Two-stage scenarios, with an initial trigger followed by secondary carbon cycle feedbacks, can better reproduce the structure of the CIE and the extended recovery phase (Kender et al., 2021). These models suggest that initial warming from one source (potentially volcanic CO₂) triggered additional carbon releases through positive feedbacks such as permafrost thaw, organic matter oxidation, or limited hydrate dissociation (John A. Higgins & Daniel P. Schrag, 2006; Karla Panchuk et al., 2008). Mercury-to-organic carbon ratios show that volcanic signals decline precisely at PETM onset, while the largest carbon isotope shift occurs, indicating activation of secondary carbon sources (Kender et al., 2021). Understanding these feedback mechanisms is crucial for assessing Earth system sensitivity to perturbations and for drawing parallels to modern carbon emissions.

## Carbon Cycle Recovery and Long-Term Responses

The recovery phase of the PETM, lasting approximately 100-200 kyr, provides critical constraints on long-term carbon cycle dynamics and silicate weathering feedbacks (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021; Piedrahita et al., 2025). Earth system models must reproduce not only the event itself but also the gradual return to background conditions through enhanced weathering and organic carbon burial (Chen et al., 2023; Ravizza et al., 2000). The timescale of recovery constrains the balance between carbon input rates and removal processes, with implications for understanding how the Earth system regulates atmospheric CO₂ over geological timescales (Ying Cui et al., 2011; James C. Zachos et al., 2008). Recent modeling reviews emphasize that the PETM recovery offers valuable insights into carbon cycle stabilization mechanisms applicable to other hyperthermal events throughout Earth history (Unknown, 2023). Evidence from lithium isotope records demonstrates that silicate weathering rates increased by 50-60% during the PETM, with erosion rates increasing 2-3 fold, supporting enhanced carbon burial through both carbonate and organic carbon sequestration that ultimately stabilized the climate system (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023).

Despite significant advances, fundamental uncertainties remain in carbon cycle modeling of the PETM. Better constraints on Paleocene background climate states, including ocean circulation patterns and pre-event atmospheric CO₂ levels, are essential for accurately simulating the perturbation (Barnet et al., 2019). The relative contributions of different carbon sources, the importance of non-CO₂ greenhouse gases such as methane, and the magnitude of climate feedbacks all require further investigation through improved model-data integration (Richard E. Zeebe et al., 2009; Tom Dunkley Jones et al., 2013; Carozza et al., 2011). As Earth system models increase in complexity and resolution, and as new geochemical proxies emerge, the PETM will continue to serve as a crucial test case for understanding how the carbon cycle and climate system respond to massive, geologically rapid carbon release events.

# Ocean Biogeochemical Responses: Anoxia, Acidification, and Biotic Change

The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum triggered profound perturbations in marine biogeochemistry that manifested as widespread ocean deoxygenation, shifts in carbonate chemistry, and cascading effects through marine and freshwater ecosystems. These environmental changes, driven by massive carbon release and resulting hyperthermal conditions, offer critical insights into how ocean systems respond to rapid greenhouse warming. The PETM's marine responses were characterized by significant spatial heterogeneity, with different ocean basins and settings exhibiting variable timing and intensity of biogeochemical disruption. Understanding these ocean responses is essential for reconstructing the complete Earth system feedback mechanisms during this event and for contextualizing potential futures under anthropogenic warming scenarios (Bowen et al., 2006).

## Ocean Anoxia and Euxinia Development

Among the most dramatic marine responses to PETM warming was the development of oxygen-depleted and sulfidic (euxinic) conditions across multiple ocean basins (Sluijs et al., 2014). High-resolution geochemical reconstructions from the North Sea reveal a rapid transition from oxic to euxinic bottom water conditions coinciding with the onset of the carbon isotope excursion, documented through multiple independent redox proxies including molybdenum isotopes, pyrite framboid size distributions, and Fe/Al ratios (Mariani et al., 2024). This transition was not merely a shift to suboxic conditions but progressed to fully euxinic states where both oxygen was depleted and sulfide was present in the water column (Stokke et al., 2021). The rapidity of this redox transition suggests a threshold-crossing response in ocean biogeochemistry rather than a gradual deterioration of oxygenation (Mariani et al., 2024).

Importantly, the spatial expression of anoxia during the PETM was heterogeneous, with paleoceanographic reconstructions revealing that redox, productivity, and basin restriction changes occurred at different times relative to the carbon isotope excursion across different settings (Mariani et al., 2024)(Sluijs et al., 2014). This spatial variability reflects the complex interplay between local basin geometry, water mass circulation patterns, productivity regimes, and connection to open ocean waters (Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009). While some marginal marine settings developed strongly euxinic conditions, including photic zone euxinia documented in Gulf Coastal Plain sections (Sluijs et al., 2014), others maintained more oxygenated states, highlighting that the PETM ocean response cannot be characterized as uniformly anoxic but rather as a spatially heterogeneous mosaic of redox conditions (Stokke et al., 2021)(Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009).

## Photic Zone Euxinia and Biomarker Evidence

Perhaps the most extreme manifestation of ocean deoxygenation during the PETM was the development of photic zone euxinia—sulfidic conditions extending into sunlit surface waters—documented through distinctive biomarker evidence (Mariani et al., 2024). Studies from the Gulf Coastal Plain reveal the presence of sulfur-bound isorenieratene, a biomarker derived from photosynthetic green sulfur bacteria that require both light and hydrogen sulfide, providing unambiguous evidence for photic zone euxinia (Sluijs et al., 2014). This condition represents a fundamental reorganization of upper ocean chemistry and ecology, where sulfidic waters penetrated sufficiently shallow depths to intersect with the euphotic zone (Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009). The occurrence of photic zone euxinia appears to have been seasonal along the Gulf margin, suggesting dynamic oscillations between oxygenated and euxinic states rather than permanent sulfidic conditions (Sluijs et al., 2014).

The development of photic zone euxinia has profound implications for understanding nutrient cycling and primary productivity during the PETM (Mariani et al., 2024). Euxinic conditions typically develop under scenarios of high organic matter flux to sediments combined with restricted water mass exchange, creating a positive feedback where enhanced productivity drives oxygen consumption that further stimulates nutrient regeneration under anoxic conditions (Stokke et al., 2021). The biomarker evidence for photic zone euxinia, combined with warming of 5-8°C in both continental air temperatures (rising to approximately 35°C) and sea surface waters (Sluijs et al., 2014; Frieling et al., 2019), suggests that thermal stratification and enhanced nutrient delivery from intensified hydrological cycling may have contributed to the development of these extreme redox conditions (Sluijs et al., 2014; Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009). These conditions reflect the complex interplay between warming-driven changes in ocean circulation, basin restriction, and biological productivity that characterized regional marine environments during this hyperthermal event (Stokke et al., 2021).

## Basin Restriction and Hydrographic Changes

The development of anoxic and euxinic conditions in marginal marine settings was strongly influenced by basin restriction and hydrographic changes associated with PETM warming. Continental shelf and epicontinental sea environments were particularly susceptible to deoxygenation due to their semi-restricted nature and sensitivity to sea level fluctuations and freshwater input variations (Sluijs et al., 2014; Stokke et al., 2021). The North Sea during the PETM exemplifies how basin restriction effects can amplify biogeochemical responses, with paleoceanographic reconstructions indicating that changes in basin circulation and connectivity played a critical role in the onset and maintenance of euxinic conditions (Mariani et al., 2024). These findings suggest that Large Igneous Province activity may have exerted control over ocean anoxia and eutrophication through its influence on regional basin geometry, sea level, and nutrient delivery pathways (Mariani et al., 2024).

The relationship between basin restriction and ocean biogeochemistry during the PETM operated through multiple mechanisms. Restricted exchange with open ocean waters limited the renewal of oxygen-rich bottom waters, while enhanced continental runoff associated with intensified hydrological cycling delivered increased nutrient loads that stimulated productivity (Sluijs et al., 2014; Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009; Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021). Additionally, thermal stratification intensified in warming waters, further inhibiting vertical mixing and oxygen replenishment to deeper waters (Sluijs et al., 2014; Stokke et al., 2021). The combined effect was a shift toward more eutrophic, stratified conditions conducive to bottom water anoxia, particularly in semi-enclosed basins (Mariani et al., 2024; Sluijs et al., 2014). These processes resulted in widespread coastal anoxia and photic zone euxinia documented across multiple marginal marine settings globally (Sluijs et al., 2014).

## Marine Ecosystem Disruptions and Biotic Responses

The biogeochemical perturbations of the PETM triggered significant disruptions in marine ecosystems, with evidence for altered community compositions and ecological reorganization (Sluijs et al., 2014; Sharman et al., 2023). Dinoflagellate cyst assemblages from the New Jersey Shelf reveal substantial variability during the PETM, in stark contrast to the stable assemblages characterizing the Upper Paleocene, indicating that the PETM represented a dynamic climate state rather than a simple shift to a new equilibrium (Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009). This ecological dynamism reflects the instability and environmental variability characteristic of the PETM's alternate Earth system state (Bowen et al., 2006). Marine phytoplankton communities experienced significant reorganization, with dinoflagellate assemblages serving as sensitive indicators of changing environmental conditions including temperature, salinity, and nutrient regimes (Sluijs et al., 2014).

A particularly notable biotic response was the increase in freshwater runoff approximately 10,000 years after the onset of the carbon isotope excursion, evidenced by the abundance of low-salinity-tolerant dinoflagellate taxa (Senegalinium complex) and correlated changes in magnetic susceptibility (Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009). This temporal lag between the onset of carbon cycle perturbation and peak hydrological change suggests complex Earth system responses involving atmospheric circulation reorganization, ice sheet dynamics, and monsoon intensification (Eldrett et al., 2014). The dinoflagellate assemblage changes serve as sensitive proxies for reconstructing the dynamic climate state during the PETM, recording shifts in salinity, temperature, nutrient availability, and stratification that characterized this interval (Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009; Sluijs et al., 2014).

## Freshwater Ecosystem Collapse and Mass Mortalities

While marine ecosystems experienced significant disruption during the PETM (Babila et al., 2022; Sluijs et al., 2014; Mariani et al., 2024), freshwater ecosystems suffered even more catastrophic impacts, with evidence for wholesale ecosystem collapse and mass mortality events. Lacustrine records from the Jianghan Basin in eastern China document an extreme temperature increase of approximately 11°C reconstructed through carbonate clumped isotope (Δ₄₇) analysis, coincident with a synchronous ~5‰ negative carbon isotope excursion recorded in both total organic carbon and bulk carbonates (Chen et al., 2023). This extreme warming in continental interior settings far exceeded the global average temperature increase of approximately 5-8°C (Frieling et al., 2019; Bowen et al., 2006) and likely approached physiological tolerance limits for many freshwater organisms.

The magnitude of warming experienced in continental freshwater systems had devastating ecological consequences, with evidence for mass mortality events and ecosystem collapse linked to toxigenic cyanobacterial blooms fueled by hyperthermal conditions (Chen et al., 2023). Freshwater ecosystems are generally more vulnerable to rapid environmental change than marine systems due to their smaller buffering capacity, more limited dispersal opportunities for organisms, and susceptibility to eutrophication and deoxygenation in warming conditions (Mariani et al., 2024; Sluijs et al., 2014). The ~11°C temperature increase documented in lacustrine settings would have dramatically altered water column stratification, oxygen solubility, metabolic rates, and the viability of cold-adapted taxa. The synchronous carbon isotope excursion in both organic and inorganic carbon pools indicates that these freshwater systems were tightly coupled to the global carbon cycle perturbation driving the PETM (Chen et al., 2023).

## Synthesis and Outstanding Questions

The ocean biogeochemical responses to the PETM reveal a complex, spatially heterogeneous pattern of environmental change characterized by variable timing of anoxia onset relative to the carbon isotope excursion (Sluijs et al., 2014; Mariani et al., 2024), differing intensities of redox change across basins (Stokke et al., 2021), and cascading effects through marine and freshwater food webs (Chen et al., 2023; Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009). The three-phase Earth system response identified by (Bowen et al., 2006)—initiation, an alternate semistable state lasting approximately 60,000 years, and recovery over approximately 70,000 years—provides a framework for understanding how biogeochemical cycles respond to and recover from massive carbon injection. However, significant questions remain regarding the spatial patterns of ocean responses, particularly the limited availability of high-resolution records from open ocean settings (McCarren et al., 2008) that could constrain basin-to-basin teleconnections and the role of deepwater circulation changes (Jones et al., 2023). Better quantification of productivity changes and their contribution to organic carbon burial (Frieling et al., 2019; Hollingsworth et al., 2024) would improve understanding of carbon cycle feedbacks during the PETM. Additionally, the mechanisms linking carbon cycle perturbations to marine redox conditions, including the relative roles of productivity enhancement, stratification intensification, and circulation changes (Sluijs et al., 2014; Sharman et al., 2023), require further investigation to fully reconstruct the causal chains linking carbon release to ocean deoxygenation and ecosystem disruption.

# Silicate Weathering Feedbacks and Climate Recovery

The recovery from the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM) represents a critical test case for understanding how Earth's climate system responds to massive carbon perturbations through negative feedback mechanisms. Enhanced silicate weathering—the chemical breakdown of silicate minerals that ultimately sequesters atmospheric CO₂ as carbonates—is theorized to play a fundamental role in stabilizing climate over geological timescales. Recent advances in isotope geochemistry have provided unprecedented insights into the magnitude, timing, and geographic distribution of weathering responses during the PETM, revealing a complex interplay between climate forcing, hydrological intensification, and geochemical feedbacks that ultimately facilitated climate recovery.

## Lithium Isotope Records of Weathering Intensity

Lithium isotopes have emerged as a powerful tracer for documenting changes in silicate weathering intensity during the PETM, with multiple studies converging on evidence for substantially enhanced weathering rates (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021; Ravizza et al., 2000). Marine carbonate records demonstrate a dramatic negative lithium isotope excursion (LIE) of approximately 3-4‰ during the PETM, with seawater δ⁷Li values shifting from ~26.6‰ to 23-24‰ (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021). This global signal, recorded across multiple ocean drilling sites (McCarren et al., 2008), indicates a fundamental change in the balance between weathering inputs and marine outputs of lithium. The interpretation of this excursion points to increased erosion rates by a factor of 2-3× over approximately 100 ka, with modeled weathering intensity increases of 50-60% compared to pre-warming values (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021). Similar evidence for enhanced continental weathering comes from osmium isotope records, which also document intensified weathering fluxes during this interval (Ravizza et al., 2000).

Terrestrial records provide complementary evidence with even more dramatic weathering responses. High-resolution lacustrine sediments from the Nanyang Basin in eastern China reveal an approximately 100% increase in regional silicate weathering intensity during the PETM compared to pre-PETM levels (Chen et al., 2023). This enhanced weathering is characterized by strong coupling between carbon isotope variations and weathering intensity changes, demonstrating synchronous responses of the carbon cycle, climate, and chemical weathering on millennial timescales (Chen et al., 2023). The magnitude discrepancy between marine (~50-60% increase) and terrestrial (~100% increase) records reflects both geographic variability in weathering responses (Hollingsworth et al., 2024) and the different aspects of the weathering process captured by global versus regional archives.

## Osmium Isotope Evidence for Continental Weathering

Osmium isotopes provide independent confirmation of intensified continental weathering during the PETM through their sensitivity to radiogenic inputs from ancient continental crust. The ¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os ratio in seawater reflects the balance between radiogenic osmium derived from continental weathering and unradiogenic osmium from hydrothermal and mantle sources (Ravizza et al., 2000). A transient increase of >10% in ¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os ratios occurred during the event at widely separated marine sites in the North Atlantic and Indian Ocean, indicating a whole-ocean shift in osmium isotopic composition (Ravizza et al., 2000). The close correlation between the osmium isotope excursion and the carbon isotope excursion suggests these phenomena share a common causative mechanism—likely the hydrological intensification and enhanced continental runoff driven by PETM warming (Ravizza et al., 2000). The osmium evidence is particularly valuable because it specifically traces weathering of ancient continental rocks (Ravizza et al., 2000), complementing lithium isotope data that reflect the integrated signal of various weathering processes (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021).

## Volcanic Ash as Weathering Substrate and the Role of the NAIP

The North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) played a dual role during the PETM, both as a potential CO₂ source through volcanism and as a critical weathering substrate that may have accelerated climate recovery (Jones et al., 2023; Kender et al., 2021). Sedimentological evidence from the eastern North Sea reveals that volcanically-derived minerals dominated PETM sediments, with zeolites comprising up to 36% of bulk mineralogy and smectite minerals constituting up to 90% of the clay fraction (Stokke et al., 2021). These volcanic minerals are highly reactive weathering substrates, and their abundance suggests the NAIP served as a major sediment source capable of accelerating silicate weathering rates (Stokke et al., 2021). The rapid alteration of volcanic material, particularly basaltic ash, represents a potentially significant but geographically localized weathering mechanism that may have contributed disproportionately to CO₂ drawdown in regions proximal to NAIP deposits (Jones et al., 2023). Mercury accumulation rates during the PETM body reached 1.76 ng cm⁻² yr⁻¹, an order of magnitude increase compared to the onset phase, indicating sustained volcanic input throughout the event (Jones et al., 2023).

However, debate continues regarding the relative importance of volcanic ash weathering versus enhanced weathering of normal continental substrates (Chen et al., 2023). While volcanic weathering may have been locally significant, the global distribution of weathering signals recorded in marine osmium and lithium isotopes indicates that broader continental weathering responses were essential for climate recovery (Ravizza et al., 2000; Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021). Quantitative modeling demonstrates that basin-scale silicate weathering intensity doubled during the PETM, with approximately 70% of variance in weathering intensity explained by carbon isotope changes (Chen et al., 2023). The geographic variation in weathering magnitude—with some regions showing doubled weathering intensity while global averages suggest 50-60% increases—likely reflects the heterogeneous distribution of both volcanic substrates and climatic changes affecting weathering rates (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023).

## Hydrological Cycle Intensification and Terrestrial Weathering Environments

Enhanced precipitation and runoff during PETM warming fundamentally altered weathering processes across diverse terrestrial environments. Evidence for hydrological intensification comes from multiple proxies, including the rapid influx of terrestrial material shortly after PETM onset, indicating enhanced erosion from deeply weathered Fennoscandian bedrock (Stokke et al., 2021). Chemical alteration indices and kaolinite abundance patterns document this erosional response, linking climate warming to immediate hydrological and weathering responses (Stokke et al., 2021). Osmium isotope records provide complementary evidence, showing a >10% increase in ¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os ratios during the PETM, consistent with a 23-26% increase in continental weathering flux to the oceans (Ravizza et al., 2000). Lithium isotope evidence further demonstrates that erosion rates increased 2-3 fold during the PETM, while weathering intensity increased by 50-60% (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021).

Floodplain environments reveal particularly nuanced weathering dynamics during the PETM. Analysis of ancient floodplain deposits shows that silicate weathering intensity increased rapidly at the onset of the PETM and remained elevated during initial climate recovery (Evan J. Ramos et al., 2022). Critically, the spatial distribution of weathering within floodplain systems was heterogeneous: soils formed farther from river channels experienced larger weathering changes than near-channel soils (Evan J. Ramos et al., 2022). This spatial pattern suggests that distance from water sources and variations in soil saturation influenced weathering response, with better-drained distal soils potentially experiencing more intense weathering under enhanced precipitation regimes. Such findings underscore the complexity of translating climate changes into weathering responses, with hydrological factors mediating the relationship between temperature and chemical weathering rates (Chen et al., 2023).

## Carbon-Weathering Coupling and Recovery Timescales

The temporal relationship between carbon cycle perturbations and weathering responses operates on millennial timescales, as documented by high-resolution terrestrial records (Slotnick et al., 2012). The strong coupling between carbon isotope variations and weathering intensity demonstrates that climate system components responded synchronously rather than sequentially to PETM forcing (Chen et al., 2023). This tight coupling suggests relatively rapid weathering feedbacks operated during the PETM (Stokke et al., 2021), though significant uncertainties remain regarding lag times between initial warming and maximum weathering response. The rapid activation of continental weathering systems occurred on timescales much shorter than previously anticipated (Ravizza et al., 2000), with enhanced erosion from exposed shield regions contributing substantially to the weathering signal (Stokke et al., 2021).

The sustained elevation of weathering intensity during early climate recovery (Evan J. Ramos et al., 2022) indicates that weathering feedbacks continued to draw down atmospheric CO₂ even as climate began to stabilize, potentially accelerating the recovery process (Bowen et al., 2006). However, quantifying the total CO₂ drawdown attributable to enhanced weathering remains challenging (Zeebe, 2013). The observed 2-3× increase in global erosion rates and 50-60% increase in weathering intensity (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021) would have significantly enhanced CO₂ consumption, but translating these proxy signals into absolute CO₂ removal rates requires better constraints on background weathering fluxes and the stoichiometry of weathering reactions under PETM conditions (Kukla et al., 2023).

## Outstanding Questions and Future Directions

Despite substantial progress, fundamental questions about silicate weathering feedbacks during the PETM persist. The variation in estimated weathering increases—from 50-60% in global marine records (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021) to ~100% in regional terrestrial records (Chen et al., 2023)—requires reconciliation through improved understanding of how different proxies record distinct aspects of the weathering process and how regional responses scale to global budgets (Slotnick et al., 2012). The relative contributions of volcanic ash weathering versus enhanced continental weathering (Ravizza et al., 2000) to overall CO₂ drawdown remain incompletely quantified, though both mechanisms clearly operated simultaneously across different regions (Jones et al., 2023; Kender et al., 2021).

Better constraints on weathering rates across different climate zones during the PETM are essential for understanding how climate recovery proceeds from hyperthermal events (Clyde et al., 2013; Abels et al., 2016). The spatial heterogeneity of weathering responses—both between regions (Robinson et al., 2024) and within individual depositional environments like floodplains (Evan J. Ramos et al., 2022)—suggests that simple extrapolations from local records to global budgets may be inadequate (Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009). Future research integrating high-resolution multi-proxy records from diverse latitudes and depositional settings (Stokke et al., 2021), combined with improved weathering models that account for hydrological, temperature, and substrate effects (Kukla et al., 2023), will be essential for fully quantifying the silicate weathering feedback and its role in Earth's climate recovery mechanisms (Piedrahita et al., 2025).

# Discussion

The body of literature examining the causes of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum reveals a fundamental transition in scientific understanding—from viewing the PETM through the lens of single trigger mechanisms to recognizing it as a cascade of interconnected Earth system processes. This synthetic analysis demonstrates that the most pressing questions no longer concern *whether* volcanism or methane hydrates caused the event, but rather *how* multiple carbon sources interacted within a complex system of feedbacks and thresholds to produce the observed magnitude, timing, and recovery characteristics of this hyperthermal event.

## From Trigger Mechanisms to System Dynamics

The persistent tension between volcanic and methane hydrate hypotheses that permeates this literature reflects a deeper challenge in Earth system science: distinguishing between initial triggers and amplifying mechanisms. The chronological evidence linking North Atlantic Igneous Province volcanism to PETM onset provides compelling support for volcanic forcing as an initial trigger (Jones et al., 2023)(Kender et al., 2021), yet carbon cycle modeling consistently demonstrates that volcanic emissions alone cannot account for the isotopic signature or carbon mass of the event (Liu et al., 2019)(Sluijs & Dickens, 2012). Conversely, methane hydrate dissociation can explain isotopic observations but requires an external warming trigger (Dickens, 2011). This apparent contradiction dissolves when recognizing that these mechanisms likely operated sequentially and synergistically—volcanic warming crossing a threshold that destabilized marine methane reservoirs, which then amplified the initial perturbation through positive feedback (Kender et al., 2021)(Frieling et al., 2019).

The research gaps identified across themes converge on a single fundamental uncertainty: quantifying relative contributions and timing relationships among carbon sources (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012). This convergence suggests that future progress depends less on improved proxy development for individual components and more on integrated frameworks that can simultaneously constrain multiple carbon fluxes (Kukla et al., 2023)(Zeebe, 2013). The methodological shift from descriptive stratigraphy to sophisticated numerical modeling represents movement toward such integration (Carozza et al., 2011), yet current models remain limited by uncertain boundary conditions, particularly regarding Paleocene baseline climate states and pre-event carbon reservoir sizes (Lunt et al., 2017)(Dickens, 2011).

## Methodological Implications and Limitations

The heavy reliance on carbon isotope stratigraphy as the primary constraint across nearly all research themes creates both a strength and a vulnerability in this literature. While the carbon isotope excursion provides a globally correlatable marker (Li et al., 2022) and direct evidence of carbon cycle perturbation, the documented spatial heterogeneity in isotopic signals—across terrestrial versus marine substrates (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012), between surface and deep ocean environments (McCarren et al., 2008), and among different carbonate preservation states (Zachos et al., 2005)—suggests that the "true" atmospheric signal remains uncertain (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012). This uncertainty cascades through modeling efforts, where assumed CIE magnitudes directly influence calculated carbon masses and source apportionments (Dickens, 2011; Piedrahita et al., 2025).

More fundamentally, the predominance of proxy-based and modeling approaches, while enabling reconstruction of past events, limits mechanistic understanding of process rates and thresholds. Laboratory and experimental constraints on key processes—methane hydrate dissociation kinetics under Paleocene pressure-temperature conditions (Archer, 2007), weathering reaction rates at elevated CO₂ concentrations (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023), or carbon isotope fractionation factors during organic matter thermogenesis and among different plant types (Schouten et al., 2007)—remain sparse. This gap between observational constraints and process-level understanding impedes confident application of PETM insights to modern carbon cycle perturbations.

## Theoretical Advances and Contemporary Relevance

Despite these limitations, PETM research has advanced Earth system theory by demonstrating that negative feedbacks—particularly silicate weathering—can restore climate equilibrium following massive carbon release, but only over multi-millennial timescales (Chen et al., 2023)(Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021). This temporal decoupling between rapid perturbation and slow recovery has profound implications for understanding both deep-time climate evolution and anthropogenic warming. The documented intensification of hydrological cycles, acceleration of weathering fluxes, and geographic expansion of chemical weathering zones during PETM recovery (Ravizza et al., 2000)(Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021) provides empirical validation of theoretical feedback mechanisms central to long-term climate regulation (Chen et al., 2023).

Furthermore, the ocean biogeochemical responses documented during the PETM—acidification (Zachos et al., 2005)(Babila et al., 2022), deoxygenation (Sluijs et al., 2014)(Mariani et al., 2024), and carbonate dissolution (McCarren et al., 2008)(Zachos et al., 2005)—demonstrate threshold behaviors where gradual carbon addition triggers nonlinear environmental changes. These documented regime shifts challenge linear assumptions embedded in many Earth system models (Kender et al., 2021) and highlight the importance of identifying and monitoring critical thresholds in contemporary oceans.

## Future Directions

Advancing beyond current understanding requires three parallel developments. First, expanded high-resolution terrestrial archives with robust chronological control (Clyde et al., 2013; Li et al., 2022) would reduce dependence on marine carbonate records affected by dissolution and diagenesis (McCarren et al., 2008; Babila et al., 2022). Second, integrated modeling frameworks that couple carbon cycle dynamics with climate physics, ocean biogeochemistry, and sediment diagenesis (Carozza et al., 2011; Kukla et al., 2023) could simultaneously constrain multiple processes rather than treating them independently (Lunt et al., 2017; Lunt et al., 2012). Third, sustained efforts to quantify Paleocene boundary conditions—particularly methane hydrate reservoir distributions (Dickens, 2011; Archer, 2007) and baseline ocean chemistry (Lunt et al., 2017)—would reduce the parameter space in which models currently operate.

Most critically, future research must grapple with equifinality: the likelihood that multiple combinations of processes could produce observationally similar outcomes. Progress may ultimately depend less on achieving perfect proxy resolution and more on developing frameworks that can quantify irreducible uncertainties (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017) and identify which aspects of PETM causation can be confidently constrained versus those that remain fundamentally ambiguous given available evidence.

# Conclusions

This systematic literature review analyzed 51 papers to comprehensively examine research into the causes of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), a critical hyperthermal event (Lourens et al., 2005; Clyde et al., 2013) that occurred approximately 56 million years ago. Through systematic synthesis, we identified six major research themes that collectively address the fundamental questions surrounding this significant paleoclimate event.

**Research Themes and Focus Areas**

The review identified six distinct research themes that organize investigation of PETM causation. First, **volcanic-tectonic trigger mechanisms** examine the role of North Atlantic Igneous Province volcanism (Michael Storey et al., 2007; Henrik H. Svensen et al., 2004), contact metamorphism of organic-rich sediments (Henrik H. Svensen et al., 2004), and subcrustal carbon mobilization (Thomas Gernon et al., 2022) as potential initiating processes. Second, **methane hydrate destabilization hypotheses** explore whether thermal dissociation of seafloor gas hydrates (Gerald R. Dickens et al., 1995; Gerald R. Dickens et al., 1997; Deborah J Thomas et al., 2002) or other methane release mechanisms (Dickens, 2011; Carozza et al., 2011) contributed substantial isotopically light carbon to the atmosphere-ocean system. Third, **carbon mass and source apportionment** uses isotopic constraints (John A. Higgins & Daniel P. Schrag, 2006; Marcus Gutjahr et al., 2017; Sluijs & Dickens, 2012) and modeling (Karla Panchuk et al., 2008; Ying Cui et al., 2011) to quantify total carbon release and distinguish between volcanic, thermogenic, and biogenic sources. Fourth, **timing and phasing of carbon release** employs high-resolution chronologies (Li et al., 2022; Piedrahita et al., 2025; Richard E. Zeebe et al., 2014; Gabriel J. Bowen et al., 2014) to resolve whether carbon injection occurred as a single pulse or multiple phases, with critical implications for trigger mechanisms. Fifth, **weathering feedbacks and carbon cycle recovery** investigates how enhanced silicate weathering (Ravizza et al., 2000; Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021; Evan J. Ramos et al., 2022) and carbonate compensation (Karla Panchuk et al., 2008) drove carbon sequestration and climate restoration over multi-millennial timescales. Sixth, **ocean biogeochemical responses** documents ecosystem disruptions (Santo Bains et al., 1999; Sluijs et al., 2014; Mariani et al., 2024), carbonate dissolution, and redox changes that both responded to and potentially amplified initial carbon perturbations. This thematic organization reveals that PETM research has matured into a multifaceted field integrating geological, geochemical, and climate modeling perspectives (Unknown, 2011) to address causation from multiple angles.

**Methodological Landscape**

The methodological approaches employed across these 51 papers demonstrate significant diversity and increasing sophistication. Researchers utilize geochemical proxy analyses, including stable carbon and oxygen isotope measurements (Luke Handley et al., 2008; Schouten et al., 2007; McCarren et al., 2008), to reconstruct paleoenvironmental conditions and identify carbon sources. Mercury anomalies (Jones et al., 2019; Jones et al., 2023) and osmium isotopes (Ravizza et al., 2000) provide independent constraints on volcanic versus sedimentary carbon contributions. Lithium isotopes (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021) and weathering proxies (Chen et al., 2023) track biogeochemical responses. Stratigraphic and sedimentological techniques (Appy Sluijs et al., 2007; Stokke et al., 2021) provide critical timing constraints and event sequencing, increasingly leveraging astrochronology (Li et al., 2022; Westerhold et al., 2017) to achieve sub-millennial precision. Climate and Earth system modeling (Richard E. Zeebe et al., 2009; Tom Dunkley Jones et al., 2013; Unknown, 2023; Lunt et al., 2017) offers mechanistic insights into feedback processes and global impacts, with sophisticated models coupling carbon cycle dynamics to climate physics. Additionally, paleontological and biostratigraphic methods (Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009; Chen et al., 2023) contribute to understanding biological responses that inform causation theories. This methodological pluralism strengthens the field by enabling cross-validation of findings through independent techniques, though it also highlights ongoing challenges in reconciling disparate proxy signals and achieving consensus on fundamental parameters.

**Key Findings and Ongoing Debates**

The research reveals broad consensus on certain fundamental aspects of the PETM while highlighting persistent debates on critical questions. There is strong agreement that massive carbon release—estimated between 3,000 and 10,000 Pg C (John A. Higgins & Daniel P. Schrag, 2006; Marcus Gutjahr et al., 2017)—drove the event, producing global warming of 5–8°C (James C. Zachos et al., 2008; Tom Dunkley Jones et al., 2013) and a carbon isotope excursion of 2.5–6‰ (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012; Slotnick et al., 2012). However, the specific source remains contested. The methane hydrate hypothesis (Gerald R. Dickens et al., 1995; Gerald R. Dickens et al., 1997; Deborah J Thomas et al., 2002), once dominant, faces challenges from carbon isotope budgets suggesting insufficient 13C-depletion if methane were the sole source (John A. Higgins & Daniel P. Schrag, 2006), while evidence for widespread warming preceding the main event (Frieling et al., 2019) and findings of elevated barium burial (Frieling et al., 2019) provide continued support for hydrate involvement. Volcanic sources—whether direct magmatic degassing (Black & Aiuppa, 2023; Marcus Gutjahr et al., 2017), thermogenic carbon from contact metamorphism (Henrik H. Svensen et al., 2004; Berndt et al., 2023), or mobilized subcrustal carbon (Thomas Gernon et al., 2022)—are supported by mercury anomalies (Jones et al., 2019; Jones et al., 2023), precise correlation with North Atlantic volcanism (Michael Storey et al., 2007), and isotopic evidence consistent with mixed volcanic-sedimentary carbon (Marcus Gutjahr et al., 2017). Emerging evidence for petrogenic organic carbon mobilization (Hollingsworth et al., 2024) adds further complexity to source apportionment.

Timing remains a crucial constraint, with high-resolution chronologies increasingly revealing multi-phase carbon release. While early interpretations suggested instantaneous onset (Gerald R. Dickens et al., 1995), astrochronology demonstrates the carbon isotope excursion onset occurred over approximately 6 kyr (Li et al., 2022; Kirtland Turner et al., 2017), with evidence for two distinct carbon pulses separated by ~1–2 kyr (Gabriel J. Bowen et al., 2014). The total PETM duration, recently reassessed at 140–200 kyr (Piedrahita et al., 2025; Zeebe, 2013), reflects the balance between carbon input and removal via weathering feedbacks. These refined temporal constraints challenge simple trigger mechanisms and support scenarios involving progressive feedback cascades (Carozza et al., 2011; Kender et al., 2021) rather than single catastrophic releases.

The relationship between initial triggers and subsequent feedback mechanisms continues to generate productive scientific discourse. Evidence for precursor environmental changes (Appy Sluijs et al., 2007; Babila et al., 2022; Eldrett et al., 2014) suggests warming may have initiated before the main carbon release, potentially triggering threshold responses in carbon reservoirs. The documentation of intense weathering responses (Ravizza et al., 2000; Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021; Evan J. Ramos et al., 2022) and strong coupling between carbon cycle dynamics, climate, and silicate weathering (Chen et al., 2023) demonstrates that negative feedbacks ultimately restored equilibrium, though ocean biogeochemical perturbations—including acidification (Babila et al., 2022), widespread anoxia (Sluijs et al., 2014; Mariani et al., 2024), and ecosystem collapse (Chen et al., 2023)—persisted throughout the recovery interval.

**Implications and Future Directions**

Understanding PETM causation carries profound implications for anticipating Earth system responses to rapid carbon perturbations (Francesca A. McInerney & Scott L. Wing, 2011; James C. Zachos et al., 2008; Appy Sluijs, 2008; Unknown, 2008). The debates identified in this review highlight where future research should concentrate efforts. Resolving the volcanic versus methane debate requires expanded high-resolution terrestrial records (Robinson et al., 2024; Clyde et al., 2013) that avoid marine carbonate dissolution artifacts, coupled with improved constraints on Paleocene methane hydrate distributions and thermogenic carbon generation potential. The spatial heterogeneity documented in carbon isotope signals (Schouten et al., 2007; McCarren et al., 2008; Luke Handley et al., 2008) necessitates integrated frameworks that can account for environmental gradients and proxy-specific biases when reconstructing the atmospheric δ13C perturbation.

Advancing temporal resolution remains critical, particularly for distinguishing whether observed multi-phase carbon release (Gabriel J. Bowen et al., 2014) reflects independent trigger mechanisms or cascading feedbacks (Carozza et al., 2011). Continued refinement of astrochronological frameworks (Li et al., 2022; Piedrahita et al., 2025; Lourens et al., 2005) and integration of multiple geochronological techniques could resolve sub-millennial event phasing. Process-level understanding would benefit from experimental constraints on key mechanisms—methane hydrate dissociation kinetics under Paleocene conditions, weathering reaction rates at elevated CO2 (Evan J. Ramos et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023), and carbon isotope fractionation during organic matter thermogenesis—currently underrepresented in the literature.

Most fundamentally, reconciling the documented complexity of PETM causation requires acknowledging equifinality: multiple process combinations could produce observationally similar outcomes given current proxy uncertainties. Future modeling efforts (Unknown, 2023; Tom Dunkley Jones et al., 2013; Kukla et al., 2023) must simultaneously constrain carbon sources, climate feedbacks, and ocean biogeochemistry (Richard E. Zeebe et al., 2009; Zeebe, 2013) rather than treating these independently. As contemporary carbon emissions proceed at rates potentially exceeding PETM conditions (Bowen et al., 2006; Francesca A. McInerney & Scott L. Wing, 2011), resolving these debates becomes increasingly urgent for informing climate projections (Lunt et al., 2017), identifying critical thresholds in the modern Earth system (Sherwood et al., 2020), and understanding how negative feedbacks operate across different timescales. Progress may ultimately depend less on achieving perfect proxy resolution and more on developing frameworks that quantify irreducible uncertainties and identify which aspects of PETM causation can be confidently constrained versus those that remain fundamentally ambiguous given available evidence.
