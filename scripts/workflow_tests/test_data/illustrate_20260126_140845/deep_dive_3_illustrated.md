# When oceans turned toxic

# When Oceans Turned Toxic

![Explore the stunning colors of a geothermal landscape captured in vivid detail.](/home/dave/thala/testing/test_data/illustrate_20260126_140845/images/header.jpg)


The comfortable assumption about ocean deoxygenation goes something like this: warming oceans hold less dissolved oxygen, metabolic rates increase, and over centuries to millennia, oxygen minimum zones gradually expand into previously habitable waters. It's a slow squeeze. Marine life has time to adapt, migrate, or at least persist in refugia while systems equilibrate. The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum, we might assume, followed this script—a protracted deterioration of ocean chemistry as the planet warmed by 5-8°C over tens of thousands of years.

This assumption feels solid because it aligns with our intuitions about how large systems change. Oceans are vast. Water masses circulate on timescales of centuries. Surely the biogeochemical machinery of the sea requires extended periods to fundamentally reorganize. And the PETM, though geologically rapid, still unfolded over perhaps 170,000 years total—plenty of time, it would seem, for gradual adjustment.

## The Crack in the Foundation

Here's the problem: the geological evidence doesn't show gradual deterioration. It shows a threshold-crossing event. High-resolution geochemical records from the North Sea reveal that the transition from oxic to euxinic bottom waters—not merely low-oxygen, but actively sulfidic—coincided essentially instantaneously with the onset of the carbon isotope excursion [@YFSXQJH4]. Multiple independent redox proxies converge on the same conclusion: ocean chemistry in certain basins didn't slowly slide toward anoxia. It snapped.

Molybdenum isotopes tell the story most clearly. In oxic marine environments, dissolved molybdenum is essentially unreactive, drifting through the water column at concentrations around 105 nanomoles per kilogram. But when hydrogen sulfide appears, a geochemical switch flips. Molybdenum converts to particle-reactive forms and precipitates into sediments, carrying with it a distinctive isotopic signature. In core E-8X from the Danish North Sea, sediment δ⁹⁸/⁹⁵Mo values shift abruptly from light compositions (indicating oxic conditions with partial molybdenum removal) to heavy compositions approaching seawater values—the fingerprint of quantitative molybdenum drawdown under strongly euxinic conditions [@YFSXQJH4]. This isn't a decades-long transition. The isotopic shift occurs over perhaps centimeters of sediment, representing years to decades at most.

Pyrite framboid size distributions corroborate the story. These microscopic iron sulfide spheres form only when hydrogen sulfide is present, and their size reflects where that sulfide existed in the water column. Small framboids (3-5 micrometers mean diameter) indicate syngenetic formation in an euxinic water column, while larger framboids grow more slowly in sediment pore waters under merely anoxic conditions. Across the PETM onset in the North Sea, framboid sizes plummet, indicating that sulfidic conditions penetrated the water column itself—not just the underlying sediments [@YFSXQJH4].

## Sulfide in Sunlit Waters

![Illustration for Sulfide in Sunlit Waters](/home/dave/thala/testing/test_data/illustrate_20260126_140845/images/section_2.png)


The most extreme manifestation of this biogeochemical disruption appears in an unexpected form: evidence that hydrogen sulfide—a gas toxic to essentially all aerobic life—reached the sunlit surface ocean. This condition, called photic zone euxinia, represents a fundamental inversion of normal ocean chemistry. Under ordinary circumstances, the ocean's upper layers are the most oxygen-rich, constantly replenished by photosynthesis and air-sea gas exchange. Photic zone euxinia means that sulfide-producing processes overwhelmed these oxygen sources, creating conditions where the very waters receiving sunlight became chemically incompatible with most marine life.

The evidence comes from an unusual biomarker: isorenieratene, a carotenoid pigment produced exclusively by green sulfur bacteria. These organisms are obligate anaerobes that require both light and hydrogen sulfide—a combination that exists naturally in only a handful of modern environments like the deep, permanently stratified Black Sea. Finding isorenieratene (or its degradation product isorenieratane) in sediments therefore constitutes unambiguous evidence that sulfidic conditions extended into the euphotic zone.

Work on the Gulf Coastal Plain identified sulfur-bound isorenieratane within PETM sediments from the Harrell Core in Mississippi, where it had not been present in underlying Paleocene deposits [@C5A4BTTB]. The presence of this biomarker creates an apparent paradox: contemporaneous with photic zone euxinia, the sediments contain glauconite grains (which require oxygen-bearing bottom waters to form) and organic linings of benthic foraminifera (which indicate seafloor colonization by organisms requiring oxygen). How can sunlit waters be sulfidic while the seafloor supports aerobic life?

The resolution points to temporal variability. Rather than permanent euxinia, the evidence suggests seasonal oscillations between oxygenated and sulfidic states—a pattern analogous to modern "dead zones" but far more extreme [@C5A4BTTB]. During certain seasons, perhaps when stratification strengthened and productivity peaked, sulfide accumulated faster than oxygen could penetrate, driving the chemocline upward into photic depths. During other periods, mixing or reduced productivity allowed reoxygenation. This seasonality means organisms experienced repeated lethal pulses rather than constant stress—a pattern potentially more devastating than permanent anoxia because it prevents adaptation to stable harsh conditions.

## Basin Restriction and the North Atlantic Connection

The development of euxinia wasn't merely a passive response to warming. It reflected active tectonic changes occurring simultaneously with climate disruption. The North Sea during the latest Paleocene was a semi-restricted basin, connected to the open Atlantic through shallow seaways that were themselves being modified by volcanic activity associated with the opening of the North Atlantic.

Geochemical proxies for basin restriction—particularly the ratio of molybdenum to total organic carbon (Mo/TOC)—reveal that the North Sea became increasingly isolated during the PETM. In well-connected basins with unlimited molybdenum resupply from the open ocean, Mo/TOC ratios remain high even under euxinic conditions. But when basins become restricted, the initial pulse of molybdenum removal depletes the reservoir, and subsequent organic-rich sediments accumulate with progressively lower Mo/TOC values. This "basin reservoir effect" serves as a proxy for water mass exchange with the global ocean [@YFSXQJH4].

In core E-8X, Mo/TOC values are highest at the CIE onset (~0.4 × 10²) and then decline steadily through the PETM main phase to values between 0.05 and 0.15. This pattern indicates that the North Sea experienced initial rapid drawdown of its molybdenum inventory followed by restricted resupply [@YFSXQJH4]. The basin was essentially trapping its sulfidic waters, preventing dilution with oxygenated Atlantic waters that might otherwise have buffered the system.

The mechanism for this restriction appears to have been thermal uplift associated with the North Atlantic Igneous Province. As mantle plume activity spread beneath northwestern Europe, it generated thousands of meters of surface uplift across what are now the Faroe-Shetland Basin and Norwegian Sea. This uplift partially blocked the shallow seaways connecting the North Sea to the open Atlantic, converting a marginally restricted basin into a strongly isolated one [@YFSXQJH4]. The timing correlation between uplift, volcanism (evidenced by sedimentary mercury anomalies), and the onset of euxinia suggests these weren't independent phenomena but causally linked aspects of a single Earth system perturbation.

## The Global Pattern of Deoxygenation

While the North Sea represents an exceptionally well-documented case study, oxygen depletion during the PETM was not limited to this basin. Compilation of proxy records across multiple ocean basins reveals a consistent pattern: oxygen concentrations dropped essentially everywhere in response to warming, hydrological changes, and carbon cycle feedbacks [@C5A4BTTB]. However, the expression of this deoxygenation varied enormously with water depth and setting.

Deep-sea sites experienced only limited reductions in seafloor oxygen, likely because the vast volume of the deep ocean and its continued circulation maintained oxygenation above critical thresholds [@C5A4BTTB]. Continental slopes and outer shelves developed pronounced oxygen minimum zones, with some locations experiencing intermittent anoxia. But it was the inner shelves and marginal seas—the settings closest to continental nutrient sources and most susceptible to stratification—that repeatedly crossed into truly euxinic conditions.

This pattern has a troubling analog in modern oceanographic trends. Contemporary "dead zones" are expanding in coastal waters globally, driven by nutrient pollution and warming-enhanced stratification. The PETM suggests that under sufficient warming, what are currently localized coastal phenomena could become widespread features of continental margins. The difference in scale between modern dead zones (typically tens to thousands of square kilometers) and PETM shelf euxinia (potentially encompassing entire continental margins) represents a regime shift that current ocean models struggle to capture.

The geographic distribution of documented PETM anoxia and euxinia is itself informative. Evidence for oxygen depletion has been found along the Gulf Coastal Plain, the northern and southern Tethyan margins, the Arctic Ocean, the North Sea, epicontinental Russia, and margins in the southwest Pacific [@C5A4BTTB]. This global distribution argues against purely local explanations and implies that warming-related mechanisms—reduced oxygen solubility, enhanced stratification, increased nutrient delivery via intensified hydrological cycling—operated across diverse oceanographic settings.

## The Freshwater Catastrophe

The marine record, dramatic as it is, may understate the severity of PETM impacts on aquatic ecosystems. Freshwater systems, with their smaller volumes and greater vulnerability to environmental change, experienced even more extreme perturbations.

Lacustrine records from the Jianghan Basin in eastern China document temperature increases of approximately 11°C based on carbonate clumped isotope (Δ₄₇) paleothermometry—far exceeding the global average ocean warming of 5-8°C [@NVB36TDB]. This amplified continental warming pushed lake ecosystems toward conditions incompatible with most complex life. The carbon isotope excursion in these lacustrine sediments reaches approximately -5‰ in both organic matter and carbonates, confirming that these inland waters were responding to the same global carbon cycle perturbation affecting the oceans [@NVB36TDB].

What makes the Jianghan Basin record particularly significant is the preservation of fossil assemblages that document ecosystem collapse in real time. Mass mortality events, recorded as accumulations of diverse vertebrate remains, coincide precisely with the interval of peak warming. Taphonomic analysis of these death assemblages—examining how the animals died and were preserved—combined with geochemical and sedimentological evidence, points toward toxigenic cyanobacterial blooms as the proximate cause of death [@NVB36TDB].

Harmful algal blooms fueled by warm, nutrient-rich conditions can produce potent neurotoxins (such as microcystins and anatoxins) that kill fish, birds, and mammals within hours of exposure. Modern examples include mass die-offs of flamingos in East African soda lakes and elephant deaths in Botswana linked to cyanobacterial proliferation. The PETM evidence from China suggests this phenomenon operated at catastrophic scales during the hyperthermal, with blooms intense and widespread enough to trigger ecosystem collapse across entire lake systems [@NVB36TDB].

The freshwater record exposes a mechanism that may have operated in marine settings as well but left less diagnostic evidence. Dinoflagellate assemblages in marine PETM sections show dramatic shifts, with the genus *Apectodinium* achieving global dominance under conditions that remain poorly understood [@C5A4BTTB]. While *Apectodinium* is not known to be toxic, the paleontological evidence for harmful bloom events in freshwater systems raises the possibility that analogous phenomena affected marine communities in ways we cannot currently detect.

## Nutrient Cycling and Positive Feedbacks

The development of widespread anoxia had consequences extending beyond direct mortality from oxygen depletion or toxic blooms. Anoxic and euxinic sediments behave fundamentally differently than oxygenated ones with respect to nutrient cycling, particularly phosphorus—the element that ultimately limits marine productivity on geological timescales.

Under oxygenated conditions, phosphorus released from decomposing organic matter is efficiently trapped in sediments through binding to iron oxides. But when oxygen disappears and sulfide accumulates, iron oxides dissolve, releasing their bound phosphorus back into the water column. This regenerated phosphorus then fuels additional productivity, which consumes more oxygen upon decomposition, which releases more phosphorus—a self-amplifying feedback loop that can maintain eutrophic conditions long after the initial trigger has subsided [@C5A4BTTB].

Evidence for this feedback during the PETM comes from multiple sources. Micropaleontological studies document sustained elevated primary productivity in shelf settings throughout the event, consistent with enhanced nutrient availability [@C5A4BTTB]. Nitrogen isotope records from the Arctic indicate high rates of nitrogen fixation, suggesting that nitrogen limitation was overcome through biological mechanisms while phosphorus remained available [@C5A4BTTB]. And the duration of anoxic conditions—persisting for tens of thousands of years in some locations—exceeds what would be expected from warming alone, implying that biogeochemical feedbacks maintained oxygen depletion long after temperatures had stabilized.

The phosphorus regeneration feedback may also have contributed to carbon burial that eventually drew down the excess atmospheric CO₂. Organic-rich black shales deposited under anoxic conditions represent a significant carbon sink. If phosphorus regeneration enhanced productivity while anoxia prevented the remineralization of sinking organic matter, the net effect would be accelerated organic carbon burial—a negative feedback on warming that helps explain the eventual recovery from the PETM [@C5A4BTTB]. This mechanism represents a geological analog to proposals for enhanced biological carbon pumps as climate mitigation strategies, though operating on timescales of millennia rather than decades.

## The Rapidity Problem

Perhaps the most challenging aspect of the PETM biogeochemical record to explain is the apparent rapidity of the transition to anoxic and euxinic conditions. Ocean circulation models suggest that changes in deep-water formation and ventilation should unfold over timescales of centuries to millennia—the overturning time of the ocean. Yet the geochemical evidence from the North Sea indicates that euxinia developed essentially contemporaneously with the carbon isotope excursion, implying response times of years to decades.

Several mechanisms might explain this rapidity. First, the North Sea (and other affected marginal seas) may have been poised near a tipping point prior to the PETM onset. Precursor signals in the E-8X core—brief episodes of laminated sedimentation, elevated molybdenum, and negative carbon isotope excursions occurring before the main CIE—suggest that the basin had already become sufficiently restricted that small perturbations could trigger temporary anoxia [@YFSXQJH4]. The main PETM carbon release then pushed a metastable system across a threshold it was already approaching.

Second, thermal stratification responds much faster than ocean circulation changes. The 5-8°C warming documented in sea surface temperatures would have dramatically strengthened the density contrast between surface and deep waters, suppressing vertical mixing on timescales of seasonal to annual variability. In already-restricted basins, this enhanced stratification could rapidly isolate bottom waters from oxygen replenishment.

Third, the carbon release itself—if it included significant methane oxidation in the water column—would have directly consumed oxygen independent of productivity-driven mechanisms. Methane oxidation stoichiometry requires two moles of oxygen per mole of methane, meaning a large methane pulse could rapidly draw down dissolved oxygen in waters through which it passed.

The precursor events documented in the North Sea are particularly intriguing. Short-lived episodes designated E1 and E2 in core E-8X show concurrent negative carbon isotope spikes, productivity increases (elevated TOC), and mercury enrichments indicative of volcanic activity [@YFSXQJH4]. These events suggest that volcanic pulses could temporarily trigger eutrophication and anoxia even before the main PETM carbon release, and that the basin had already crossed into a state where such responses were possible.

## What the Toxicity Reveals

The PETM's biogeochemical record reveals something profound about how ocean systems respond to rapid warming: the response is not linear, not gradual, and not uniform. Different basins crossed different thresholds at different times, but the crossings themselves were abrupt. The comfortable assumption of slow squeeze gives way to a more uncomfortable reality of threshold dynamics where systems can appear stable until suddenly they're not.

The development of photic zone euxinia represents perhaps the most extreme state transition documented during the PETM—a condition where the ocean's most productive waters became chemical death traps for aerobic life. That this condition developed at all speaks to the magnitude of the perturbation. That it developed seasonally rather than permanently speaks to the dynamism of the system. And that it developed rapidly speaks to the inadequacy of gradual forcing scenarios for explaining abrupt environmental change.

The freshwater record adds another dimension: mass mortality events linked to harmful algal blooms under hyperthermal conditions [@NVB36TDB]. These weren't gradual population declines or evolutionary bottlenecks—they were catastrophic kill events affecting diverse taxa simultaneously. The inference that cyanobacterial toxicity was responsible highlights a mechanism that current climate projections rarely emphasize but that may become increasingly relevant as lakes and coastal waters warm, stratify, and receive enhanced nutrient loading.

The PETM's biogeochemical perturbation persisted for tens of thousands of years, far longer than any anthropogenic forcing will likely continue if fossil fuel reserves are eventually exhausted. But the rapidity of the initial response—the development of euxinia within years to decades of the carbon isotope excursion onset—suggests that the consequences of carbon release cannot be deferred indefinitely. The ocean's biogeochemical machinery can reorganize faster than its circulation patterns, meaning that threshold-crossing events may precede any opportunity for gradual adaptation.

The productive uncertainty here concerns the spatial heterogeneity of the response. We now know that some basins developed photic zone euxinia while others did not, that some experienced seasonal oscillations while others maintained more stable conditions, and that the relationship between carbon release, warming, and oxygen depletion varied with local factors like basin geometry and restriction. Understanding why certain systems proved more vulnerable than others—and whether analogous modern systems share those characteristics—remains an active area of research with direct implications for predicting the trajectory of contemporary ocean deoxygenation.
