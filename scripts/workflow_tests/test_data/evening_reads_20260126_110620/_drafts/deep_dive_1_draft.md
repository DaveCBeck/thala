# The drilling ship and the smoking gun

# The Drilling Ship and the Smoking Gun

In the summer of 2021, the scientific drilling vessel *JOIDES Resolution* positioned itself over a curious depression on the seafloor of the Norwegian continental margin. Nearly 200 meters below the seabed lay the remains of an ancient explosion—a funnel-shaped crater that had torn through Paleocene sediments some 56 million years ago. The scientists aboard Expedition 396 had come to drill directly into what they believed was a crime scene: one of thousands of hydrothermal vent complexes that may have helped trigger the most dramatic warming event of the past 66 million years.

What they found would provide the most direct evidence yet linking North Atlantic volcanism to the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum. The crater infill contained the unmistakable chemical signature of the PETM itself—a sharp negative carbon isotope excursion coincident with the first appearance of *Apectodinium augustum*, a dinoflagellate cyst that serves as a global marker for this hyperthermal event [@SFHXHQXJ]. The vent had erupted at almost exactly the moment the planet began its dramatic lurch toward extreme greenhouse conditions.

Here's the thing about scientific smoking guns: they're rarely as clean as we'd like. The case for volcanic causation of the PETM has been circumstantial for decades—temporal coincidence, plausible mechanisms, consistent isotopic signatures. But IODP Expedition 396 and the broader investigation of North Atlantic Igneous Province volcanism have transformed this circumstantial case into something approaching forensic evidence, tracing the pathways by which magma became methane became warming.

## The North Atlantic Igneous Province: A Carbon Factory

To understand why geologists have long suspected the North Atlantic Igneous Province of triggering the PETM, you need to appreciate its sheer scale. The NAIP is among the largest known volcanic provinces in Earth's history, with an estimated 5–10 million cubic kilometers of magma emplaced at or near the surface within just a few million years [@47YBCEFM]. This wasn't gradual, distributed volcanism—it was a concentrated outpouring of molten rock accompanying the violent separation of Greenland from Europe.

The temporal overlap with the PETM was recognized early. High-precision dating of East Greenland flood basalts places massive volcanism at 56.1 ± 0.4 million years ago, synchronous with both the PETM and continental breakup [@INWM2JEG]. In East Greenland, flood basalts accumulated at rates exceeding 1 centimeter per year for roughly 500,000 years, building a volcanic pile 5–6 kilometers thick [@47YBCEFM]. This represents an extraordinary concentration of volcanic activity precisely when the carbon cycle went haywire.

But correlation isn't causation, and the volcanic trigger hypothesis required a mechanism. How exactly could erupting basalt deliver enough carbon to the atmosphere to warm the planet by 5°C?

The answer, it turns out, involves two distinct pathways—and the second proved far more important than early investigators suspected.

## Direct Volcanic CO₂: Necessary but Insufficient

The most obvious source of volcanic carbon is the CO₂ dissolved in magma itself. When basalt erupts, dissolved gases escape to the atmosphere. A province the size of the NAIP would have released prodigious quantities of CO₂ through this mechanism alone.

Quantifying this direct volcanic contribution has required painstaking geochemical detective work. Researchers have used trace element ratios—particularly strontium to samarium and strontium to neodymium—as proxies for original magmatic CO₂ content, since the volatile gases themselves were lost long ago to eruption and weathering [@ASJD2TES]. These analyses reveal substantial heterogeneity: CO₂ concentrations in Large Igneous Province tholeiitic magmas vary from roughly 1,000 to 7,500 ppm depending on the specific province and magma batch [@ASJD2TES].

The numbers suggest that direct volcanic CO₂ from the NAIP was substantial—but probably not sufficient on its own to explain the observed warming and carbon isotope excursion. This is where the volcanic trigger hypothesis becomes more interesting, because the real carbon factory operated underground.

## The Thermogenic Mechanism: Cooking Sediments

When magma intrudes into sedimentary basins, it doesn't just pile up on the surface as lava flows. Much of it infiltrates laterally as sills—horizontal sheets of molten rock that snake between sedimentary layers. The NAIP sill complexes are enormous, extending across the Norwegian, Faroese, and Greenland continental margins in a nearly continuous network [@47YBCEFM].

Here's where it gets climatically significant: these sills intruded into organic-rich Cretaceous and Paleocene sediments. Organic-rich shales, when heated to several hundred degrees Celsius, undergo thermal decomposition—they literally cook, releasing their carbon as methane and CO₂. Seismic surveys have revealed thousands of hydrothermal vent complexes on the Norwegian margin alone, explosive structures that formed at sill edges where gas pressure from this thermal metamorphism exceeded the strength of overlying rock [@LVFPSGAY].

The thermogenic methane hypothesis, first articulated in the early 2000s, proposed that this sediment-cooking mechanism could release carbon with a distinctive isotopic signature—much lighter (more negative δ¹³C) than volcanic CO₂ alone, because the source material was biogenic organic matter. This lighter carbon would be more efficient at producing the observed carbon isotope excursion per unit of warming.

But the hypothesis remained theoretical until someone could actually drill into one of these vent structures and determine when it erupted.

## Drilling the Modgunn Vent

The Modgunn Vent, located on the Norwegian margin less than 10 kilometers from the Paleocene-Eocene rift axis, presented an ideal target. High-resolution 3D seismic data showed its anatomy clearly: a funnel-shaped crater approximately 80 meters deep and 200–240 meters wide, connected to underlying sill complexes by a 400-meter-deep conduit of disrupted rock [@SFHXHQXJ].

Expedition 396 drilled five boreholes in a transect from the crater rim toward its center, recovering over 540 meters of core with exceptional completeness [@SFHXHQXJ]. The crater infill told a story of explosive formation followed by rapid, largely undisturbed sedimentation—exactly what you'd expect if a single catastrophic gas release had excavated the crater, which then filled passively with settling sediment.

The critical question was: when did this explosion occur?

Carbon isotope stratigraphy provided the answer. The lowermost crater fill sediments showed stable δ¹³C values matching underlying Paleocene strata—normal pre-PETM conditions. But approximately 10–15 meters above the vent base, δ¹³C plunged by roughly 6‰, marking the onset of the carbon isotope excursion [@SFHXHQXJ]. Coincident with this shift, the dinoflagellate *Apectodinium augustum* made its first appearance—a taxon that serves as a global index fossil for the PETM [@SFHXHQXJ].

The timing implications were stark: the vent had formed just before the onset of the PETM. The explosion that excavated this crater occurred perhaps a few thousand years before the carbon cycle went into crisis. And this was just one of thousands of similar structures along the North Atlantic margins.

## Shallow Water, Direct Atmospheric Injection

One finding from Expedition 396 proved particularly consequential for understanding climate impact: the Modgunn Vent erupted in very shallow water.

Multiple lines of evidence supported this conclusion. An erosional unconformity truncated the crater fill, indicating subaerial or very shallow marine conditions shortly after the vent formed [@SFHXHQXJ]. The palynological assemblages—pollen, spores, and dinoflagellate cysts—overwhelmingly reflected coastal to restricted marine environments rather than open ocean conditions [@SFHXHQXJ]. The diatom community was dominated by species typically associated with benthic or floating macroalgae, indicating water depths probably below wave base but not oceanic [@SFHXHQXJ].

This matters enormously for climate. When methane releases in deep water, much of it oxidizes to CO₂ during its slow rise through the water column. But in shallow water—or subaerial settings—methane can reach the atmosphere directly. And methane is approximately 25 times more potent as a greenhouse gas than CO₂ over decadal timescales [@SFHXHQXJ]. Shallow-water venting would have delivered a concentrated warming punch that deep-water release could not match.

The regional setting reinforced this interpretation. The Modgunn Vent sat close to the ocean-continent boundary during the Paleocene-Eocene transition, meaning many other vents along the margin would have been in similarly shallow or even subaerial positions [@SFHXHQXJ]. The entire North Atlantic rift zone was emerging as a massive, distributed source of direct atmospheric greenhouse gas injection.

## Mercury Traces the Volcanic Signal

The drilling results meshed with independent geochemical evidence that had been accumulating from sedimentary sections across the North Atlantic region and beyond. Mercury—a trace element preferentially emitted during volcanic eruptions—provided a complementary tracer of NAIP activity.

Mercury concentrations in PETM sediments show dramatic spatial variation that maps onto proximity to the NAIP. At the Grane field in the Norwegian North Sea, just hundreds of kilometers from active rift volcanism, mercury concentrations reached extraordinary levels—up to 90,100 ppb, orders of magnitude above anything recorded in the rock record [@LVFPSGAY]. Even 700–1500 kilometers away in Denmark, Hg/TOC anomalies reached values consistent with elevated volcanic input [@47YBCEFM; @LVFPSGAY].

The temporal pattern of mercury deposition proved equally informative. Hg/TOC anomalies were most prominent in two intervals: just before the onset of the carbon isotope excursion, and during its recovery phase [@LVFPSGAY]. These intervals coincided with abundant tephra layers—volcanic ash—providing direct evidence of explosive eruptions. Mercury accumulation rates during the PETM body reached 1.76 ng cm⁻² yr⁻¹, an order of magnitude higher than during the onset phase and substantially elevated above modern volcanic contributions [@47YBCEFM].

Osmium isotopes told a complementary story. Seawater ¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os ratios reflect the relative contributions of continental weathering (radiogenic) versus mantle-derived material (unradiogenic). Pre-PETM and PETM-body sediments in Svalbard and Denmark showed distinctly unradiogenic values, indicating substantial input from basalt weathering or direct volcanic contribution [@47YBCEFM; @LVFPSGAY]. A regional shift to unradiogenic osmium just before the PETM onset—observed at multiple localities—provided additional evidence for an early pulse of NAIP activity preceding the main carbon release [@LVFPSGAY].

## Assembling the Timeline

The combined evidence from drilling, geochemistry, and geochronology now supports a detailed—if still incomplete—timeline for how North Atlantic volcanism initiated the PETM.

Magmatic activity in the NAIP began intensifying in the latest Paleocene, perhaps 200,000 years before the PETM onset. Mercury anomalies and unradiogenic osmium isotopes appear in pre-PETM sediments at multiple localities, coincident with the first tephra layers recorded in Danish sequences after a prolonged volcanic hiatus [@47YBCEFM; @LVFPSGAY]. This pre-onset volcanic pulse may have initiated gradual warming that primed the climate system for more dramatic change.

The main acme of NAIP activity—dominated by flood basalt volcanism and thermogenic degassing from contact metamorphism—was concentrated in a remarkably brief interval of roughly 200,000 years, from approximately 56.0 to 55.8 Ma [@47YBCEFM]. This coincides almost exactly with the PETM carbon isotope excursion. Hydrothermal vent complexes along the Norwegian, Faroese, and Greenland margins erupted in rapid succession, releasing methane and CO₂ from thermally decomposing sediments directly into the shallow water column or atmosphere.

After the PETM body phase, volcanic activity continued but apparently shifted in character. The hundreds of thick basaltic ash layers in post-PETM Danish and North Sea sequences represent explosive phreatomagmatic eruptions—a style change that likely reflected evolving rift geometry as surface water bodies developed along the nascent Atlantic [@47YBCEFM]. Mercury accumulation rates declined substantially during the recovery phase, despite the dramatic increase in preserved ash layers [@47YBCEFM]. This apparent paradox suggests that the thermogenic carbon source—sill intrusion into organic-rich sediments—was more important than direct volcanic degassing for driving the PETM itself.

## The Complexity Beneath the Correlation

This narrative has satisfying internal consistency, but the relationship between NAIP volcanism and PETM warming remains more complex than a simple cause-and-effect story.

One puzzle involves the timing of peak volcanic evidence relative to the carbon isotope excursion. Mercury accumulation rates and Hg/TOC anomalies actually peaked during the PETM body and into the recovery phase, yet the carbon isotope excursion shows its most negative values—indicating maximum atmospheric carbon perturbation—during the onset and early body phases [@47YBCEFM; @LVFPSGAY]. This temporal offset could reflect changing eruption styles, evolving sediment source areas for mercury, or the complex filtering of volcanic signals through Earth system feedbacks.

Another complication involves spatial heterogeneity. Mercury anomalies at sites distant from the NAIP—in the Arctic Ocean and New Jersey, for instance—are muted or absent despite the global nature of the carbon isotope excursion [@LVFPSGAY]. Several explanations are possible: submarine mercury release may have been deposited locally before reaching distant basins; organic matter sourcing differences may have affected mercury preservation; or the volcanic mercury signal may simply have been diluted below detection at distal sites.

Perhaps most fundamentally, the magnitude of carbon release from thermogenic metamorphism alone may be insufficient to explain the full PETM warming. Estimates for carbon release from Norwegian margin hydrothermal vents range from 225 to 2,250 Gt C, with potential NAIP-wide thermogenic emissions reaching perhaps 13,000 Gt C [@47YBCEFM]. These numbers are substantial but at the lower end of estimates derived from pH proxy inversions, which suggest total carbon release of 10,000 Gt C or more [@JUNM4JH9].

This discrepancy points toward the likelihood that volcanic carbon release triggered additional feedbacks—methane hydrate destabilization, permafrost thawing, or other mechanisms that amplified the initial volcanic forcing. The volcanic trigger hypothesis is strengthened, not weakened, by this inference: the NAIP provided the match that lit a larger carbon cycle fire.

## What the Vents Reveal About Earth System Sensitivity

The drilling of the Modgunn Vent and the broader investigation of NAIP-PETM connections carry implications beyond resolving a paleoclimatic mystery. They illuminate how Earth's climate responds to carbon injection from geological sources—a question of more than academic interest given ongoing anthropogenic emissions.

The NAIP provides a natural experiment in Large Igneous Province carbon cycling. The proximity of detailed sedimentary records to the volcanic source, the exceptional preservation of organic biomarkers and volcanic proxies, and the ability to directly sample vent structures create an unusually complete picture of cause and consequence. Unlike more ancient LIP-climate associations, the PETM is recent enough that sedimentary archives retain molecular-level information about environmental conditions.

One lesson involves the timescales of volcanic forcing. The main phase of NAIP activity was brief by geological standards—roughly 200,000 years—yet the climate perturbation persisted for at least 150,000 years and affected Earth systems for far longer [@47YBCEFM]. The carbon cycle operates as an integrator of forcing, not a simple responder to it. Carbon injected over thousands of years remains in the Earth system for tens to hundreds of thousands of years, even as its distribution shifts between atmosphere, ocean, and sediments.

Another lesson involves the importance of volatile speciation and release pathway. The shallow-water setting of North Atlantic hydrothermal vents allowed methane to reach the atmosphere with minimal oxidation, enhancing the short-term warming effect [@SFHXHQXJ]. Had the same carbon been released in deeper water, more would have converted to CO₂ before reaching the atmosphere, potentially reducing peak warming while extending its duration. The geography and bathymetry of volcanic carbon release matter enormously for climate impact.

## Open Questions

Despite the accumulating evidence, fundamental uncertainties remain about the volcanic trigger mechanism.

The relative contributions of direct volcanic CO₂ versus thermogenic methane are still debated. Isotopic mixing calculations suggest that volcanic carbon dominated [@JUNM4JH9], but the exact partitioning depends on assumptions about magma chemistry, sediment organic content, and vent degassing efficiency that are difficult to constrain precisely. Drilling additional hydrothermal vent complexes—and especially catching one that has not been subsequently buried and altered—would help resolve this question.

The role of additional carbon cycle feedbacks in amplifying volcanic forcing remains unclear. Did NAIP warming destabilize methane hydrates? Trigger permafrost emissions? Acidify surface oceans sufficiently to impair the carbonate pump? Each of these mechanisms has been proposed, and each would amplify the initial volcanic carbon release, but distinguishing their contributions from the volcanic signal itself is challenging.

Finally, the transition from PETM conditions back to normal Paleocene-Eocene climate remains incompletely explained. If volcanic forcing initiated and maintained the warming, why did the PETM end despite continued volcanic activity during the recovery phase? The answer likely involves silicate weathering feedbacks—a story for elsewhere—but may also require changes in volcanic emission rates, emission speciation, or other factors that await investigation.

## The Smoking Gun, Refined

The case for volcanic initiation of the PETM has evolved from plausible hypothesis to strongly supported theory. The temporal coincidence between NAIP emplacement and the carbon isotope excursion is now established at resolution finer than the duration of the CIE itself. The mechanism—thermogenic gas release from sills intruding organic-rich sediments—has been demonstrated through direct drilling of hydrothermal vent structures. The pathway—shallow-water venting delivering methane directly to the atmosphere—has been documented through paleoenvironmental reconstruction of vent settings.

We can now trace the sequence: as the North Atlantic rift widened around 56 million years ago, magma infiltrated sedimentary basins along the Norwegian, Faroese, and Greenland margins. Contact metamorphism cooked organic-rich shales, generating methane and CO₂ in quantities sufficient to overwhelm the pressure-bearing capacity of overlying rock. Thousands of explosive vents breached the seafloor in shallow coastal waters, releasing greenhouse gases directly to the atmosphere. Global temperatures rose by 5°C or more, triggering additional carbon cycle feedbacks that sustained warming for over 100,000 years.

This is not the simple story of a single eruption triggering catastrophic warming. It is a more complex narrative of distributed magmatism, sediment-magma interaction, and geographic coincidence conspiring to create a nearly perfect natural experiment in greenhouse forcing. The drilling ship found its smoking gun—but the gun turned out to be thousands of hydrothermal vents firing in near-unison along the margins of a nascent ocean.
