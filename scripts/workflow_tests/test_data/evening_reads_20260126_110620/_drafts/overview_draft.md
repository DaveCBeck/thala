# Literature Review: the causes of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), sometimes also spelled Palaeocene

# The Fifty-Six Million Year Old Warning

Fifty-six million years ago, Earth experienced what might be the closest natural analog to what we're doing right now. Over a few thousand years—a geological eyeblink—massive amounts of carbon flooded into the atmosphere. Global temperatures spiked by 5-8°C. The oceans acidified. Oxygen-depleted dead zones spread across continental shelves. Ecosystems from the deep sea to freshwater lakes collapsed.

Then, slowly, the planet healed itself.

This event, the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum, has become something of an obsession for climate scientists. Not because it tells us exactly what will happen as we pump carbon into the atmosphere—the parallels are imperfect, and the timescales differ—but because it offers something rare: a complete record of what happens when Earth's carbon cycle goes haywire, and how the planet eventually recovers.

I've spent the past several months immersed in the scientific literature on the PETM's causes. What emerges from this research is both humbling and fascinating—a story of scientific detective work spanning three decades, where each answer seems to spawn new questions, and where the most important insights often come from unexpected places.

---

The basic outline of the PETM is well established. Around 56 million years ago, something injected between 3,000 and 10,000 gigatons of carbon into the ocean-atmosphere system [@Y4DAR7Z9; @4VJB3IE6]. We know this happened because the event left an unmistakable fingerprint in the geologic record: a sharp negative shift in carbon isotope ratios—the ratio of carbon-13 to carbon-12—preserved in everything from marine shells to terrestrial plant fossils to soil carbonates [@PKCA3IP9]. This "carbon isotope excursion" tells us that whatever carbon was released, it was isotopically light—depleted in carbon-13 relative to the background.

The warming that followed was extraordinary. Sea surface temperatures increased by 5-8°C globally, with even larger changes in some regions [@TBYRHSCY]. The Arctic Ocean, based on proxy reconstructions, may have reached temperatures comparable to a modern subtropical lagoon. The warming wasn't limited to the surface—deep ocean temperatures rose by more than 4°C, fundamentally reorganizing ocean circulation and chemistry [@PXR3JYSE].

But here's where things get complicated. Knowing that massive carbon release caused the warming doesn't tell us where that carbon came from. And for three decades, that question has driven some of the most intense debates in paleoclimate science.

The challenge is essentially forensic. The carbon isotope excursion constrains the isotopic composition of the source, but different sources can produce similar signatures depending on how much carbon they release. A relatively small amount of extremely isotopically light carbon—like methane from decomposing organic matter—could produce the same isotope shift as a much larger amount of moderately light carbon, like volcanic CO₂ or oxidized organic matter [@3HVXCE9H]. Scientists have had to triangulate from multiple lines of evidence, each with its own uncertainties and limitations.

---

For years, the leading hypothesis centered on methane hydrates—ice-like structures on the seafloor that trap methane within their crystalline lattice. The idea was elegant: initial warming from some external trigger destabilized these hydrates, releasing methane that caused additional warming, which destabilized more hydrates, in a runaway positive feedback [@DR9EQIKF]. The isotopic signature fit reasonably well, and the mechanism explained why the carbon release appeared so rapid.

But the methane hydrate hypothesis always had a problem. Methane hydrates don't spontaneously dissociate—something has to warm them first. The hypothesis required some initial trigger, some external push that started the cascade. What was that trigger?

Over the past decade, attention has increasingly focused on a massive volcanic province that was erupting at precisely the right time, in precisely the right place. The North Atlantic Igneous Province—the volcanic system that accompanied the opening of the Atlantic Ocean between Greenland and Europe—was in full swing around 56 million years ago [@INWM2JEG]. High-precision dating shows that peak volcanic activity coincided with the PETM, not just roughly but precisely, within the uncertainty of the measurements [@47YBCEFM].

The evidence linking this volcanism to the PETM goes beyond timing. Mercury concentrations in PETM sediments spike dramatically, particularly in regions close to the volcanic province [@LVFPSGAY]. Mercury is preferentially emitted during volcanic eruptions, so these spikes serve as independent tracers of volcanic activity. Osmium isotopes tell a similar story, with unradiogenic values indicating sustained volcanic input throughout the event [@47YBCEFM].

But perhaps the most compelling evidence comes from the seafloor itself. I explore this in detail in a separate piece, but the short version is this: recent ocean drilling expeditions have penetrated ancient hydrothermal vent structures in the North Atlantic and found direct evidence that these vents were erupting during the PETM, releasing methane and other volatiles directly into the atmosphere [@SFHXHQXJ]. The mechanism wasn't just volcanic CO₂—it was magma intruding into organic-rich sediments, cooking them, and releasing the resulting gases through thousands of seafloor vents.

---

If the volcanic trigger hypothesis is gaining ground, why does debate continue? The answer lies in the details—and the details matter enormously for understanding how Earth's climate system responds to carbon perturbations.

One major complication is timing. High-resolution records from the Atlantic Coastal Plain and elsewhere reveal that the PETM wasn't a single event but a sequence of changes with distinct phases [@AG7I2UA9; @WLLAETYT]. There appears to have been a "pre-onset excursion"—a smaller carbon release and warming pulse that preceded the main event by perhaps 2,000 years [@TBYRHSCY]. This pre-onset warming is crucial because it suggests that CO₂-driven climate change triggered positive carbon cycle feedbacks, rather than everything happening simultaneously.

The details of this pre-onset phase deserve their own exploration, which I provide elsewhere. But the key point is this: the evidence increasingly suggests a two-step process. Initial warming—probably from volcanic CO₂—crossed some threshold that destabilized additional carbon reservoirs, whether methane hydrates, permafrost, or other sources. The main carbon isotope excursion, the big shift that defines the PETM, appears to have involved carbon from these secondary sources as much as or more than from the volcanic trigger itself [@UTQMAH7X].

This interpretation resolves the long-standing tension between volcanic and methane hypotheses. They're not competing explanations—they're different parts of the same cascade. Volcanism pulled the trigger; positive feedbacks amplified the signal.

But even this more sophisticated understanding leaves significant uncertainties. Recent modeling work has produced a troubling result: the first phase of carbon release during the PETM cannot be associated with any currently proposed mechanism [@9DLALEHA]. The isotopic signature and timing don't match volcanic CO₂, don't match methane hydrates, don't match any of the usual suspects. This suggests that our inventory of possible carbon sources may be incomplete—that there are processes we haven't yet identified or adequately characterized.

---

Beyond the question of carbon sources, the PETM raises profound questions about how Earth's climate system responds to perturbations. One of the most important—and most debated—is the question of climate sensitivity: how much warming do you get for a given amount of carbon?

Standard calculations based on the carbon isotope excursion and the observed warming suggest that PETM climate sensitivity may have been higher than modern climate models predict [@9L889AVK]. Even accounting for uncertainties in carbon mass and isotopic composition, it's difficult to produce 5-8°C of warming from CO₂ forcing alone using conventional radiative transfer calculations [@QBL568GM]. Something seems to be missing.

Several possibilities have been proposed. Methane, if it reached the atmosphere before being oxidized to CO₂, would have exerted much stronger short-term warming per molecule [@SFHXHQXJ]. Changes in clouds or water vapor could have amplified the warming. The background climate state—already warmer than today—may have made the climate system more sensitive to additional forcing [@I3FZQVMT]. Or some combination of all three.

This question matters for more than academic reasons. If PETM climate sensitivity was genuinely higher than models suggest, it could indicate that Earth's climate system behaves nonlinearly under extreme forcing—that feedbacks kick in at high temperatures that don't operate, or operate weakly, under modern conditions. Understanding these potential nonlinearities is crucial for anticipating future climate change.

---

The PETM lasted approximately 170,000-270,000 years from onset to full recovery—recent astrochronological work has pushed duration estimates upward, with probabilistic assessments suggesting the carbon isotope excursion persisted for roughly 270,000 years [@7B8GX4DL]. This extended duration poses its own puzzles. Why did the event last so long? What processes eventually drew down the excess carbon and allowed the climate to recover?

The answer, increasingly, points to silicate weathering—the chemical breakdown of rocks that ultimately sequesters atmospheric CO₂ as carbonate minerals. This process is thought to regulate Earth's climate over geological timescales, but observing it in action during a specific event has proved challenging.

Recent advances in isotope geochemistry have changed this. Lithium isotopes in marine carbonates record a dramatic shift during the PETM, indicating that weathering intensity increased by 50-60% compared to pre-warming values [@3EA4CMM2]. Osmium isotopes tell a similar story, with a >10% increase in radiogenic osmium indicating enhanced weathering of continental crust [@HP97NAV7]. Terrestrial records from China show even more dramatic responses, with weathering intensity roughly doubling in some regions [@9P97Q29C].

The mechanisms driving this enhanced weathering are fascinating—and they involve the same volcanic province that may have triggered the event in the first place. I explore this in detail separately, but the short version is that the PETM recovery appears to have been driven by multiple reinforcing processes: intensified rainfall and runoff, expansion of chemical weathering zones, and the rapid breakdown of volcanic ash that blanketed large areas following NAIP eruptions [@7U76VLJY].

---

What, then, should we take away from three decades of research on PETM causation?

First, that Earth's carbon cycle contains positive feedbacks that can amplify initial perturbations. The PETM wasn't simply caused by volcanic CO₂—it was caused by volcanic CO₂ triggering additional carbon release from other reservoirs. Understanding these feedback mechanisms, and the thresholds that activate them, remains one of the most important challenges in climate science.

Second, that the climate system recovered, but slowly. The enhanced weathering that eventually drew down excess CO₂ operated on timescales of tens to hundreds of thousands of years. From a human perspective, this is effectively permanent—if we push the climate system into a new state, we should not expect natural processes to restore it within any timeframe relevant to civilization.

Third, that the rates matter. The PETM occurred over thousands of years—rapid by geological standards, but orders of magnitude slower than current anthropogenic emissions [@G5QN8JFL]. We are conducting an experiment that has no true analog in the geological record, pushing the system faster than it has been pushed before. The consequences of that speed remain uncertain.

Finally, that scientific understanding continues to evolve. The confident pronouncements of a decade ago—that methane hydrates were the primary source, that the event lasted 120,000 years, that we understood the basic story—have given way to a more nuanced and more uncertain picture. The volcanic trigger is now better supported than ever, but the details of carbon release remain contested. The duration has been revised upward. New sources and mechanisms continue to be proposed. This is how science works, but it's a reminder that our understanding of past climate events, like our understanding of future climate change, remains incomplete.

The PETM offers something rare in Earth science: a complete record of a rapid climate perturbation, from trigger to peak warming to recovery. It's an imperfect analog for contemporary change, but it's the best natural experiment we have. Understanding what caused it—and how Earth healed itself afterward—illuminates both the resilience and the fragility of the planetary systems we depend on.

The story has three parts that deserve deeper exploration: the remarkable evidence linking volcanic activity to the PETM trigger, preserved in rocks beneath the North Atlantic seafloor; the pre-onset warming that preceded the main event, providing our clearest window into how carbon cycle feedbacks operate; and the weathering response that ultimately drove recovery, showing how Earth's thermostat works on geological timescales.

Each of these threads reveals something important about how our planet responds to carbon perturbations—and each raises questions that remain, fifty-six million years later, not fully resolved.
