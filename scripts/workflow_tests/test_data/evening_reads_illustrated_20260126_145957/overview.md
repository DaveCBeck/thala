# Literature Review: the causes of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), sometimes also spelled Palaeocene

# When Earth Fever Spiked: What Ancient Warming Tells Us About Our Future

Fifty-six million years ago, something extraordinary happened to our planet. Over the span of a few thousand years—a geological eyeblink—global temperatures shot up by 5 to 8 degrees Celsius. The oceans warmed from surface to abyss. Tropical forests crept toward the poles. And somewhere, somehow, a massive pulse of carbon flooded into the atmosphere.

This event, the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum, has captivated scientists for three decades. Not because ancient history is inherently fascinating (though it is), but because the PETM offers something we desperately need: a real-world experiment in what happens when enormous amounts of carbon enter the climate system quickly. We can't run this experiment on the modern Earth—or rather, we're running it right now and would prefer to know the ending first.

What caused this ancient fever spike? The question sounds simple. The answer, after hundreds of studies and thousands of measurements, remains surprisingly elusive. And that elusiveness itself tells us something important about how our planet works.

---

## The Carbon Detective Story

The basic outline of the PETM isn't in dispute. Around 56 million years ago, between 3,000 and 10,000 gigatons of carbon entered the ocean-atmosphere system [@BXXAU5WS; @JUNM4JH9]. For context, humans have released roughly 500 gigatons since the Industrial Revolution. The ancient carbon injection was larger—and, relative to geological norms, shockingly fast.

We know this carbon was "light"—depleted in the heavy carbon-13 isotope that serves as a geochemical fingerprint. This depletion shows up in sedimentary records worldwide as the carbon isotope excursion, or CIE: a sudden 2.5 to 6 permil drop in δ¹³C values preserved in everything from deep-sea foraminifera to land plant waxes [@3HVXCE9H; @PKCA3IP9]. The excursion appears in rocks from New Jersey to New Zealand, from the Arctic to Antarctica. Whatever happened, it was global.

But here's where things get interesting. The magnitude of that isotopic shift depends entirely on what kind of carbon caused it. Methane, particularly methane from seafloor hydrates, carries an extremely light isotopic signature—around -60 permil. You'd need only 2,000 to 3,500 gigatons of such methane to produce the observed excursion [@Y4DAR7Z9]. Organic carbon from soils or sediments runs closer to -25 permil, requiring 6,000 to 12,000 gigatons to achieve the same isotopic effect [@4VJB3IE6]. Volcanic CO₂ is heavier still.

The math creates an uncomfortable trade-off. Light carbon sources require less mass but face other constraints—were there really enough methane hydrates in Paleocene oceans? Heavy carbon sources exist in abundance but demand massive release rates that strain credulity. Every proposed mechanism encounters its own set of objections.

This is the central puzzle that has driven PETM research for three decades. And the emerging answer is: it wasn't just one thing.

## The Volcanic Connection

For years, a coincidence nagged at researchers. The PETM occurred at almost exactly the same time as the North Atlantic Igneous Province—one of Earth's great volcanic outpourings—was ripping apart the continental margins between Greenland and Europe. Was this timing meaningful, or just bad luck for ancient life?

Recent work has transformed that coincidence into something closer to causation. High-precision argon dating now places massive flood basalt eruptions at 56.1 ± 0.4 million years ago, statistically indistinguishable from the PETM onset [@INWM2JEG]. Multiple geochemical tracers—mercury concentrations, osmium isotopes—confirm that volcanic activity persisted throughout the warming event [@47YBCEFM; @LVFPSGAY]. The temporal alignment is too tight to dismiss.

But direct volcanic CO₂ emissions face a scaling problem. Even using the highest measured CO₂ concentrations from similar large igneous provinces—around 7,500 parts per million in the magma [@ASJD2TES]—the erupted volume seems insufficient to generate the full carbon isotope excursion. Volcanoes alone can't explain the isotopic signature.

The deeper story here involves not what erupted at the surface, but what happened underground. I explore this in detail in a separate piece, but the essential insight is this: when magma intrudes into sedimentary basins rich in organic matter, it cooks that organic matter and releases its carbon as methane. The North Atlantic Igneous Province intruded into carbon-rich shales and mudstones. It baked them. And the evidence for what happened next has now been drilled directly from the seafloor.

## The Methane Question

For much of the past three decades, methane hydrates have been the default explanation for the PETM carbon. These ice-like structures, stable only under specific pressure and temperature conditions on continental margins, lock away enormous quantities of methane in modern oceans. Warm the seafloor enough, and theory suggests they should release that methane catastrophically.

The appeal is obvious. Methane is isotopically light enough to explain the carbon excursion with a plausible mass. It's a potent greenhouse gas that would amplify initial warming. And the seafloor warming documented in PETM records—more than 4 degrees Celsius at intermediate depths [@PXR3JYSE]—seems sufficient to push hydrates past their stability threshold.

But the methane hydrate hypothesis has always faced a timing problem. Heat doesn't penetrate marine sediments instantaneously. Temperature anomalies at the seafloor require 10,000 to 26,000 years to reach hydrate deposits 100 to 500 meters below [@US3YQCWH; @Q75969Z3]. This slow heat diffusion would produce a prolonged tail of methane release, potentially explaining why the PETM lasted so long. But it can't explain the rapid onset.

The most compelling recent evidence reframes hydrates not as the trigger, but as an amplifier. High-resolution records show that sea surface temperatures rose by approximately 2 degrees Celsius roughly 2,000 years *before* the main carbon isotope excursion began [@TBYRHSCY]. Something else started the warming. Then the carbon cycle kicked in. This sequence—warming first, then carbon release—suggests that methane hydrates responded to temperature change rather than causing it.

The observation of pre-onset warming has profound implications for understanding how the PETM unfolded. The early warning signal and what it means for interpreting the event's trigger mechanism is something I examine closely in another piece in this series.

## Temporal Puzzles

One of the most contentious debates in PETM research concerns timing. Not *when* the event occurred—that's reasonably well established—but *how fast* the carbon entered the system and *how long* the whole episode lasted.

Early studies suggested the carbon isotope excursion began almost instantaneously—one provocative analysis proposed a 13-year onset. That claim was definitively refuted by carbon cycle modeling showing such speed is physically impossible given ocean chemistry constraints [@NT6V92TQ]. More recent astrochronological work suggests the main excursion developed over approximately 6,000 years [@PZ3PSHWV], with some evidence for two distinct carbon pulses separated by 1,000 to 2,000 years [@WLLAETYT].

The total duration has also been revised upward. Recent probabilistic assessments place the PETM at roughly 270,000 years from onset to recovery [@7B8GX4DL]—substantially longer than earlier estimates around 120,000 to 170,000 years. This revision matters because longer durations imply slower carbon release rates, which influences which sources are plausible.

Complicating everything is the problem of preservation. The ocean acidification that accompanied carbon release dissolved carbonate sediments on the seafloor, preferentially erasing the peak of the event from deep-water sites [@PKCA3IP9]. Records from 3,600 meters depth show a carbon isotope excursion of only -1.5 permil; records from 1,500 meters show -3.5 permil. The deeper records are missing chunks of the story.

This dissolution artifact has driven researchers toward expanded shallow marine sections—particularly in the US Atlantic Coastal Plain, where the Marlboro Clay preserves PETM sediments more than 15 meters thick, with sedimentation rates one to two orders of magnitude higher than deep-sea deposits [@RV2M2PC2]. These sections offer the temporal resolution to see fine structure that deep-sea cores cannot preserve.

## Feedback Cascades

The emerging synthesis suggests the PETM wasn't a single event with a single cause, but rather a cascade of interconnected processes. Some initial perturbation—likely volcanic, given the temporal correlation with North Atlantic Igneous Province activity—pushed the climate system past a threshold. That initial warming then triggered secondary carbon releases from hydrates, permafrost, or organic-rich sediments. These secondary releases amplified the warming, potentially triggering further releases.

Two-stage modeling of the carbon release supports this interpretation [@9DLALEHA]. The first stage, lasting roughly 3,000 years, involved 900 to 1,100 gigatons of carbon with an isotopic signature consistent with organic matter or thermogenic methane. The second stage, lasting about 1,000 years, involved additional carbon with a more depleted signature consistent with biogenic methane from hydrates. Critically, the modeling concluded that the first stage "cannot be associated with any known PETM hypothesis" as currently formulated—suggesting our understanding of the initial trigger remains incomplete.

Mercury concentrations tell a similar story. Volcanic mercury signals peak *before* and *during* the early carbon isotope excursion, then decline precisely as the largest isotopic shift occurs [@UTQMAH7X]. The volcanoes apparently started something that then took on a life of its own.

This cascade model has important implications for understanding climate thresholds more generally. It suggests that moderate forcing can trigger disproportionate responses if the climate system contains reservoirs of carbon that become unstable under warming. Modern Earth has such reservoirs—permafrost, methane hydrates, soil carbon. The PETM demonstrates that these can, under sufficient forcing, release their carbon into an already warming atmosphere.

## The Ocean's Response

The carbon that flooded into the PETM atmosphere didn't stay there. Much of it dissolved into the ocean, where it triggered cascading biogeochemical changes that affected marine life from surface to seafloor.

The most direct consequence was acidification. Dissolved CO₂ forms carbonic acid, lowering ocean pH. During the PETM, this acidification drove carbonate dissolution across large areas of the seafloor—the same dissolution that has made some deep-sea records incomplete [@PKCA3IP9; @FQ4RI4B9]. Surface waters experienced pH declines of 0.08 to 0.35 units during the pre-onset phase alone [@AG7I2UA9].

But the ocean's response extended far beyond acidification. In many marginal marine settings, bottom waters became anoxic—entirely depleted of oxygen. Some locations developed euxinic conditions, with hydrogen sulfide accumulating in the water column [@C5A4BTTB; @YFSXQJH4]. In extreme cases, this sulfidic water extended into the photic zone where sunlight penetrates, creating conditions lethal to most marine life but hospitable to certain photosynthetic bacteria.

The spatial pattern of these changes was remarkably heterogeneous. Different ocean basins, different continental shelves, and different depth ranges experienced the biogeochemical perturbation at different times and different intensities [@YFSXQJH4]. The PETM ocean wasn't uniformly stagnant; it was a patchwork of locally extreme conditions superimposed on global warming.

I examine the ocean's toxic response in detail in a separate piece. The story there involves not just chemistry but the complete disruption of marine ecosystems—and offers sobering parallels to changes already underway in modern oceans.

## Recovery

Perhaps the most remarkable aspect of the PETM is that Earth recovered. The climate system didn't tip into a permanent hothouse. Within 100,000 to 200,000 years, temperatures returned to something like normal. The question is: how?

The answer lies in negative feedbacks—processes that counteract warming once it begins. The most important of these is silicate weathering. When CO₂ rises and climate warms, rainfall intensifies and chemical weathering of rocks accelerates. This weathering consumes atmospheric CO₂, eventually drawing carbon out of the atmosphere and ocean and sequestering it in carbonate sediments.

The evidence for enhanced weathering during PETM recovery is now overwhelming. Lithium isotopes record a 50 to 60 percent increase in weathering intensity, with erosion rates climbing two to three fold [@3EA4CMM2]. Osmium isotopes show a greater than 10 percent shift toward radiogenic values, indicating massive delivery of continentally-derived material to the oceans [@HP97NAV7]. Regional terrestrial records document even more dramatic responses, with weathering intensity doubling in some locations [@9P97Q29C].

But weathering operates slowly. The lag between carbon injection and weathering-driven CO₂ drawdown explains why the PETM lasted so long. The climate system had to wait for rock weathering to catch up with the carbon pulse—a process that required tens of thousands of years.

Interestingly, the volcanic ash from North Atlantic Igneous Province eruptions may have inadvertently accelerated recovery. Basaltic material weathers rapidly, and volcanic-derived minerals dominated PETM sediments in some regions [@7U76VLJY]. The same volcanism that may have triggered the event also deposited reactive material that helped terminate it.

## What We Still Don't Know

After thirty years of intensive research, fundamental uncertainties remain. The relative contributions of different carbon sources—volcanic degassing, thermogenic methane from contact metamorphism, biogenic methane from hydrates, organic carbon from soils and sediments—remain poorly constrained [@3HVXCE9H]. The precise mechanisms linking initial volcanic activity to the massive carbon release that defines the event are still debated [@UTQMAH7X]. The size and distribution of Paleocene methane hydrate reservoirs, crucial for evaluating the hydrate hypothesis, remain essentially unknown [@Y4DAR7Z9].

Perhaps most troubling is the persistent mismatch between carbon cycle constraints and climate constraints. The amount of carbon consistent with the isotopic signature, when processed through climate models, produces less warming than the proxy records indicate [@9L889AVK; @QBL568GM]. Something is missing—additional greenhouse gases, cloud feedbacks, circulation changes, or fundamental gaps in our understanding of climate sensitivity under extreme warmth.

These gaps matter for more than academic reasons. The PETM remains our best natural analog for rapid anthropogenic carbon release. If we can't fully explain what happened 56 million years ago, our ability to project the consequences of modern emissions is correspondingly limited.

## Why This Matters Now

The PETM offers both reassurance and warning. The reassurance: Earth recovered. The climate system has mechanisms for absorbing and sequestering even massive carbon injections. Life persisted, adapted, and eventually flourished again.

The warning: recovery took 100,000 to 200,000 years. The negative feedbacks that restore equilibrium operate on geological timescales, not human ones. For the organisms that lived through the PETM—including the mammals ancestral to primates—the event was a permanent feature of their existence, not a temporary perturbation.

Moreover, modern carbon emissions are proceeding faster than even the most rapid phase of PETM carbon release [@G5QN8JFL; @QBL568GM]. We are running an experiment more extreme than the geological precedent we look to for guidance. The positive feedbacks documented in the PETM—hydrate destabilization, permafrost thaw, organic carbon oxidation—exist in our world too. Whether we'll cross thresholds that trigger comparable cascades remains an open question.

What the PETM demonstrates with uncomfortable clarity is that the carbon cycle contains tipping points. Push it hard enough, and secondary processes amplify the initial perturbation. The Earth system doesn't respond linearly to forcing; it has modes, and transitions between them can be rapid and irreversible on human timescales.

The PETM isn't a perfect analog for modern climate change. The background climate was different, the continental configurations were different, the ecosystems were different. But it remains our best window into how the Earth system responds when carbon arrives faster than the planet can process it. The message from 56 million years ago is clear: the climate system has thresholds, and crossing them has consequences that outlast human civilizations by orders of magnitude.
