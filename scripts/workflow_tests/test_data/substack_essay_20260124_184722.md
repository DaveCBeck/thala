# The Problem With 56-Million-Year-Old Fingerprints

In 2023, a drilling ship retrieved cores from a strange geological formation beneath the Norwegian Sea—a structure that looked, from seismic imaging, like a frozen explosion. The formation was a hydrothermal vent complex, one of thousands that dot the seafloor in this region, and when researchers examined the sediments filling it, they found something remarkable: the vent had erupted almost exactly 56 million years ago, during the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum, one of the most dramatic warming events in Earth's history [@Berndt et al., 2023].

The timing was too precise to be coincidence. The sediments contained *Apectodinium augustum*, a dinoflagellate species that appears in marine sediments worldwide at the onset of the PETM, and the carbon isotope signature matched perfectly with the beginning of the event's characteristic excursion. Here was physical evidence that volcanic activity in the North Atlantic had vented carbon directly into the atmosphere at the exact moment the planet began its catastrophic warming.

But here's the thing: we've known for thirty years that something released an enormous amount of carbon into the atmosphere during the PETM, causing global temperatures to spike by 5-8°C in just a few thousand years [@Kirtland Turner et al., 2017]. We can see the fingerprint of this carbon everywhere—in the shells of ancient foraminifera, in the chemistry of paleosols, in the teeth of early mammals. The carbon isotope excursion (CIE) is unmistakable, a sudden negative shift of 2.5-4‰ that appears in marine and terrestrial records across the globe [@McCarren et al., 2008]. What we can't agree on is where that carbon came from, how fast it was released, or even how much there was.

The Norwegian vent provides a tantalizing clue, but it also illustrates the central puzzle of PETM research: every proposed mechanism for releasing the required carbon faces serious objections, and the more precisely we measure the event, the more difficult it becomes to explain with any single cause.

## The Carbon Budget Problem

The fundamental challenge is arithmetic. To produce the observed carbon isotope excursion, you need to inject a massive amount of isotopically light carbon—carbon depleted in the heavier ¹³C isotope—into the ocean-atmosphere system. But the amount required depends entirely on how light your carbon source is.

If the carbon came primarily from methane hydrates, which have δ¹³C values around -60‰, you might need only 2,000-3,500 Pg C. If it came from volcanic degassing or burning organic matter, with δ¹³C values closer to -25‰, you'd need 6,000-12,000 Pg C [@Dickens, 2011]. This is not a small uncertainty—it spans nearly an order of magnitude, and different carbon sources operate through entirely different mechanisms with different implications for the rate and duration of release.

The situation gets more complicated when you try to match the carbon isotope data with other constraints. When researchers use Earth system models to simulate the PETM, they find that reproducing the observed pattern of carbonate dissolution on the seafloor—shells dissolving as the ocean acidified—requires at least 6,800 Pg C if the source had a δ¹³C of -22‰ [@Panchuk et al., 2008]. But that's just a minimum, and it assumes conditions that might not be correct.

Gerald Dickens, one of the pioneers of PETM research, has spent years pointing out that methane hydrate dissociation remains "the most congruous but unproven explanation" for the event [@Dickens, 2011]. The appeal is obvious: methane locked in ice-like structures on continental margins represents a carbon reservoir with exactly the right isotopic signature to produce the CIE with a relatively modest mass release. Early modeling showed that dissociating roughly 10^18 grams of oceanic methane hydrate could produce the observed 2.5‰ excursion while simultaneously shoaling the carbonate compensation depth by hundreds of meters [@Dickens et al., 1997]. The mechanism seemed elegant: initial warming from some other source penetrates the seafloor, destabilizing hydrates, releasing methane that causes more warming, destabilizing more hydrates, and so on.

The problem is that no one can identify what triggered the initial warming with enough precision to confirm the chain of events. And increasingly precise chronologies have revealed that the CIE appears to have multiple phases and precursors that don't fit neatly into a simple feedback story.

## The Volcanic Alternative

The Norwegian vent complex represents the strongest direct evidence for an alternative hypothesis: that volcanic activity from the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) drove the PETM. This wasn't ordinary volcanism. As the North Atlantic rifted apart approximately 56 million years ago, enormous volumes of magma intruded into organic-rich sedimentary rocks, cooking them and releasing their carbon as methane and CO₂ [@Svensen et al., 2004]. The hydrothermal vents that formed—thousands of them, according to seismic surveys—would have acted as chimneys, piping greenhouse gases directly from heated sediments into the atmosphere.

The temporal correlation is striking. High-precision argon dating of East Greenland flood basalts places massive eruptions at 56.1 ± 0.4 Ma, precisely coincident with both the PETM and continental breakup [@Storey et al., 2007]. Multiple independent geochemical tracers—mercury accumulation rates, osmium isotope ratios—confirm sustained volcanic activity throughout the carbon isotope excursion [@Jones et al., 2023]. Mercury, which is preferentially emitted during volcanic eruptions, shows enrichments reaching 90,100 ppb in North Sea sediments proximal to the NAIP, an order of magnitude higher than modern volcanic contributions [@Jones et al., 2019].

Critically, the shallow-water setting of these vents meant that greenhouse gases could enter the atmosphere directly rather than being oxidized in the water column—methane reaching the atmosphere exerts a much more potent warming effect than if it were converted to CO₂ in the ocean [@Berndt et al., 2023].

But the volcanic hypothesis faces its own challenges. Direct measurements of CO₂ concentrations in large igneous province magmas reveal enormous heterogeneity—ranging from 1,080 to 7,471 ppm depending on the specific province, with one to two orders of magnitude variation within individual magmatic suites [@Black & Aiuppa, 2023]. This makes extrapolating from limited geochemical measurements to total carbon budgets highly uncertain. And while thermogenic methane from contact metamorphism could be isotopically light enough to explain the CIE magnitude, quantifying how much organic carbon was actually baked out of those sediments remains difficult.

There's another problem with single-source explanations, whether volcanic or hydrate-based: neither seems sufficient to explain everything we observe. Modeling studies have demonstrated that methane hydrate destabilization alone cannot account for the observed patterns of seafloor carbonate dissolution [@Panchuk et al., 2008], while CO₂ forcing consistent with carbon cycle constraints cannot explain the 5-8°C global temperature rise recorded in proxy records [@Zeebe et al., 2009]. Something is missing.

## The Multi-Phase Structure

Perhaps the most important development in PETM research over the past decade has been the recognition that carbon release was not a single catastrophic event but a complex, multi-phase process. High-resolution records have revealed a pre-onset excursion (POE)—a 1.0 to 1.5‰ negative CIE preceding the main event, accompanied by surface ocean warming of at least 2°C and ocean acidification with pH declines of 0.08 to 0.35 units [@Babila et al., 2022]. This POE represents rapid carbon release of approximately 400-1,600 Gt C over centuries to millennia, before the main event even began.

Even more striking, the main CIE onset itself appears to involve two distinct pulses. Gabriel Bowen and colleagues documented what they describe as "two massive, rapid releases of carbon during the onset of the Palaeocene-Eocene thermal maximum" [@Bowen et al., 2014], separated by roughly 1-2 thousand years. Mercury geochemical data add another layer: volcanic signals correlate with the first carbon isotope step, while the second step—representing the largest isotopic shift—shows an absence of volcanic signatures, indicating activation of secondary carbon reservoirs [@Kender et al., 2021].

This temporal structure creates a conceptual shift in how we should think about PETM causation. Rather than asking "was it volcanoes or methane hydrates?", the evidence increasingly points toward a cascade: volcanic CO₂ emissions triggering initial warming, which crossed some threshold that then destabilized additional carbon reservoirs—potentially hydrates, potentially permafrost, potentially oxidation of soil organic matter—that amplified the perturbation far beyond what volcanism alone could produce.

The two-stage modeling work of Carozza and colleagues illustrates both the promise and frustration of this approach [@Carozza et al., 2011]. Their simulations proposed Stage 1 (approximately 3,000 years) characterized by atmospheric methane release of 900-1,100 Pg C with δ¹³C values of -22 to -30‰, followed by Stage 2 (1,000 years) involving mixed emissions totaling 900-1,400 Pg C with more depleted δ¹³C values of -50 to -60‰. But critically, they concluded that Stage 1 results "cannot be associated with any known PETM hypothesis"—the initial carbon release requires either rapid organic carbon oxidation or thermogenic methane sources that don't fit existing frameworks. We may be missing something fundamental about how the event started.

## The Duration Debate

If the multi-phase structure complicates our understanding of PETM initiation, debates over total event duration complicate our understanding of its recovery. Recent astrochronological work has dramatically revised previous estimates. Using Monte Carlo-based probabilistic uncertainty assessments, Piedrahita and colleagues demonstrated that the PETM carbon isotope excursion lasted 268.8 +21.2/-20.5 kyr [@Piedrahita et al., 2025]—substantially longer than the widely cited range of 120-230 kyr [@Westerhold et al., 2017].

This matters enormously for understanding Earth system feedbacks. A longer event implies slower carbon release rates but also slower recovery, with implications for how efficiently negative feedbacks like silicate weathering can draw down atmospheric CO₂. The extended duration also creates an opportunity for heat to propagate deep into marine sediments—thermal diffusion calculations show that temperature anomalies at the sediment-water interface require 10-26 thousand years to penetrate to depths where methane hydrates would have been located [@Zeebe, 2013]. This slow heat penetration could explain why the PETM lasted so long: methane release from hydrate dissociation would have continued for tens of thousands of years after the initial warming pulse, extending the event far beyond what a simple carbon injection scenario would predict.

The debate over onset duration has been even more contentious. Early work suggesting an extraordinarily rapid 13-year onset was definitively refuted by modeling showing that such a timeline is physically infeasible given climate and ocean chemistry constraints [@Zeebe et al., 2014]. More recent astrochronological analysis of high-resolution records from the Atlantic Coastal Plain concludes that the CIE onset lasted approximately 6 kyr [@Li et al., 2022], achieving confidence levels exceeding 98.6% for astronomically forced variations. But this millennial-scale onset still contrasts sharply with earlier estimates ranging from years to 20,000 years [@Kirtland Turner et al., 2017], and the discrepancy highlights how much analytical methodology shapes our conclusions.

Part of the problem is that deep-sea carbonate records—the traditional archive for paleoclimate reconstruction—are compromised by the very phenomenon they're trying to record. The ocean acidification accompanying PETM carbon release caused preferential dissolution of carbonate deposited during the peak CIE at deeper sites, effectively erasing portions of the isotopic record [@McCarren et al., 2008]. The benthic foraminiferal CIE measures -3.5‰ at 1,500 m paleodepth but only -1.5‰ at 3,600 m paleodepth—a systematic bias that truncates apparent event duration and attenuates CIE magnitude. This dissolution truncation has driven researchers toward expanded shallow marine sections, particularly on the U.S. Atlantic Coastal Plain, where sedimentation rates were 1-2 orders of magnitude higher than deep-sea deposits [@Robinson et al., 2024].

## Ocean Biogeochemistry in Crisis

While debates over timing and carbon sources continue, the record of ocean biogeochemical responses provides unambiguous evidence for the severity of PETM environmental disruption. This wasn't just warming—it was a fundamental reorganization of ocean chemistry.

High-resolution geochemical reconstructions from the North Sea reveal a rapid transition from oxic to fully euxinic (sulfidic) bottom water conditions coinciding with CIE onset [@Mariani et al., 2024]. The rapidity of this redox transition suggests a threshold-crossing response rather than gradual deterioration. Perhaps most striking is the evidence for photic zone euxinia—sulfidic conditions extending into sunlit surface waters—documented through the presence of isorenieratene, a biomarker from photosynthetic green sulfur bacteria that require both light and hydrogen sulfide [@Sluijs et al., 2014]. When sulfidic waters penetrate shallow enough to intersect with the euphotic zone, it represents a fundamental reorganization of upper ocean chemistry and ecology.

The development of these extreme conditions reflects complex interactions between warming-driven stratification, enhanced nutrient delivery from intensified hydrological cycling, and restricted water mass exchange in semi-enclosed basins. Different ocean basins and settings exhibited variable timing and intensity of biogeochemical disruption [@Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009], highlighting that the PETM's marine responses cannot be characterized as uniformly anoxic but rather as a spatially heterogeneous mosaic.

Continental freshwater ecosystems may have fared even worse. Lacustrine records from the Jianghan Basin in eastern China document temperature increases of approximately 11°C—far exceeding the global average—coincident with ecosystem collapse and mass mortality events linked to toxigenic cyanobacterial blooms [@Chen et al., 2023]. Freshwater systems, with their smaller buffering capacity and limited dispersal opportunities, were catastrophically vulnerable.

## The Recovery and What It Tells Us

If the onset of the PETM reveals the fragility of Earth's climate system to rapid carbon perturbation, the recovery reveals its resilience—and the timescales on which that resilience operates.

Lithium isotope records demonstrate that silicate weathering rates increased by 50-60% during the PETM, with erosion rates increasing 2-3 fold [@Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021]. Osmium isotopes provide independent confirmation, showing a >10% increase in ¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os ratios consistent with intensified continental weathering [@Ravizza et al., 2000]. High-resolution terrestrial records from China reveal approximately 100% increases in regional silicate weathering intensity, with strong coupling between carbon isotope variations and weathering changes on millennial timescales [@Chen et al., 2023].

This enhanced weathering reflects the activation of Earth's thermostat: CO₂-driven warming intensified the hydrological cycle, accelerating chemical weathering that ultimately consumes atmospheric CO₂ through reactions with silicate minerals. The resulting products are carried to the ocean, where they're incorporated into carbonate sediments, sequestering the carbon.

The NAIP itself may have played a dual role—both as a carbon source through volcanism and as a critical weathering substrate that accelerated recovery. Sediments from the eastern North Sea show that volcanically-derived minerals dominated PETM deposits, with highly reactive zeolites and smectites comprising large fractions of the mineralogy [@Stokke et al., 2021]. These volcanic materials weather rapidly compared to typical continental rocks.

But the key lesson is temporal: recovery took approximately 100,000-200,000 years. The negative feedbacks that stabilize Earth's climate operate on geological timescales, not human ones. Carbon injected rapidly takes tens to hundreds of millennia to be removed, regardless of how efficiently weathering responds.

## What We Still Don't Know

Three decades of PETM research have produced remarkable progress in reconstructing this ancient warming event, yet fundamental questions remain unresolved.

The relative contributions of different carbon sources—volcanic degassing, thermogenic methane from contact metamorphism, hydrate dissociation, organic matter oxidation—remain poorly quantified. We know that multiple sources contributed, but parsing their relative importance and temporal relationships requires constraints we don't yet have on Paleocene carbon reservoir sizes, particularly methane hydrate distributions.

The triggering mechanism for the cascade remains uncertain. Evidence for pre-onset warming preceding the CIE by roughly 2,000 years [@Frieling et al., 2019] suggests that initial warming—perhaps from volcanic CO₂—crossed some threshold that activated additional carbon reservoirs. But the Stage 1 carbon release in two-stage models "cannot be associated with any known PETM hypothesis" [@Carozza et al., 2011], implying our framework may be incomplete.

The relationship between carbon release rates and environmental impacts needs further investigation. Current estimates suggest PETM carbon release occurred at rates roughly an order of magnitude slower than modern anthropogenic emissions [@Babila et al., 2022], yet the event still produced devastating environmental consequences. Understanding why requires better constraints on feedback mechanisms and climate sensitivity under warm background states.

Perhaps most fundamentally, we face the problem of equifinality: multiple combinations of processes could produce observationally similar outcomes. Progress may ultimately depend less on achieving perfect proxy resolution and more on developing frameworks that can quantify irreducible uncertainties [@Kirtland Turner et al., 2017] and identify which aspects of PETM causation can be confidently constrained versus those that remain fundamentally ambiguous.

## Why This Matters Now

The PETM offers the closest geological analog to anthropogenic climate change—a rapid injection of carbon into the ocean-atmosphere system under greenhouse conditions, producing warming, acidification, and ecological disruption. The comparison is not perfect: modern carbon release rates appear to exceed those of the PETM by roughly an order of magnitude [@Babila et al., 2022], and background conditions differ in important ways.

But the PETM demonstrates several things that are directly relevant to our current trajectory. First, that threshold behaviors exist in the Earth system: gradual carbon addition can trigger nonlinear environmental changes including rapid warming, widespread ocean deoxygenation, and ecological reorganization. Second, that negative feedbacks can restore climate equilibrium, but only over multi-millennial timescales—far too slow to prevent severe impacts on biological systems. Third, that multiple carbon reservoirs can be activated in cascade, potentially amplifying an initial perturbation far beyond its direct effects.

The drilling ship that retrieved those cores from the Norwegian Sea provided a physical piece of the puzzle—direct evidence linking volcanic activity to the moment of climate catastrophe. But it also illustrated how much remains unknown. The vent was one of thousands. How much carbon did they collectively release? How did that carbon interact with other reservoirs? What determined whether the cascade stopped or continued?

Fifty-six million years later, we're conducting another experiment in rapid carbon release, this time with ourselves as both experimenters and subjects. The PETM can't tell us exactly what will happen, but it can tell us what's possible—and what's possible includes transformations of Earth's climate and ecosystems far more severe than anything in recorded human history. The ancient warming event that opened the modern era of mammalian diversification began with processes we're only beginning to understand. The warmth that followed lasted for millions of years.
