## 3. Volcanic and Magmatic Carbon Sources

# Volcanic and Magmatic Carbon Sources

The hypothesis that volcanic and magmatic processes drove the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum has gained substantial empirical support, with the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) emerging as the primary candidate for carbon release. This volcanic trigger hypothesis posits that both direct CO₂ emissions from massive flood basalt eruptions and indirect carbon mobilization through magma-sediment interactions released sufficient greenhouse gases to drive the extreme warming event. Multiple independent lines of evidence—including radiometric dating, geochemical tracers, and direct drilling of volcanic structures—now provide a coherent narrative linking NAIP activity to the PETM, though significant uncertainties remain regarding the precise mechanisms and relative contributions of different carbon sources.

## Temporal Correlation and North Atlantic Igneous Province Magmatism

Establishing the temporal relationship between NAIP volcanism and PETM onset has been critical to evaluating the volcanic trigger hypothesis. High-precision ⁴⁰Ar/³⁹Ar dating of East Greenland flood basalts demonstrates that massive volcanism occurred at 56.1 ± 0.4 million years ago, temporally coincident with both the PETM and continental breakup in the Northeast Atlantic [@INWM2JEG]. This chronological alignment is further refined by recent integrated geochemical analyses showing that peak NAIP volcanism occurred during the body of the PETM itself, spanning approximately 200,000 years from 56.0 to 55.8 Ma [@47YBCEFM]. The eruption style during this critical interval was dominated by effusive volcanism and thermogenic degassing, with volcanic activity persisting throughout the carbon isotope excursion rather than representing a singular pulse [@47YBCEFM]. Notably, the character of NAIP volcanism evolved after the PETM, shifting from effusive eruptions to explosive phreatomagmatic activity in the early Eocene as developing rift systems allowed greater magma-water interactions [@47YBCEFM].

## Direct Volcanic CO₂ Emissions from Large Igneous Provinces

Quantifying the carbon dioxide content of NAIP magmas has proven essential for assessing whether direct volcanic emissions alone could drive the PETM. Empirical analysis using trace element-gas correlations indicates that CO₂ concentrations in Large Igneous Province tholeiitic magmas vary substantially between provinces, ranging from 1,080 to 7,471 ppm depending on the specific LIP [@ASJD2TES]. The Siberian Traps exhibit the highest CO₂ concentrations at approximately 4,500 ppm, while the Deccan Traps contain the lowest at roughly 1,500 ppm [@ASJD2TES]. Critically, CO₂/S ratios and absolute CO₂ concentrations vary by one to two orders of magnitude within individual LIP magma suites, indicating substantial heterogeneity and systematic chemical evolution throughout the magmatic episode [@ASJD2TES]. A comprehensive assessment combining multiple geochemical and paleoenvironmental proxies concludes that volcanic carbon constituted the dominant source during the PETM [@JUNM4JH9], though the precise volume of CO₂ released directly from NAIP magmas remains subject to uncertainty given the heterogeneity in magma chemistry.

## Thermogenic Methane from Contact Metamorphism

Beyond direct magmatic CO₂, the interaction of NAIP magmas with carbon-rich sedimentary rocks provided a potentially massive source of greenhouse gases through contact metamorphism. The volcanic trigger mechanism specifically invokes greenhouse gas release from magma intrusion into organic-rich sediments during Northeast Atlantic opening [@INWM2JEG]. Modeling of volcanic basin systems suggests that methane release from such interactions served as a significant driver of early Eocene warming [@L358KUND]. The relative importance of this thermogenic methane pathway compared to direct volcanic CO₂ remains a central debate within the volcanic hypothesis framework. Mercury accumulation rates during the PETM body were an order of magnitude greater than modern volcanic contributions, with this elevated mercury linked specifically to thermogenic degassing processes accompanying effusive volcanism [@47YBCEFM], suggesting that sediment-heating mechanisms were indeed active and substantial during peak NAIP activity.

## Shallow-Water Hydrothermal Vent Complexes

Direct geological evidence for carbon release mechanisms has emerged from drilling of North Atlantic hydrothermal structures. IODP Expedition 396 penetrated a hydrothermal vent complex in the NAIP, revealing that shallow-water hydrothermal vents erupted during the PETM itself, directly releasing methane and other volatiles into the atmosphere [@SFHXHQXJ]. Crucially, the shallow-water setting of these vents enabled greenhouse gases to bypass oxidation to CO₂, delivering methane directly to the atmosphere where it exerts a more potent warming effect [@SFHXHQXJ]. Stable carbon isotope stratigraphy and dinoflagellate cyst biostratigraphy—specifically the appearance of *Apectodinium augustum*—firmly tie the infilling of these vent structures to PETM onset, with vent formation occurring only a few millennia before the carbon isotope excursion [@SFHXHQXJ]. This near-synchronicity between vent activity and CIE onset provides compelling evidence for a direct causal relationship rather than mere temporal coincidence.

## Subcrustal Carbon Mobilization

Beyond carbon sourced from surface sediments and magmas themselves, evidence suggests that deeper reservoirs contributed to the carbon budget. Subcrustal carbon was mobilized coincident with the PETM, representing an additional carbon source that contributed to extreme climate warming [@IAXX62EA]. This mobilization of carbon from deep crustal and subcrustal sources occurred simultaneously with the thermal maximum event [@IAXX62EA], though the mechanisms driving this deeper carbon release and its quantitative contribution to the total carbon budget remain poorly constrained compared to shallower volcanic and thermogenic sources.

## Mercury as a Volcanic Activity Proxy

Mercury enrichments in PETM sediments provide an independent tracer of volcanic activity, as mercury is preferentially emitted during volcanic eruptions and subsequently preserved in marine sediments. Mercury anomalies across PETM sections display strong spatial variation, with the greatest concentrations—reaching up to 90,100 ppb—occurring proximal to the NAIP in the Grane field, North Sea [@LVFPSGAY]. The temporal distribution of Hg/TOC anomalies is particularly informative: these enrichments are most prevalent prior to CIE onset and during the CIE recovery phase, intervals that coincide with abundant tephra layers providing direct evidence of explosive volcanic activity [@LVFPSGAY]. This temporal pattern suggests that volcanic activity may have preceded and outlasted the main carbon isotope excursion, though the relationship between mercury deposition and the specific carbon release mechanisms driving the CIE requires further investigation.

## Alternative Carbon Sources: Comet Impact Contributions

While the volcanic hypothesis has gained predominance, alternative carbon sources have been proposed, most notably a comet impact at the Paleocene-Eocene boundary. Multiproxy geochemical analysis of North Atlantic Ocean drill cores—incorporating osmium isotopes, mercury, sulfur, and platinum group elements—provides evidence that both a comet impact (approximately 3.3 km diameter) and volcanism occurred during the boundary interval [@2S4QVJSR]. However, quantitative estimates indicate that this comet contained only approximately 0.4 Gt of carbon, far less than the 3,000-10,000 Gt required to drive the PETM [@2S4QVJSR]. This negligible carbon contribution effectively rules out comet impact as a primary driver of the thermal maximum, though the impact may have contributed to environmental perturbations through other mechanisms such as atmospheric dust loading or wildfires.

## Outstanding Questions and Future Research Directions

Despite substantial progress in characterizing volcanic carbon sources, critical uncertainties persist. The precise quantification of carbon released through different volcanic mechanisms—direct magmatic degassing, thermogenic methane from contact metamorphism, hydrothermal venting, and subcrustal mobilization—remains elusive due to challenges in constraining the spatial extent of intrusions, the organic carbon content of affected sediments, and the efficiency of carbon transfer from sediments to atmosphere. Better temporal constraints on individual volcanic phases relative to the detailed structure of the CIE onset would clarify whether carbon release occurred as a single massive pulse or through multiple episodes. Additionally, comparative analysis of carbon release rates from volcanic processes versus alternative mechanisms such as methane hydrate destabilization would inform our understanding of which processes could plausibly generate the rapid CIE onset observed in high-resolution records. The heterogeneity in magma chemistry within individual LIPs [@ASJD2TES] further complicates efforts to extrapolate from limited geochemical measurements to total carbon budgets, highlighting the need for extensive sampling across the full temporal and spatial extent of NAIP magmatism.

## 4. Methane Hydrate Dissociation and Positive Feedbacks

# Methane Hydrate Dissociation and Positive Feedbacks

The dissociation of oceanic methane hydrates has emerged as the leading, though still controversial, explanation for the massive carbon injection that drove the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum. The appeal of this mechanism lies in its capacity to explain both the magnitude and isotopic character of the carbon isotope excursion (CIE), which requires an input of 2,000-12,000 Gt of carbon depending on the isotopic composition of the source [@Y4DAR7Z9]. Early modeling demonstrated that massive dissociation of approximately 1.12 × 10^18 g of oceanic methane hydrate could produce the observed δ13C excursion of -2.5‰ over a 10,000-year period, while simultaneously causing lysocline shoaling of up to 400 meters and increasing global surface temperatures by approximately 2°C [@DR9EQIKF]. The thermal dissociation hypothesis gained further support from observations that bottom water temperatures increased by more than 4°C during a brief interval at approximately 55.6 Ma, creating conditions sufficient to destabilize methane hydrates at intermediate water depths [@PXR3JYSE]. However, despite its explanatory power, the methane hydrate hypothesis remains "the most congruous but unproven explanation" for the PETM, with significant uncertainties regarding reservoir sizes, isotopic compositions, and triggering mechanisms [@Y4DAR7Z9].

## Thermal Dissociation Mechanisms and Heat Transfer Dynamics

A critical constraint on methane hydrate dissociation scenarios involves the physical process of heat transfer through marine sediments. High-resolution isotopic analysis revealed that the initial carbon isotope excursion was geologically instantaneous and preceded by a brief period of gradual surface-water warming, providing evidence for thermal destabilization of hydrate reservoirs [@362Y5E26]. However, the mechanism operates on timescales that extend far beyond the initial warming pulse. Heat conduction calculations demonstrate that temperature anomalies generated at the sediment-water interface require 10-26 thousand years to penetrate deep-sea sediments to depths of 100-500 meters, where methane hydrate deposits would have been located [@US3YQCWH]. This slow heat penetration creates a prolonged tail of methane hydrate dissociation that continues long after the initial warming phase, offering a physical explanation for the extended duration of the PETM that conventional carbon cycle models fail to reproduce with a single instantaneous carbon pulse [@US3YQCWH]. The combination of slow methane release from subsurface sediment hydrate dissociation, reduced hydrate deposition, and enhanced sedimentary metabolic processes can account for the 50+ thousand year duration of the event [@US3YQCWH].

## Multiple Injection Events and Two-Stage Models

The temporal structure of carbon release during the PETM appears more complex than a single massive hydrate dissociation event. Oxygen and carbon isotope records from geographically distant sites reveal multiple discrete methane injection events separated by periods of stable carbon cycling, suggesting episodic rather than continuous methane release [@FVQEB59G]. This observation has led to increasingly sophisticated two-stage models of the PETM onset. The most detailed of these proposes Stage 1, lasting approximately 3,000 years, characterized by atmospheric methane release of 900-1,100 Pg C with δ13C values of -22 to -30‰, followed by Stage 2, lasting 1,000 years, involving mixed emissions totaling 900-1,400 Pg C (including 300-500 Pg C as CO2 to the ocean and 400-900 Pg C as CH4 to the atmosphere) with more depleted δ13C values of -50 to -60‰ [@9DLALEHA]. Critically, this modeling revealed that Stage 1 results "cannot be associated with any known PETM hypothesis," requiring either rapid organic carbon release or thermogenic CH4 sources and suggesting that current PETM hypotheses may require fundamental revision [@9DLALEHA]. This finding highlights a significant gap in our understanding of the initial trigger mechanism.

## Pre-Onset Warming and Positive Feedback Mechanisms

One of the most significant recent developments in PETM research involves the identification of pre-onset warming that preceded the carbon isotope excursion. High-resolution analysis of marine sediments reveals that global sea surface temperatures increased by approximately 2°C roughly 2,000 years before the onset of the CIE [@TBYRHSCY]. This pre-onset warming provides crucial evidence that CO2-driven climate change triggered a positive carbon cycle feedback, rather than methane release serving as the initial cause of warming [@TBYRHSCY]. This temporal sequence resolves a longstanding debate about causation: methane hydrates appear to have been destabilized by antecedent warming rather than spontaneously releasing methane that then caused warming. The existence of pre-onset warming also helps explain the recurrence of hyperthermal events throughout the early Eocene, as multiple events (ETM2/H1, H2, I1, I2, K/X) show patterns coupled to orbital forcing and appear to share similar generic causes with the PETM, implying the existence of a large dynamic carbon reservoir that could be repeatedly triggered by relatively modest orbital-induced warming [@Y4DAR7Z9].

## Alternative and Complementary Carbon Sources

While methane hydrate dissociation remains the leading hypothesis, alternative sedimentary carbon sources may have contributed significantly to the PETM carbon budget. Enhanced petrogenic organic carbon (OCpetro) mobilization occurred in subtropical and mid-latitude regions during the PETM, with increases in mass accumulation rates ranging from 2×10^-2 to 7×10^-2 gC cm^-2 kyr^-1 [@GTA8KIWY]. Notably, high-latitude sites showed limited changes in organic carbon sources, suggesting spatially variable feedback mechanisms rather than a globally uniform carbon source [@GTA8KIWY]. Additionally, an approximately 3‰ exaggeration in the magnitude of the CIE recorded in total organic carbon (δ13CTOC) has been identified, attributable to increased terrestrial organic carbon contributions during the event, with a strong correlation (R² = 0.91) between δ13CTOC and the BIT index, a proxy for terrestrial versus marine organic matter [@3HVXCE9H]. This finding necessitates careful consideration of which carbon pools accurately record the global exogenic carbon signal.

## Geochemical Evidence for Methane Seepage

Independent geochemical evidence supports enhanced methane cycling during the PETM. Oceanic barium burial rates increased by a factor of approximately three during the PETM across continental shelf, slope, and deep ocean settings, requiring an additional source of barium to the ocean [@TBYRHSCY]. This elevated barium burial is consistent with enhanced methane seepage, as barium precipitation is associated with anaerobic oxidation of methane in marine sediments, providing indirect but compelling evidence for widespread methane release from seafloor sources [@TBYRHSCY]. The spatial pattern of barium enrichment across diverse depositional environments suggests that methane seepage was a globally significant phenomenon rather than a localized event.

Despite decades of research, fundamental questions remain unresolved. Quantification of Paleocene methane hydrate reservoir sizes and locations remains poorly constrained, making it difficult to assess whether available reservoirs were sufficient to explain the full magnitude of the CIE. Better constraints on the isotopic composition of released methane are essential for distinguishing between biogenic and thermogenic sources. The discrepancy between two-stage modeling results and known PETM hypotheses particularly for Stage 1 emissions suggests that our current framework may be missing critical processes or carbon sources [@9DLALEHA]. Understanding the spatial heterogeneity in hydrate dissociation responses, as evidenced by variable petrogenic carbon mobilization across latitudes [@GTA8KIWY], will be crucial for developing comprehensive models of this extraordinary climate event.

## 5. Carbon Isotope Stratigraphy, Chronology, and Event Duration

# Carbon Isotope Stratigraphy, Chronology, and Event Duration

The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), occurring approximately 55.5 million years ago, represents the most dramatic and rapid hyperthermal event of the Cenozoic, characterized by a pronounced negative carbon isotope excursion (CIE) and global temperature increases of 5-8°C [@SAB4DKXU; @RV2M2PC2]. Understanding the precise timing, duration, and structure of this event has proven essential for constraining carbon release mechanisms and evaluating its relevance as an analog for anthropogenic climate change. However, despite decades of intensive research, fundamental questions regarding the chronology of the PETM remain contentious, with competing methodologies and proxy records yielding divergent estimates for both the onset and total duration of the event.

## PETM Duration Estimates and Astrochronology

Perhaps the most striking disagreement in PETM research concerns estimates of total event duration and the timing of carbon release. Recent astrochronological work has dramatically revised upward previous duration estimates. [@7B8GX4DL] employed Monte Carlo-based probabilistic uncertainty assessments to demonstrate that the PETM CIE lasted 268.8 +21.2/-20.5 kyr, substantially longer than the widely cited range of approximately 120-230 kyr. This revision has profound implications for carbon release rates and climate sensitivity calculations. Critically, [@7B8GX4DL] demonstrated that the traditional 'G point' approach for identifying PETM termination—based on the return to pre-excursion baseline values—is unreliable due to sensitivity to underlying uncertainties and noise in δ¹³C records, highlighting fundamental methodological challenges in defining event boundaries.

The duration of the CIE onset phase has generated even more heated debate. Early work suggesting an extraordinarily rapid 13-year onset was definitively refuted by [@NT6V92TQ], who demonstrated through carbon cycle modeling that such a timeline is physically infeasible given climate and ocean chemistry constraints. More recently, [@PZ3PSHWV] applied rigorous astrochronological methods including Lomb-Scargle spectral analysis and statistical evaluation using COCO and ASM techniques to high-resolution records from the Howards Tract cores on the Atlantic Coastal Plain, concluding that the CIE onset lasted approximately 6 kyr. Their analysis achieved confidence levels exceeding 98.6% for astronomically forced variations, with significance levels below 0.05 for statistical tests of orbital forcing [@PZ3PSHWV]. This millennial-scale onset duration contrasts sharply with earlier estimates ranging from years to 20,000 years, underscoring how analytical methodology fundamentally shapes our understanding of carbon release dynamics.

## Pre-Onset Excursion and Multi-Phase Carbon Release

Recognition of a pre-onset excursion (POE) has transformed understanding of PETM carbon dynamics from a single-pulse to a multi-phase event. [@AG7I2UA9] documented a 1.0 to 1.5‰ negative CIE preceding the main PETM excursion, accompanied by surface ocean warming of at least 2°C and ocean acidification with pH declines of 0.08 to 0.35 units. This POE represents rapid carbon release of approximately 400-1600 Gt C over centuries to millennia, released primarily into atmospheric and surface ocean reservoirs [@AG7I2UA9]. The existence of environmental precursors to the main carbon injection was further supported by [@AYMERNS9], who identified distinct environmental changes preceding the Palaeocene/Eocene boundary. 

[@WLLAETYT] provided additional evidence for complexity in carbon release timing, arguing that the PETM involved two massive, rapid carbon release events rather than a single continuous injection. This multi-phase structure has critical implications for identifying carbon sources and understanding feedback mechanisms, as different phases may reflect distinct reservoirs or triggering processes. The temporal structure of carbon release remains an active area of investigation, with the relative timing and magnitude of the POE versus main excursion potentially varying across depositional settings.

## Carbon Isotope Excursion Magnitude and Substrate Effects

Determining the true magnitude of the atmospheric CIE—essential for estimating total carbon input—has been complicated by systematic differences among substrate types. [@PKCA3IP9] documented strong covariance between benthic foraminifera and terrestrial n-alkane biomarkers from the same cores at Site 1263, supporting a rapid CIE of at least -3.5‰ but no more than -5.0‰. However, [@JUUPBBR2] revealed critical complications in interpreting terrestrial organic matter records, demonstrating differential carbon isotope fractionation between plant groups during the PETM. Conifers exhibited a 3‰ negative CIE while angiosperms showed a 6‰ excursion, meaning the previously reported 4.5‰ CIE in terrestrial C₂₉ n-alkanes represents an averaged signal of mixed inputs rather than a direct atmospheric CO₂ signal [@JUUPBBR2]. This differential fractionation reflects physiological differences in carbon fixation and potentially differential responses to environmental changes including temperature, humidity, and atmospheric CO₂ concentrations.

The Tanzania section studied by [@9SVR963C] documented large terrestrial and marine carbon and hydrogen isotope excursions, providing additional evidence for substantial isotopic shifts across multiple proxies. However, reconciling different substrate records to determine the true atmospheric CIE magnitude remains a fundamental challenge, particularly given uncertainties about baseline isotopic compositions and environmental factors affecting fractionation.

## Depth-Dependent Dissolution and Record Truncation

A critical methodological concern affecting deep-sea carbonate records involves depth-dependent dissolution effects. [@PKCA3IP9] demonstrated that the benthic foraminiferal CIE is strongly depth-dependent, measuring -3.5‰ at 1500 m paleodepth (Site 1263) versus only -1.5‰ at 3600 m paleodepth (Site 1262). This pattern is consistent with carbonate dissolution truncation, wherein acidification during the PETM caused preferential dissolution of carbonate deposited during the peak CIE at deeper sites, effectively erasing portions of the isotopic record [@PKCA3IP9]. Such dissolution effects systematically bias duration estimates derived from deep-sea carbonates toward shorter values and attenuate apparent CIE magnitudes, potentially explaining some discrepancies among published records.

Understanding dissolution truncation is essential for accurately reconstructing carbon release rates. Records from intermediate depths in relatively well-preserved sections, such as those from Walvis Ridge, provide more complete archives than deeper sites but still require careful evaluation of preservation [@PKCA3IP9]. The depth dependency of dissolution also complicates spatial comparisons and requires site-specific assessment of carbonate preservation when synthesizing global datasets.

## High-Resolution Coastal Plain Records and Terrestrial-Marine Integration

Recognition of the limitations of deep-sea carbonate records has driven intensive focus on expanded shallow marine sections. [@RV2M2PC2] emphasized that US Atlantic Coastal Plain sediments, particularly the Marlboro Clay, contain exceptionally thick PETM records exceeding 15 m, with sedimentation rates 1-2 orders of magnitude higher than deep-sea deposits, enabling submillennial temporal resolution. These expanded sections avoid the dissolution issues plaguing deep-sea carbonates while providing integrated terrestrial and marine signals [@RV2M2PC2]. The Howards Tract cores analyzed by [@PZ3PSHWV] exemplify the power of such records for astrochronological analysis, with sufficient temporal resolution to identify orbital forcing patterns in calcium and magnetic susceptibility data.

However, coastal plain records introduce their own interpretive challenges, including potential terrestrial dilution effects, variable sediment accumulation rates, and complexities in correlating between sites. [@RV2M2PC2] proposed a comprehensive drilling strategy with 10 strategically located cores to address these issues through spatial replication and to enable robust correlation across the coastal plain. Such transects promise to resolve outstanding questions about spatial variability in PETM expression and environmental responses.

## Outstanding Challenges and Future Directions

Despite substantial progress, reconciling competing duration estimates and understanding spatial variability in CIE magnitude remain critical challenges. The 170 kyr duration cited by [@SAB4DKXU] contrasts markedly with the ~270 kyr estimate of [@7B8GX4DL], reflecting both genuine analytical differences and potentially variable definitions of event boundaries. Improved statistical methods for uncertainty quantification, as applied by [@7B8GX4DL], represent important methodological advances but must be applied consistently across records to enable meaningful comparisons.

The massive carbon release during the PETM, involving over 4500 Gt of carbon [@RV2M2PC2] or at least 1500 Gt [@SAB4DKXU], occurred over timescales that remain imperfectly constrained, with critical implications for evaluating the event as an analog for modern emissions. Resolving debates about onset duration, distinguishing the POE from main excursion phases, and accounting for substrate-specific and depth-dependent biases in isotopic records will require continued integration of high-resolution terrestrial and marine records with robust astrochronological frameworks and advanced statistical approaches.

## 6. Carbon Cycle Modeling and Source Quantification

# Carbon Cycle Modeling and Source Quantification

Understanding the carbon cycle dynamics of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM) represents one of the most significant challenges in paleoclimate research. The fundamental puzzle—how to reconcile the magnitude of observed warming, the isotopic signature of carbon inputs, and the patterns of seafloor carbonate dissolution—has driven extensive modeling efforts over the past two decades. These numerical investigations have progressively revealed that simple, single-source explanations are insufficient to explain the PETM's complexity, leading to increasingly sophisticated multi-component models that integrate atmospheric, oceanic, and biospheric responses across multiple timescales [@HGJY9YK6; @QBL568GM].

## Carbon Source Quantification and Isotopic Constraints

Early attempts to quantify the PETM carbon release relied on linking the observed negative carbon isotope excursion (CIE) to specific carbon sources with distinct isotopic signatures. The challenge lies in the fundamental relationship between carbon mass, its δ¹³C value, and the resulting perturbation to ocean and atmosphere chemistry. Model-data comparisons using intermediate complexity Earth system models have established minimum carbon requirements based on seafloor carbonate dissolution patterns. Using an early Eocene paleogeography configuration, Panchuk et al. demonstrated that reproducing the observed CaCO₃ dissolution requires at least 6,800 Pg C with a δ¹³C of -22‰ [@4VJB3IE6]. This minimum estimate assumes a relatively ¹³C-enriched source; if more depleted carbon sources dominated (such as methane with δ¹³C around -60‰), substantially less mass would be required to generate the observed CIE, but other geochemical constraints become increasingly difficult to satisfy.

The isotopic approach reveals a critical tension in PETM modeling: sources capable of producing the observed 2.5-4‰ CIE vary dramatically in their total mass requirements depending on their isotopic composition. Estimates consequently range from approximately 1,500 Pg C for extremely ¹³C-depleted sources to over 6,800 Pg C for sources more similar to terrestrial organic matter [@4VJB3IE6; @QBL568GM]. This wide range reflects fundamental uncertainty about source composition and highlights the need for additional geochemical constraints beyond the carbon isotope record alone.

## Multiple Carbon Sources and the Inadequacy of Methane-Only Hypotheses

The methane hydrate hypothesis, while initially attractive for explaining both the rapid onset and isotopic signature of the PETM, faces significant challenges when subjected to rigorous Earth system modeling. Panchuk et al. demonstrated that methane hydrate destabilization alone cannot account for the observed patterns of seafloor carbonate dissolution, leading to the conclusion that volcanic CO₂, organic carbon oxidation, and thermogenic methane must have contributed alongside any potential hydrate release [@4VJB3IE6]. This "beyond methane" perspective, articulated by Higgins and Schrag, argues for a multi-component theory incorporating various carbon sources and climate feedback mechanisms rather than relying on a single dominant process [@BXXAU5WS].

The recognition of multiple carbon sources has profound implications for understanding PETM dynamics. Different sources operate on different timescales: volcanic degassing occurs over thousands to tens of thousands of years, while contact metamorphism of organic-rich sediments can release carbon more rapidly. Each source also has distinct chemical signatures beyond carbon isotopes, including effects on alkalinity, nutrient cycles, and ocean redox conditions. Comprehensive Earth system models must therefore incorporate the geochemical fingerprints of each potential contributor to determine which combination best matches the paleoceanographic record [@HGJY9YK6].

## Climate Forcing, Climate Sensitivity, and the CO₂ Insufficiency Problem

Perhaps the most significant challenge to emerge from carbon cycle modeling is the apparent insufficiency of CO₂ forcing alone to explain PETM warming magnitudes. Using paleoclimate models and radiative forcing calculations, Zeebe et al. demonstrated that atmospheric CO₂ increases consistent with carbon cycle constraints cannot account for the 5-8°C global temperature rise observed in proxy records [@9L889AVK; @QBL568GM]. This finding suggests that additional climate forcing mechanisms—potentially including other greenhouse gases, changes in atmospheric water vapor, cloud feedbacks, or alterations to ocean circulation—must have amplified the initial CO₂-driven warming.

Understanding this discrepancy requires distinguishing between different measures of climate sensitivity. Equilibrium Climate Sensitivity (ECS) describes the temperature response to CO₂ doubling including only "fast feedbacks"—water vapor, clouds, sea ice, and lapse rate changes—that operate on timescales of years to decades. In contrast, Earth System Sensitivity (ESS) incorporates additional "slow feedbacks" including ice sheet dynamics, vegetation changes, and carbon cycle feedbacks that operate over centuries to millennia [@9UXGAISZ; @2W5LC24Q]. For deep-time paleoclimate events like the PETM, where the perturbation persisted for over 100,000 years, slow feedbacks had ample time to operate, meaning the observed temperature response reflects ESS rather than ECS [@NZHCUYVP; @JBCNYFIP].

This distinction has profound implications for interpreting PETM warming. Fast-feedback climate sensitivity is estimated at approximately 3±1°C for doubled CO₂ based on multiple lines of evidence, while equilibrium sensitivity including slow surface albedo feedbacks approaches 6°C for doubled CO₂ [@9UXGAISZ; @2W5LC24Q]. Furthermore, recent multi-proxy reconstructions indicate that climate sensitivity is state-dependent, with warmer climates exhibiting higher sensitivity than cooler ones [@I3FZQVMT; @5KWUB7G4]. Proxy evidence from the Eocene demonstrates that ECS was significantly higher during the warmest intervals (including the PETM and Early Eocene Climatic Optimum) and declined as global climate cooled through the Eocene, driven primarily by cloud feedbacks and secondarily by changing paleogeography and ocean circulation [@I3FZQVMT]. This state-dependency means that applying modern sensitivity estimates to reconstruct PETM forcing may systematically underestimate the temperature response to a given CO₂ increase.

The Deep-time Model Intercomparison Project (DeepMIP) has established coordinated experimental protocols to test climate models under early Eocene conditions, specifying simulations at 3×, 6×, and 12× pre-industrial CO₂ to represent different scenarios for pre-PETM, PETM, and Early Eocene Climatic Optimum climates [@NL8GKWDD]. These coordinated experiments enable systematic evaluation of how well models reproduce proxy-constrained temperatures and help identify which models with specific climate sensitivities best match paleoclimate observations. Recent analyses demonstrate that CMIP6/PMIP4 ensemble mean simulations show remarkable agreement with paleoclimate proxy data for global mean surface temperatures across multiple past climate periods, and that models with very high (>5°C) or very low (<2°C) equilibrium climate sensitivity simulate paleoclimate temperatures outside proxy uncertainty ranges [@R2FBJDRM]. This suggests that paleoclimate data—including the PETM—provide more stringent constraints on climate sensitivity than historical records alone.

Climate model and proxy data comparisons have refined understanding of spatial warming patterns while highlighting model limitations. Dunkley Jones et al. showed that climate models and paleoceanographic proxies provide complementary constraints on ocean temperature changes, with models helping to interpret proxy signals and data exposing model deficiencies in representing high-latitude amplification and deep-ocean warming [@KPX8V7AQ]. Recent work confirms that deep ocean temperature serves as a robust proxy for global mean surface temperature throughout the Cenozoic, with a 1:1 scaling relationship, yielding revised Cenozoic temperature reconstructions that are substantially warmer than previous estimates and support higher-than-modern ECS estimates of 4.5-5.7°C or potentially higher during warm periods [@Q69ZBYNY]. The persistent model-data mismatch, particularly in simulating the degree of polar amplification and deep-ocean temperature increases, points to missing or inadequately represented processes in current Earth system models, including potentially important non-CO₂ greenhouse gas contributions and inadequately constrained background climate states [@9L889AVK].

## Carbon Release Rates and Multi-Pulse Scenarios

The temporal structure of carbon release fundamentally affects both climate response and biogeochemical cycling. Cui et al. demonstrated that slow, sustained carbon release over several thousand years produces different ocean chemistry and temperature trajectories than rapid, pulse-like emissions [@JM6F7DZE]. This finding has important implications for identifying potential carbon sources, as different mechanisms operate at different rates. Volcanic processes typically sustain emissions over 10-20 kyr, while sediment heating or slope failure events might inject carbon more rapidly.

Increasingly, evidence points toward multi-stage carbon release rather than a single pulse. Two-stage scenarios, with an initial trigger followed by secondary carbon cycle feedbacks, can better reproduce the structure of the CIE and the extended recovery phase. These models suggest that initial warming from one source (potentially volcanic CO₂) triggered additional carbon releases through positive feedbacks such as permafrost thaw, organic matter oxidation, or limited hydrate dissociation [@BXXAU5WS; @4VJB3IE6]. High-fidelity benthic isotope records demonstrate that carbon cycle perturbations consistently lagged behind initial warming by approximately 23,000 years at the 405-kyr eccentricity band and 3,000-4,500 years at the 100-kyr band, indicating that temperature-sensitive light carbon was released as a positive feedback to orbital forcing rather than as the primary driver [@69H5XANR]. Understanding these feedback mechanisms is crucial for assessing Earth system sensitivity to perturbations and for drawing parallels to modern carbon emissions.

## Carbon Cycle Recovery and Long-Term Responses

The recovery phase of the PETM, lasting approximately 100-200 kyr, provides critical constraints on long-term carbon cycle dynamics and silicate weathering feedbacks. Earth system models must reproduce not only the event itself but also the gradual return to background conditions through enhanced weathering and organic carbon burial. The timescale of recovery constrains the balance between carbon input rates and removal processes, with implications for understanding how the Earth system regulates atmospheric CO₂ over geological timescales [@JM6F7DZE; @HGJY9YK6]. Recent modeling reviews emphasize that the PETM recovery offers valuable insights into carbon cycle stabilization mechanisms applicable to other hyperthermal events throughout Earth history [@PZUTM9CI].

Despite significant advances, fundamental uncertainties remain in carbon cycle modeling of the PETM. Better constraints on Paleocene background climate states, including ocean circulation patterns and pre-event atmospheric CO₂ levels, are essential for accurately simulating the perturbation. Recent high-resolution boron isotope-derived CO₂ records show that atmospheric CO₂ peaked at approximately 1470-1790 ppm during the Early Eocene Climatic Optimum and fluctuated substantially during the PETM interval, providing crucial boundary conditions for modeling efforts [@I3FZQVMT; @Z2KSPL2R]. The relative contributions of different carbon sources, the importance of non-CO₂ greenhouse gases, and the magnitude of climate feedbacks all require further investigation through improved model-data integration [@9L889AVK; @KPX8V7AQ]. As Earth system models increase in complexity and resolution, and as new geochemical proxies emerge, the PETM will continue to serve as a crucial test case for understanding how the carbon cycle and climate system respond to massive, geologically rapid carbon release events.

## 7. Ocean Biogeochemical Responses: Anoxia, Acidification, and Biotic Change

# Ocean Biogeochemical Responses: Anoxia, Acidification, and Biotic Change

The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum triggered profound perturbations in marine biogeochemistry that manifested as widespread ocean deoxygenation, shifts in carbonate chemistry, and cascading effects through marine and freshwater ecosystems. These environmental changes, driven by massive carbon release and resulting hyperthermal conditions, offer critical insights into how ocean systems respond to rapid greenhouse warming. The PETM's marine responses were characterized by significant spatial heterogeneity, with different ocean basins and settings exhibiting variable timing and intensity of biogeochemical disruption. Understanding these ocean responses is essential for reconstructing the complete Earth system feedback mechanisms during this event and for contextualizing potential futures under anthropogenic warming scenarios [@G5QN8JFL].

## Ocean Anoxia and Euxinia Development

Among the most dramatic marine responses to PETM warming was the development of oxygen-depleted and sulfidic (euxinic) conditions across multiple ocean basins. High-resolution geochemical reconstructions from the North Sea reveal a rapid transition from oxic to euxinic bottom water conditions coinciding with the onset of the carbon isotope excursion, documented through multiple independent redox proxies including molybdenum isotopes, pyrite framboid size distributions, and Fe/Al ratios [@YFSXQJH4]. This transition was not merely a shift to suboxic conditions but progressed to fully euxinic states where both oxygen and sulfide were present in the water column. The rapidity of this redox transition suggests a threshold-crossing response in ocean biogeochemistry rather than a gradual deterioration of oxygenation.

Importantly, the spatial expression of anoxia during the PETM was heterogeneous, with paleoceanographic reconstructions revealing that redox, productivity, and basin restriction changes occurred at different times relative to the carbon isotope excursion across different settings [@YFSXQJH4]. This spatial variability reflects the complex interplay between local basin geometry, water mass circulation patterns, productivity regimes, and connection to open ocean waters. While some marginal marine settings developed strongly euxinic conditions, others maintained more oxygenated states, highlighting that the PETM ocean response cannot be characterized as uniformly anoxic but rather as a spatially heterogeneous mosaic of redox conditions.

## Photic Zone Euxinia and Biomarker Evidence

Perhaps the most extreme manifestation of ocean deoxygenation during the PETM was the development of photic zone euxinia—sulfidic conditions extending into sunlit surface waters—documented through distinctive biomarker evidence. Studies from the Gulf Coastal Plain reveal the presence of sulfur-bound isorenieratene, a biomarker derived from photosynthetic green sulfur bacteria that require both light and hydrogen sulfide, providing unambiguous evidence for photic zone euxinia [@C5A4BTTB]. This condition represents a fundamental reorganization of upper ocean chemistry and ecology, where sulfidic waters penetrated sufficiently shallow depths to intersect with the euphotic zone. The occurrence of photic zone euxinia appears to have been seasonal along the Gulf margin, suggesting dynamic oscillations between oxygenated and euxinic states rather than permanent sulfidic conditions [@C5A4BTTB].

The development of photic zone euxinia has profound implications for understanding nutrient cycling and primary productivity during the PETM. Euxinic conditions typically develop under scenarios of high organic matter flux to sediments combined with restricted water mass exchange, creating a positive feedback where enhanced productivity drives oxygen consumption that further stimulates nutrient regeneration under anoxic conditions. The biomarker evidence for photic zone euxinia, combined with warming of 5-8°C in both continental air temperatures (rising to approximately 35°C) and sea surface waters, suggests that thermal stratification and enhanced nutrient delivery from intensified hydrological cycling may have contributed to the development of these extreme redox conditions [@C5A4BTTB].

## Ocean Acidification and Carbonate Dissolution

The massive carbon release during the PETM caused rapid and severe ocean acidification, with the Calcite Compensation Depth (CCD) shoaling by more than 2 kilometers within a few thousand years [@FQ4RI4B9]. Evidence from five South Atlantic deep-sea cores demonstrates that a mass of carbon greater than 2,000 × 10⁹ metric tons—and possibly exceeding 4,500 Gt C—dissolved in the ocean at the Paleocene-Eocene boundary [@FQ4RI4B9]. The depth-dependent expression of carbonate dissolution is particularly striking: clay layers with less than 1 weight percent carbonate content increased in thickness from 5 cm at 2.7 km paleodepth to 35 cm at 4.8 km depth, indicating severe acidification-driven dissolution at deeper sites [@FQ4RI4B9]. The subsequent gradual recovery of the CCD over approximately 100,000 years reflects the slow operation of silicate weathering feedbacks that ultimately restored ocean carbonate chemistry to near-baseline conditions.

## Basin Restriction and Hydrographic Changes

The development of anoxic and euxinic conditions in marginal marine settings was strongly influenced by basin restriction and hydrographic changes associated with PETM warming. Continental shelf and epicontinental sea environments were particularly susceptible to deoxygenation due to their semi-restricted nature and sensitivity to sea level fluctuations and freshwater input variations. The North Sea during the PETM exemplifies how basin restriction effects can amplify biogeochemical responses, with paleoceanographic reconstructions indicating that changes in basin circulation and connectivity played a critical role in the onset and maintenance of euxinic conditions [@YFSXQJH4]. These findings suggest that Large Igneous Province activity may have exerted control over ocean anoxia and eutrophication through its influence on regional basin geometry, sea level, and nutrient delivery pathways [@YFSXQJH4].

The relationship between basin restriction and ocean biogeochemistry during the PETM operated through multiple mechanisms. Restricted exchange with open ocean waters limited the renewal of oxygen-rich bottom waters, while enhanced continental runoff associated with intensified hydrological cycling delivered increased nutrient loads that stimulated productivity. Additionally, thermal stratification intensified in warming waters, further inhibiting vertical mixing and oxygen replenishment to deeper waters. The combined effect was a shift toward more eutrophic, stratified conditions conducive to bottom water anoxia, particularly in semi-enclosed basins.

## Marine Ecosystem Disruptions and Biotic Responses

The biogeochemical perturbations of the PETM triggered significant disruptions in marine ecosystems, with evidence for altered community compositions and ecological reorganization. Dinoflagellate cyst assemblages from the New Jersey Shelf reveal substantial variability during the PETM, in stark contrast to the stable assemblages characterizing the Upper Paleocene, indicating that the PETM represented a dynamic climate state rather than a simple shift to a new equilibrium [@FB8PIYHQ]. This ecological dynamism reflects the instability and environmental variability characteristic of the PETM's alternate Earth system state [@G5QN8JFL].

A particularly notable biotic response was the increase in freshwater runoff approximately 10,000 years after the onset of the carbon isotope excursion, evidenced by the abundance of low-salinity-tolerant dinoflagellate taxa (Senegalinium complex) and correlated changes in magnetic susceptibility [@FB8PIYHQ]. This temporal lag between the onset of carbon cycle perturbation and peak hydrological change suggests complex Earth system responses involving atmospheric circulation reorganization, ice sheet dynamics, and monsoon intensification. The dinoflagellate assemblage changes serve as sensitive proxies for reconstructing the dynamic climate state during the PETM, recording shifts in salinity, temperature, nutrient availability, and stratification that characterized this interval.

## Freshwater Ecosystem Collapse and Mass Mortalities

While marine ecosystems experienced significant disruption, freshwater ecosystems suffered even more catastrophic impacts during the PETM, with evidence for wholesale ecosystem collapse and mass mortality events. Lacustrine records from the Jianghan Basin in eastern China document an extreme temperature increase of approximately 11°C reconstructed through carbonate clumped isotope (Δ₄₇) analysis, coincident with a synchronous ~5‰ negative carbon isotope excursion recorded in both total organic carbon and bulk carbonates [@NVB36TDB]. This extreme warming in continental interior settings far exceeded the global average temperature increase and likely approached physiological tolerance limits for many freshwater organisms.

The magnitude of warming experienced in continental freshwater systems had devastating ecological consequences, with evidence for mass mortality events and ecosystem collapse [@NVB36TDB]. Freshwater ecosystems are generally more vulnerable to rapid environmental change than marine systems due to their smaller buffering capacity, more limited dispersal opportunities for organisms, and susceptibility to eutrophication and deoxygenation in warming conditions. The ~11°C temperature increase documented in lacustrine settings would have dramatically altered water column stratification, oxygen solubility, metabolic rates, and the viability of cold-adapted taxa. The synchronous carbon isotope excursion in both organic and inorganic carbon pools indicates that these freshwater systems were tightly coupled to the global carbon cycle perturbation driving the PETM [@NVB36TDB].

## Synthesis and Outstanding Questions

The ocean biogeochemical responses to the PETM reveal a complex, spatially heterogeneous pattern of environmental change characterized by variable timing of anoxia onset relative to the carbon isotope excursion, differing intensities of redox change across basins, and cascading effects through marine and freshwater food webs. The three-phase Earth system response identified by [@G5QN8JFL]—initiation, an alternate semistable state lasting approximately 60,000 years, and recovery over approximately 70,000 years—provides a framework for understanding how biogeochemical cycles respond to and recover from massive carbon injection. However, significant questions remain regarding the spatial patterns of ocean responses, particularly the limited availability of high-resolution records from open ocean settings that could constrain basin-to-basin teleconnections and the role of deepwater circulation changes. Better quantification of productivity changes and their contribution to organic carbon burial would improve understanding of carbon cycle feedbacks during the PETM. Additionally, the mechanisms linking carbon cycle perturbations to marine redox conditions, including the relative roles of productivity enhancement, stratification intensification, and circulation changes, require further investigation to fully reconstruct the causal chains linking carbon release to ocean deoxygenation and ecosystem disruption.

## 8. Silicate Weathering Feedbacks and Climate Recovery

# Silicate Weathering Feedbacks and Climate Recovery

The recovery from the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM) represents a critical test case for understanding how Earth's climate system responds to massive carbon perturbations through negative feedback mechanisms. Enhanced silicate weathering—the chemical breakdown of silicate minerals that ultimately sequesters atmospheric CO₂ as carbonates—is theorized to play a fundamental role in stabilizing climate over geological timescales. Recent advances in isotope geochemistry have provided unprecedented insights into the magnitude, timing, and geographic distribution of weathering responses during the PETM, revealing a complex interplay between climate forcing, hydrological intensification, and geochemical feedbacks that ultimately facilitated climate recovery.

## Lithium Isotope Records of Weathering Intensity

Lithium isotopes have emerged as a powerful tracer for documenting changes in silicate weathering intensity during the PETM, with multiple studies converging on evidence for substantially enhanced weathering rates. Marine carbonate records demonstrate a dramatic negative lithium isotope excursion (LIE) of approximately 3-4‰ during the PETM, with seawater δ⁷Li values shifting from ~26.6‰ to 23-24‰ [@3EA4CMM2]. This global signal, recorded across multiple ocean drilling sites, indicates a fundamental change in the balance between weathering inputs and marine outputs of lithium. The interpretation of this excursion points to increased erosion rates by a factor of 2-3× over approximately 100 ka, with modeled weathering intensity increases of 50-60% compared to pre-warming values [@3EA4CMM2].

Terrestrial records provide complementary evidence with even more dramatic weathering responses. High-resolution lacustrine sediments from the Nanyang Basin in eastern China reveal an approximately 100% increase in regional silicate weathering intensity during the PETM compared to pre-PETM levels [@9P97Q29C]. This enhanced weathering is characterized by strong coupling between carbon isotope variations and weathering intensity changes, demonstrating synchronous responses of the carbon cycle, climate, and chemical weathering on millennial timescales [@9P97Q29C]. The magnitude discrepancy between marine (~50-60% increase) and terrestrial (~100% increase) records reflects both geographic variability in weathering responses and the different aspects of the weathering process captured by global versus regional archives.

## Osmium Isotope Evidence for Continental Weathering

Osmium isotopes provide independent confirmation of intensified continental weathering during the PETM through their sensitivity to radiogenic inputs from ancient continental crust. A transient increase of >10% in ¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os ratios occurred during the event at widely separated marine sites in the North Atlantic and Indian Ocean, indicating a whole-ocean shift in osmium isotopic composition [@HP97NAV7]. The close correlation between the osmium isotope excursion and the carbon isotope excursion suggests these phenomena share a common causative mechanism—likely the hydrological intensification and enhanced continental runoff driven by PETM warming [@HP97NAV7]. The osmium evidence is particularly valuable because it specifically traces weathering of ancient continental rocks, complementing lithium isotope data that reflect the integrated signal of various weathering processes.

## Volcanic Ash as Weathering Substrate and the Role of the NAIP

The North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) played a dual role during the PETM, both as a potential CO₂ source through volcanism and as a critical weathering substrate that may have accelerated climate recovery. Sedimentological evidence from the eastern North Sea reveals that volcanically-derived minerals dominated PETM sediments, with zeolites comprising up to 36% of bulk mineralogy and smectite minerals constituting up to 90% of the clay fraction [@7U76VLJY]. These volcanic minerals are highly reactive weathering substrates, and their abundance suggests the NAIP served as a major sediment source capable of accelerating silicate weathering rates [@7U76VLJY]. The rapid alteration of volcanic material represents a potentially significant but geographically localized weathering mechanism that may have contributed disproportionately to CO₂ drawdown in regions proximal to NAIP deposits.

However, debate continues regarding the relative importance of volcanic ash weathering versus enhanced weathering of normal continental substrates. While volcanic weathering may have been locally significant, the global distribution of weathering signals recorded in marine osmium and lithium isotopes indicates that broader continental weathering responses were essential for climate recovery [@HP97NAV7; @3EA4CMM2]. The geographic variation in weathering magnitude—with some regions showing doubled weathering intensity while global averages suggest 50-60% increases—likely reflects the heterogeneous distribution of both volcanic substrates and climatic changes affecting weathering rates.

## Hydrological Cycle Intensification and Terrestrial Weathering Environments

Enhanced precipitation and runoff during PETM warming fundamentally altered weathering processes across diverse terrestrial environments. Evidence for hydrological intensification comes from multiple proxies, including the rapid influx of terrestrial material shortly after PETM onset, indicating enhanced erosion from deeply weathered Fennoscandian bedrock [@7U76VLJY]. Chemical alteration indices and kaolinite abundance patterns document this erosional response, linking climate warming to immediate hydrological and weathering responses [@7U76VLJY].

Floodplain environments reveal particularly nuanced weathering dynamics during the PETM. Analysis of ancient floodplain deposits shows that silicate weathering intensity increased rapidly at the onset of the PETM and remained elevated during initial climate recovery [@AD2MKDMV]. Critically, the spatial distribution of weathering within floodplain systems was heterogeneous: soils formed farther from river channels experienced larger weathering changes than near-channel soils [@AD2MKDMV]. This spatial pattern suggests that distance from water sources and variations in soil saturation influenced weathering response, with better-drained distal soils potentially experiencing more intense weathering under enhanced precipitation regimes. Such findings underscore the complexity of translating climate changes into weathering responses, with hydrological factors mediating the relationship between temperature and chemical weathering rates.

## Carbon-Weathering Coupling and Recovery Timescales

The temporal relationship between carbon cycle perturbations and weathering responses operates on millennial timescales, as documented by high-resolution terrestrial records. The strong coupling between carbon isotope variations and weathering intensity demonstrates that climate system components responded synchronously rather than sequentially to PETM forcing [@9P97Q29C]. This tight coupling suggests relatively rapid weathering feedbacks operated during the PETM, though significant uncertainties remain regarding lag times between initial warming and maximum weathering response.

The sustained elevation of weathering intensity during early climate recovery [@AD2MKDMV] indicates that weathering feedbacks continued to draw down atmospheric CO₂ even as climate began to stabilize, potentially accelerating the recovery process. However, quantifying the total CO₂ drawdown attributable to enhanced weathering remains challenging. The observed 2-3× increase in global erosion rates and 50-60% increase in weathering intensity [@3EA4CMM2] would have significantly enhanced CO₂ consumption, but translating these proxy signals into absolute CO₂ removal rates requires better constraints on background weathering fluxes and the stoichiometry of weathering reactions under PETM conditions.

## Outstanding Questions and Future Directions

Despite substantial progress, fundamental questions about silicate weathering feedbacks during the PETM persist. The variation in estimated weathering increases—from 50-60% in global marine records [@3EA4CMM2] to ~100% in regional terrestrial records [@9P97Q29C]—requires reconciliation through improved understanding of how different proxies record distinct aspects of the weathering process and how regional responses scale to global budgets. The relative contributions of volcanic ash weathering versus enhanced continental weathering to overall CO₂ drawdown remain incompletely quantified, though both mechanisms clearly operated simultaneously across different regions.

Better constraints on weathering rates across different climate zones during the PETM are essential for understanding how climate recovery proceeds from hyperthermal events. The spatial heterogeneity of weathering responses—both between regions and within individual depositional environments like floodplains [@AD2MKDMV]—suggests that simple extrapolations from local records to global budgets may be inadequate. Future research integrating high-resolution multi-proxy records from diverse latitudes and depositional settings, combined with improved weathering models that account for hydrological, temperature, and substrate effects, will be essential for fully quantifying the silicate weathering feedback and its role in Earth's climate recovery mechanisms.
