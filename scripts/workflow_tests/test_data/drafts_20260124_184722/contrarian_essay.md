# The Comfortable Story About Ancient Climate Catastrophe—and What Actually Happened

Fifty-six million years ago, the Earth experienced one of the most dramatic warming events in its history. Global temperatures spiked 5-8°C in a geological instant. The oceans acidified. Ecosystems collapsed. And somewhere between 3,000 and 10,000 gigatons of carbon flooded into the atmosphere—more than humanity has released in all of industrial history.

This event, the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum, has become the go-to ancient analog for understanding our climate future. The story is appealingly simple: massive carbon release caused rapid warming, the Earth system responded with predictable feedbacks, and eventually—over about 200,000 years—weathering processes scrubbed the excess carbon from the atmosphere and things returned to normal. It's a cautionary tale with a happy ending, at least on geological timescales.

The comfortable assumption embedded in this narrative is that we understand what happened. That we've identified the carbon source (probably methane hydrates, maybe volcanoes), documented the consequences, and can now use this ancient event to calibrate our climate models and project our own future. The PETM appears regularly in IPCC reports and climate policy discussions as evidence that the Earth can recover from carbon perturbations—eventually—and that our models can capture these dynamics.

But here's the crack in the foundation: after three decades of intensive research, we still don't know what actually caused the PETM. Not in any confident, settled sense. The more precisely researchers constrain the timing and magnitude of carbon release, the harder it becomes to explain the observations with any single mechanism. The leading hypotheses don't quite work on their own. And the attempts to combine them reveal that we may be missing something fundamental about how the Earth's carbon cycle operates under stress.

---

The methane hydrate hypothesis has dominated PETM explanations since the 1990s. The logic is elegant: warming oceans destabilized vast reservoirs of frozen methane on the seafloor, releasing isotopically light carbon that explains both the magnitude and the distinctive chemical signature of the event [@dickens2011]. Methane hydrates are real, they're sensitive to temperature, and they contain carbon with the right isotopic composition to produce the observed excursion.

The problem is math. Gerald Dickens, the researcher who originally proposed and has spent decades refining the methane hydrate hypothesis, himself calls it "the most congruous but unproven explanation" [@dickens2011]. To produce a carbon isotope excursion of 2.5-4‰, you need either a relatively modest amount of extremely depleted carbon (around 2,000-3,500 Pg C if the source is pure methane at δ¹³C ≈ -60‰) or a much larger amount of less depleted carbon (6,000-12,000 Pg C if the source is closer to organic matter at δ¹³C ≈ -25‰) [@dickens2011; @panchuk2008].

But the seafloor carbonate dissolution patterns during the PETM—a separate and independent constraint—require at least 6,800 Pg C to reproduce the observed deep-sea chemistry changes [@panchuk2008]. This creates a problem. If the carbon source was pure methane (highly depleted in ¹³C), you don't need much of it to explain the isotope excursion, but then you can't explain the dissolution. If it was something less isotopically extreme, you need more carbon to explain the isotopes, and then dissolution works—but now you need a much larger reservoir than methane hydrates alone can plausibly provide.

There's also a causation problem. Methane hydrates don't spontaneously dissociate; they need to be warmed. Something had to heat the ocean first. And indeed, high-resolution records now show that global sea surface temperatures increased by approximately 2°C roughly 2,000 years *before* the main carbon isotope excursion began [@frieling2019]. This pre-onset warming provides crucial evidence that "CO₂-driven climate change triggered a positive carbon cycle feedback, rather than methane release serving as the initial cause of warming" [@frieling2019].

So methane hydrates can't be the initial trigger. At best, they're a feedback mechanism, amplifying warming that started elsewhere. But what started it?

---

The volcanic hypothesis has gained substantial ground in recent years, centering on the North Atlantic Igneous Province—a massive volcanic system associated with the opening of the Atlantic Ocean. The timing is compelling: high-precision dating places peak volcanism at 56.1 ± 0.4 million years ago, directly coincident with both the PETM and continental breakup [@storey2007]. Mercury accumulation rates in sediments—a proxy for volcanic activity—reached an order of magnitude above background during the event [@jones2023]. Osmium isotopes point to sustained volcanic input throughout the carbon isotope excursion [@jones2023].

Even more intriguing is the mechanism. When magma intrudes into organic-rich sediments, it doesn't just release volcanic CO₂—it bakes the surrounding rock, generating thermogenic methane through contact metamorphism [@svensen2004]. IODP Expedition 396 actually drilled one of these structures and found direct evidence that shallow-water hydrothermal vents erupted during the PETM itself, delivering methane directly to the atmosphere in a shallow-water setting where it couldn't oxidize to CO₂ in the water column [@berndt2023].

This is genuinely exciting evidence. It provides a mechanism for generating isotopically light carbon (thermogenic methane) at rates fast enough to match the observed onset, while also explaining the volcanic mercury signals that don't fit pure methane hydrate scenarios.

But the volcanic hypothesis has its own problems. Estimates of CO₂ content in large igneous province magmas vary enormously—from 1,080 to 7,471 ppm depending on the specific province and time in the eruption sequence [@black2023]. Extrapolating from limited geochemical measurements to total carbon budgets requires knowing the volume of magma, the organic content of intruded sediments, and the efficiency of carbon transfer to the atmosphere—all of which remain poorly constrained [@kender2021].

More fundamentally, a comprehensive modeling study found that reproducing the observed patterns "cannot be associated with any known PETM hypothesis" during the first stage of carbon release [@carozza2011]. The model required either rapid organic carbon release or thermogenic methane sources that don't match what we think we know about the volcanic system. The authors concluded that "current PETM hypotheses may require fundamental revision" [@carozza2011].

---

The emerging consensus, such as it is, holds that the PETM wasn't caused by any single mechanism but by multiple carbon sources operating in sequence. Volcanic warming crossed some threshold that destabilized methane hydrates. Or volcanic intrusions generated thermogenic methane while simultaneously releasing CO₂. Or initial warming mobilized organic carbon from permafrost or continental soils, which then triggered hydrate dissociation, which then...

This multi-source framework is intellectually satisfying in that it allows different lines of evidence to be reconciled. Mercury signals that don't correlate with the main carbon isotope excursion? That's volcanic activity triggering secondary carbon reservoirs [@kender2021]. Two distinct carbon pulses separated by 1-2 thousand years? That's Stage 1 (volcanic/thermogenic) followed by Stage 2 (hydrate dissociation or organic carbon oxidation) [@bowen2014; @kender2021]. Pre-onset warming before the main excursion? That's the trigger warming before the feedback kicked in [@frieling2019; @babila2022].

But here's the uncomfortable reality: the more mechanisms you invoke, the more parameters you need to constrain, and the harder it becomes to definitively test any of them. The multi-source framework risks becoming unfalsifiable—a way of explaining away discrepancies rather than resolving them.

Consider the two-stage carbon release model of Carozza et al. [@carozza2011]. Stage 1, lasting approximately 3,000 years, involved atmospheric methane release of 900-1,100 Pg C with moderately depleted isotopic values (-22 to -30‰). Stage 2, lasting 1,000 years, involved mixed emissions totaling 900-1,400 Pg C with more depleted values (-50 to -60‰). The model reproduces the observations reasonably well. But the authors explicitly note that Stage 1 results "cannot be associated with any known PETM hypothesis." The model works, but we don't know what physical process corresponds to it.

This is the state of PETM science: we can build numerical frameworks that reproduce the observations, but the frameworks require carbon sources and release rates that don't clearly map onto known geological processes. Either our models are wrong, or our understanding of the relevant geological processes is incomplete, or both.

---

The chronological uncertainties compound the problem. Establishing when carbon was released relative to when temperatures changed is absolutely critical for distinguishing triggers from feedbacks. Yet the estimates for PETM duration have fluctuated wildly.

Recent astrochronological work using Monte Carlo-based probabilistic methods estimates that the carbon isotope excursion lasted 268.8 +21.2/-20.5 thousand years [@piedrahita2025]. This is substantially longer than the widely cited range of approximately 120-230 thousand years based on earlier cyclostratigraphy [@westerhold2017; @lourens2005]. The difference matters enormously for calculating carbon release rates and climate sensitivity.

The onset duration is even more contested. Early claims of a 13-year onset were definitively refuted by modeling showing that such rapid release is physically impossible given ocean chemistry constraints [@zeebe2014]. More recent estimates suggest the onset lasted approximately 6,000 years [@li2022]. But the confidence intervals on these estimates remain large, and different analytical approaches yield different answers.

Part of the problem is that many deep-sea records are corrupted by carbonate dissolution. During the PETM, ocean acidification caused preferential dissolution of carbonate deposited during the peak excursion, effectively erasing portions of the isotopic record at deeper sites [@mccarren2008]. The benthic foraminiferal carbon isotope excursion measures -3.5‰ at 1,500 meters paleodepth but only -1.5‰ at 3,600 meters [@mccarren2008]. You can't simply average these values and call it the "true" excursion.

Researchers have increasingly turned to expanded shallow-marine sections on continental margins, which avoided the worst dissolution effects and have sedimentation rates 1-2 orders of magnitude higher than deep-sea deposits [@robinson2024]. The Marlboro Clay on the US Atlantic Coastal Plain, for example, contains exceptionally thick PETM records exceeding 15 meters. But these coastal sections introduce their own complications—variable sedimentation rates, terrestrial dilution effects, and difficulties in correlation between sites [@slotnick2012].

Even the magnitude of the carbon isotope excursion—the single most diagnostic feature of the event—remains disputed. Measurements from different substrates yield different values. Marine carbonates show one signal; terrestrial biomarkers show another. And it turns out that different plant groups fractionate carbon differently: conifers exhibited a 3‰ negative excursion while angiosperms showed a 6‰ excursion, meaning the previously reported 4.5‰ excursion in terrestrial leaf waxes represents an averaged signal of mixed plant inputs rather than a direct atmospheric measurement [@schouten2007].

Trying to reconstruct the "true" atmospheric carbon isotope excursion from proxy records that each carry their own biases and fractionation effects is like trying to determine the original color of a painting when every photograph was taken through a different filter.

---

What we do know with more confidence concerns the recovery—how the Earth system eventually returned to equilibrium. The silicate weathering feedback operated as theory predicts, scrubbing excess CO₂ from the atmosphere through enhanced chemical weathering of continental rocks.

Lithium isotope records show a dramatic negative excursion during the PETM, indicating increased erosion rates by a factor of 2-3× and weathering intensity increases of 50-60% compared to pre-warming values [@pogge2021]. Osmium isotope records independently confirm intensified continental weathering, with a >10% increase in ¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os ratios at widely separated marine sites [@ravizza2000]. Terrestrial records from China show an approximately 100% increase in regional silicate weathering intensity during the PETM [@chen2023a].

The North Atlantic Igneous Province itself may have contributed to recovery through an ironic feedback: the volcanic ash it deposited across the region provided highly reactive weathering substrates. Sediments from the eastern North Sea show that volcanically-derived minerals dominated PETM deposits, with zeolites comprising up to 36% of bulk mineralogy [@stokke2021]. These volcanic minerals weather rapidly, potentially accelerating CO₂ drawdown in regions proximal to the volcanic province.

This part of the story is actually reassuring for understanding Earth system dynamics. Enhanced silicate weathering really did operate as a negative feedback, eventually restoring climate equilibrium. The problem is the timescale: approximately 200,000 years. That's geologically fast but practically meaningless for human concerns. And we still can't accurately predict *how* fast weathering responds to a given warming magnitude, or how that response varies across different climate zones and rock types.

---

The ocean responses reveal something more troubling about threshold behaviors in the Earth system. During the PETM, widespread ocean deoxygenation developed—not just low-oxygen conditions but fully euxinic (sulfidic) states in multiple basins [@sluijs2014; @mariani2024]. Biomarker evidence documents photic zone euxinia—sulfidic conditions extending into sunlit surface waters—in Gulf Coastal Plain sections [@sluijs2014]. This represents a fundamental reorganization of upper ocean chemistry where toxic hydrogen sulfide penetrated shallow enough to kill most marine life.

These extreme redox conditions weren't uniform. Paleoceanographic reconstructions reveal that deoxygenation, productivity changes, and basin restriction occurred at different times relative to the carbon isotope excursion across different settings [@mariani2024]. Some regions developed severe euxinia; others maintained more oxygenated states. The PETM ocean response can't be characterized as uniformly anoxic but rather as a spatially heterogeneous mosaic of redox conditions.

Freshwater ecosystems may have suffered even worse. Lacustrine records from China document an extreme temperature increase of approximately 11°C—far exceeding the global average of 5-8°C—coincident with evidence for mass mortality events linked to toxigenic cyanobacterial blooms [@chen2023b]. Continental interior freshwater systems, with their smaller buffering capacity and more limited dispersal opportunities for organisms, proved more vulnerable than marine environments.

These findings matter for understanding tipping points. The transition from oxic to euxinic conditions appears to involve threshold-crossing behavior rather than gradual deterioration [@mariani2024]. Once certain combinations of warming, stratification, and nutrient loading are exceeded, the system flips to an alternate state that may persist even as the initial forcing moderates. Similar nonlinear responses may characterize other Earth system components during rapid warming.

---

Where does this leave us? The PETM remains our best natural experiment for understanding how the Earth system responds to rapid carbon injection. But the experiment is less legible than we'd like.

The fundamental uncertainty about carbon sources isn't just a gap in historical knowledge—it reflects genuine limits in our ability to constrain ancient carbon cycle dynamics. The methods we use to identify carbon sources (isotopes, mercury, osmium) each tell us something, but they don't converge on a single coherent picture. Different approaches yield different answers, and reconciling them requires assumptions that themselves introduce uncertainty.

More troubling is the recognition of equifinality: multiple combinations of processes could produce observationally similar outcomes given current proxy uncertainties. A volcanic trigger plus methane hydrate feedback might look the same in the rock record as thermogenic methane generation plus organic carbon oxidation plus limited hydrate dissociation. We can build models that reproduce the observations, but multiple model configurations work approximately equally well.

This doesn't mean the PETM is useless as a climate analog. It demonstrates that massive carbon release produces warming, ocean acidification, and deoxygenation—hardly surprising but important to confirm. It shows that silicate weathering feedback eventually restores equilibrium, on timescales of hundreds of thousands of years. It documents threshold behaviors in ocean chemistry that should inform our thinking about potential futures.

But it cannot tell us, with the precision we might like, exactly how much warming we'll get from a given carbon injection, exactly which feedbacks will engage and when, or exactly how long recovery will take. The uncertainties are large, and they're unlikely to narrow dramatically given the inherent limitations of reconstructing ancient Earth system dynamics from incomplete rock records.

The real lesson of PETM research may be epistemological. We're trying to use a 56-million-year-old event to calibrate predictions about the next century, and the event itself remains partially illegible despite three decades of intensive study. The Earth system is complex enough that even well-preserved ancient events don't yield simple answers.

This isn't an argument for ignoring the PETM or abandoning efforts to understand it. The research has generated genuine insights about weathering feedbacks, ocean biogeochemistry, and the spatial heterogeneity of climate responses. It's an argument for intellectual humility about what ancient events can and cannot tell us, and for recognizing that some uncertainties may be irreducible given the nature of the evidence.

The comfortable story—that we understand what caused the PETM and can use it to project our climate future—papers over genuine complexity that the field's own evidence reveals. The more interesting story is that 56 million years of geological processing still can't fully obscure how complicated Earth system dynamics become under stress. That's a different kind of cautionary tale, and perhaps a more honest one.
