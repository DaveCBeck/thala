# Literature Review: the causes of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), sometimes also spelled Palaeocene

# When Earth Ran a Fever

Fifty-six million years ago, something went terribly wrong with our planet's climate. In geological terms, it happened almost overnight—a few thousand years at most. Global temperatures shot up by 5 to 8 degrees Celsius. The oceans turned acidic. Vast stretches of seafloor dissolved. And somewhere, somehow, an enormous pulse of carbon flooded into the atmosphere.

We call this event the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum, or PETM, and three decades after scientists first identified it, we're still arguing about what caused it. That might sound like a failure of science. I think it's actually a testament to how fiendishly complicated Earth's climate system turns out to be—and why understanding this ancient catastrophe matters so much for our own carbon-soaked future.

## The fingerprint in the rock

The PETM left behind a distinctive calling card: a sharp negative spike in carbon isotopes recorded in sediments worldwide [@PKCA3IP9]. This carbon isotope excursion, as geochemists call it, tells us that enormous quantities of isotopically light carbon—the kind that comes from organic sources rather than volcanic gases—entered the ocean-atmosphere system in a geological instant. The magnitude of this spike varies depending on where and what you measure, ranging from about 2.5 to 6 per mil [@3HVXCE9H], but its global presence is unmistakable.

How much carbon are we talking about? Estimates range from roughly 3,000 to more than 10,000 petagrams [@Y4DAR7Z9]. For context, humans have released about 700 petagrams since the Industrial Revolution began. The PETM involved something like five to fifteen times that amount, released over thousands of years rather than centuries.

The environmental consequences were severe. Sea surface temperatures in the tropics may have exceeded 35°C [@C5A4BTTB]. The deep ocean warmed by more than 4°C [@PXR3JYSE]. Ocean pH dropped as acidification spread [@AG7I2UA9]. In some marine basins, oxygen disappeared entirely, replaced by toxic hydrogen sulfide [@YFSXQJH4]. The event persisted for roughly 200,000 years before Earth's climate gradually recovered [@7B8GX4DL].

What could possibly have released that much carbon, that fast?

## Two suspects, one crime

For most of the past thirty years, the scientific debate has centered on two main suspects: methane hydrates and volcanism.

Methane hydrates are ice-like compounds that form when methane gas, produced by microbes in ocean sediments, becomes trapped in water molecules under high pressure and low temperature. Vast quantities of these hydrates exist today on continental margins worldwide. If the ocean warms enough, the hydrates destabilize and release their methane—a potent greenhouse gas that eventually oxidizes to carbon dioxide.

The hydrate hypothesis has intuitive appeal [@DR9EQIKF]. Methane from hydrates is extremely depleted in heavy carbon isotopes, with values around -60 per mil, making it easy to explain the observed isotopic excursion with a relatively modest carbon mass. And the mechanism comes with a built-in amplifier: initial warming destabilizes some hydrates, the released methane causes more warming, which destabilizes more hydrates, and so on. It's a textbook positive feedback.

The volcanic hypothesis points instead to the North Atlantic Igneous Province, or NAIP—a massive pulse of volcanic activity associated with the opening of the North Atlantic Ocean [@INWM2JEG]. When continents rift apart, enormous volumes of magma rise from the mantle and erupt onto the surface. These eruptions release carbon dioxide directly, but they can also cook organic-rich sediments that the magma encounters on its way up, generating additional greenhouse gases through what geologists call contact metamorphism [@L358KUND].

Recent evidence has strengthened the volcanic case considerably. High-precision dating shows that peak NAIP activity occurred precisely during the PETM [@INWM2JEG]. Mercury concentrations in PETM sediments—mercury being a distinctive volcanic tracer—spike dramatically at the event's onset [@47YBCEFM]. And in 2021, a scientific drilling expedition made a discovery that I'll explore in a separate piece: direct evidence that volcanic vents were actively releasing methane into shallow seas exactly when the PETM began [@SFHXHQXJ]. The details of this discovery, and what the drilling ship found, deserve their own exploration.

## Beyond either-or

Here's what I find most interesting about the current state of PETM science: researchers have largely moved past the simple "hydrates versus volcanism" framing. The evidence increasingly suggests that both mechanisms—and possibly others—operated together in a complex cascade.

Consider the timing. High-resolution records from shallow marine sediments reveal that temperatures began rising about 2,000 years before the main carbon isotope excursion began [@TBYRHSCY]. This pre-onset warming wasn't subtle—sea surface temperatures increased by roughly 2°C before the isotopic spike that marks massive carbon release. This temporal sequence is crucial. It suggests that some initial warming process—perhaps volcanic carbon dioxide—triggered a secondary response from a different carbon reservoir [@TBYRHSCY].

There's a deeper story here about what those 2,000 years of warning might tell us about climate thresholds and tipping points. I explore this fully in a separate piece on the pre-onset excursion and what it reveals about Earth system feedbacks.

The isotopic evidence also points toward multiple sources. Detailed modeling of the carbon isotope excursion suggests the event unfolded in at least two distinct phases, with different isotopic signatures that cannot be explained by any single carbon source [@9DLALEHA]. The first phase might represent volcanic or thermogenic carbon, while the second phase—showing more depleted isotopes—could reflect hydrate dissociation triggered by the initial warming. One recent analysis concluded bluntly that the first stage results "cannot be associated with any known PETM hypothesis," suggesting our current frameworks may still be missing something important [@9DLALEHA].

## The slow burn of heat conduction

One physical constraint has profoundly shaped how we think about hydrate involvement: heat moves slowly through sediments.

If warming bottom waters triggered hydrate dissociation, the thermal signal had to propagate downward through hundreds of meters of sediment to reach the stability zone where hydrates existed. Heat conduction calculations show this process takes 10,000 to 26,000 years [@US3YQCWH]. The warming pulse doesn't simply arrive at depth and immediately destabilize everything. Instead, it creates a prolonged tail of continuing methane release that extends far beyond the initial warming.

This physics actually helps explain one of the PETM's most puzzling features: its long duration. A simple one-time carbon pulse should produce warming that begins recovering immediately as the carbon slowly leaves the atmosphere. Instead, the PETM persisted for roughly 200,000 years [@7B8GX4DL]. The slow penetration of heat into marine sediments, continuing to destabilize hydrates for tens of thousands of years after initial warming, could sustain the elevated temperatures that proxy records document [@US3YQCWH].

## What the oceans endured

The carbon injection didn't just warm the climate—it fundamentally reorganized ocean chemistry and ecosystems. In some ways, this biogeochemical response is more alarming than the temperature increase itself.

Multiple lines of evidence indicate widespread ocean deoxygenation during the PETM [@C5A4BTTB]. In marginal marine basins, conditions deteriorated past mere oxygen depletion to full euxinia—a state where hydrogen sulfide, toxic to most animal life, accumulates in the water column [@YFSXQJH4]. Biomarkers from photosynthetic green sulfur bacteria, which require both light and hydrogen sulfide, indicate that these toxic waters occasionally reached into the sunlit surface layer [@C5A4BTTB].

The ecological consequences were severe. Dinoflagellate assemblages, which had remained stable throughout the late Paleocene, became wildly variable during the PETM, indicating that marine ecosystems had entered a fundamentally different and unstable state [@FB8PIYHQ]. Freshwater systems fared even worse—lakes in interior China experienced temperature increases of 11°C, accompanied by toxic cyanobacterial blooms and ecosystem collapse [@NVB36TDB].

The ocean's response to PETM carbon injection represents perhaps the most troubling parallel to our current situation. I examine these biogeochemical cascades in detail in a separate piece on what happened when ancient oceans turned toxic.

## How Earth healed itself

The PETM didn't last forever. After roughly 60,000 years of extreme warmth, the climate began a gradual recovery that extended over another 100,000 years or more [@G5QN8JFL]. Understanding how Earth pulled itself out of this hothouse state reveals fundamental features of our planet's climate thermostat.

The primary recovery mechanism was enhanced silicate weathering—the chemical breakdown of rocks that ultimately converts atmospheric carbon dioxide into carbonate minerals buried in ocean sediments. Multiple independent geochemical tracers document a dramatic acceleration of weathering during the PETM. Lithium isotope records indicate that erosion rates increased 2 to 3 fold, while chemical weathering intensity rose by 50 to 60 percent compared to pre-event values [@3EA4CMM2]. Osmium isotopes tell a similar story, showing a surge in radiogenic osmium from continental weathering precisely during the event [@HP97NAV7].

The weathering response involved complex feedbacks. Warmer temperatures and intensified rainfall accelerated rock breakdown directly. But the presence of fresh volcanic ash from NAIP eruptions may have provided especially reactive material for chemical weathering, potentially accelerating carbon dioxide drawdown in regions near the volcanic source [@7U76VLJY]. The climate system, in a sense, used the waste products of its own disruption to help clean up the mess.

What strikes me most about the weathering evidence is the timescale involved. These negative feedbacks operated, but slowly—over tens to hundreds of thousands of years. The carbon injection itself happened perhaps ten times faster than the recovery. This asymmetry has uncomfortable implications for our own situation.

## What we still don't know

Despite three decades of intensive research, fundamental questions remain unresolved. We cannot confidently say how much carbon was released, with estimates spanning a factor of three or more. We cannot definitively identify the carbon source or sources, though volcanic mechanisms now appear more likely as at least an initial trigger. We cannot precisely date the onset—recent estimates range from less than 5,000 years to over 6,000 years for the main excursion [@PZ3PSHWV; @IRAWFJDL].

These uncertainties matter because they propagate into everything else we try to calculate. The climate sensitivity of the Paleocene Earth—how much warming resulted from each doubling of carbon dioxide—depends on how much carbon was actually released. The relevance of the PETM as an analog for modern emissions depends on whether we can trust our estimates of release rates.

Some of these uncertainties may prove irreducible. Different proxy records show systematically different signals, reflecting not errors but genuine spatial and temporal heterogeneity in how the event unfolded [@3HVXCE9H]. The deep ocean dissolved much of the carbonate that would otherwise preserve the isotopic record of peak conditions [@PKCA3IP9]. We are trying to reconstruct a crime scene that the planet itself has partially erased.

## Why this matters now

The PETM represents the closest natural analog to what humans are doing to the climate system. In both cases, large amounts of carbon enter the atmosphere rapidly by geological standards. In both cases, the oceans acidify, warm, and lose oxygen. In both cases, ecosystems face conditions unlike anything in their recent evolutionary history.

But the parallels have limits. Modern carbon emissions are occurring perhaps ten times faster than even the most rapid estimates for PETM carbon release [@G5QN8JFL]. The PETM Earth started from a warmer baseline, without polar ice sheets, meaning some amplifying feedbacks that existed then don't apply now—while others, like ice sheet collapse, add new vulnerabilities.

What the PETM teaches us most clearly is that Earth's climate system contains thresholds and feedbacks that can amplify initial perturbations. A modest volcanic warming may have triggered hydrate release that doubled or tripled the total carbon injection. Ocean circulation changes may have amplified regional oxygen loss into basin-scale dead zones. The system is not linear, and pushing it hard can activate responses that weren't obvious from studying small perturbations.

The recovery story offers both hope and caution. Earth did eventually heal. Silicate weathering drew down the excess carbon dioxide, temperatures fell, and ecosystems rebuilt. But the timescale was measured in tens of thousands of years—far longer than human civilizations have existed. The PETM was not a catastrophe that Earth couldn't survive. It was a catastrophe that took a hundred thousand years to clean up.

We are running an experiment on our planet's climate system at rates unprecedented in at least 56 million years. The ancient rocks that record the PETM are telling us something about how that experiment might unfold. The message isn't simple, and scientists are still arguing about the details. But the broad outlines—rapid warming, ocean acidification, ecosystem disruption, and a very slow recovery—are clear enough to warrant attention.

The Earth that emerged from the PETM was habitable. Many species survived and eventually thrived in the new conditions. But the world that went into that event and the world that came out were not the same place. When we study the PETM, we're studying what it means for a planet to run a fever—and what it costs to recover.
