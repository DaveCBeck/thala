# The Volcano, the Methane, and the Missing Carbon

In 2021, a drill ship positioned above the Norwegian continental shelf punched through sediments that had been accumulating for tens of millions of years. The scientists aboard were hunting for something specific: the fossilized remains of hydrothermal vents that had erupted into shallow seas 56 million years ago, right at the moment when Earth experienced the most dramatic warming event of the past 66 million years. What they found was remarkable—not just evidence that these vents existed, but that they had been shallow enough to belch methane directly into the atmosphere, bypassing the water column where it might have oxidized to the less potent greenhouse gas carbon dioxide (Berndt et al., 2023).

This discovery from IODP Expedition 396 crystallized something that had been building for years in the scientific literature: our understanding of the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM) was undergoing a fundamental revision. The story wasn't simply about methane bubbling up from destabilized seafloor hydrates, the explanation that had dominated textbooks for two decades. Nor was it purely about volcanic carbon dioxide. Instead, it appeared to be about a cascade—one process triggering another, feedbacks amplifying initial perturbations, the whole Earth system lurching through a series of thresholds into an altered state that persisted for nearly 200,000 years.

The PETM matters because it's the closest geological analog we have to what's happening now. Global temperatures rose 5-8°C within a few thousand years (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017). The oceans acidified. Oxygen-starved dead zones spread across continental shelves. Species migrated, adapted, or went extinct. And then, over the following hundred thousand years, the system slowly recovered. Understanding what triggered this event—and what sustained it—isn't merely academic. It's a window into how Earth's climate operates under stress.

## The Carbon Isotope Puzzle

The PETM announces itself in the geological record through a distinctive chemical fingerprint: a sharp negative excursion in carbon isotope ratios, recorded in everything from deep-sea foraminifera to terrestrial soil carbonates to the waxy coatings of ancient leaves. This carbon isotope excursion (CIE) tells us that an enormous pulse of isotopically light carbon—depleted in the heavier carbon-13 isotope—flooded into the atmosphere and oceans over a geologically brief interval.

The magnitude of this excursion has been the subject of intense scrutiny, because it directly constrains how much carbon was released and from what source. Different carbon reservoirs have different isotopic signatures. Volcanic carbon dioxide has δ¹³C values around -5 to -8‰. Organic matter from soils and sediments runs about -25‰. Methane from decomposing organic material in marine sediments can be as light as -60‰. The lighter the source, the less carbon you need to explain a given isotopic shift—but each source comes with its own constraints and challenges.

Here's where things get tricky. The recorded magnitude of the CIE varies depending on what you measure and where. Deep-sea carbonates at 3,600 meters paleodepth show excursions of only about -1.5‰, while shallower sites at 1,500 meters record -3.5‰ (McCarren et al., 2008). This isn't because the ocean experienced different amounts of carbon isotope change at different depths—it's because the deeper sites experienced more intense carbonate dissolution during the acidification event, preferentially erasing the sediments deposited during the peak of the excursion. The record is literally being eaten away at the points where we most need it.

Terrestrial records have their own complications. Plant compounds from different species fractionate carbon differently, and these fractionation factors themselves change with environmental conditions. Conifers recorded a 3‰ negative CIE during the PETM, while angiosperms showed a 6‰ excursion (Schouten et al., 2007). The canonical 4.5‰ terrestrial excursion is really just an average of mixed plant inputs, not a direct measure of atmospheric change. Even total organic carbon measurements can be misleading—shifts in the relative abundance of terrestrial versus marine organic matter can exaggerate the apparent excursion by over 1‰ (Sluijs & Dickens, 2012).

Working through these complications, the best estimates suggest the actual atmospheric CIE was somewhere between 3.5‰ and 5‰ (McCarren et al., 2008). This implies a total carbon release of somewhere between 3,000 and 10,000 gigatons, depending on assumptions about the isotopic composition of the source (Dickens, 2011). For comparison, humans have released roughly 700 gigatons of carbon since the Industrial Revolution, with another several thousand gigatons of fossil fuel reserves remaining. The PETM was bigger—much bigger—than what we've done so far, though perhaps not bigger than what we're capable of doing.

## The Volcanic Trigger

The North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) represents one of Earth's most impressive volcanic episodes. As the supercontinent Laurasia began to rift apart, creating the North Atlantic Ocean, massive volumes of magma poured across what is now Greenland, the British Isles, and the Faroe Islands. Flood basalts blanketed landscapes. Sills—horizontal sheets of magma—intruded into sedimentary basins. The Earth's crust cracked and bled.

The temporal coincidence between peak NAIP volcanism and the PETM has always been suggestive, but coincidence isn't causation. Recent high-precision dating has sharpened the correlation considerably. Argon-argon dating of East Greenland flood basalts places the main eruptive pulse at 56.1 ± 0.4 million years ago, squarely coincident with the PETM onset (Michael Storey et al., 2007). More importantly, integrated geochemical studies using multiple volcanic tracers—mercury accumulation rates, osmium isotope ratios—demonstrate that volcanism wasn't just present at the onset but persisted throughout the roughly 200,000-year body of the event (Jones et al., 2023).

Mercury serves as a particularly useful volcanic fingerprint because it's preferentially released during eruptions and preserved in marine sediments. During the PETM, mercury accumulation rates reached 1.76 ng cm⁻² yr⁻¹—an order of magnitude higher than background levels (Jones et al., 2023). Osmium isotopes shifted toward unradiogenic (mantle-like) values and stayed there through the event, consistent with sustained volcanic input (Jones et al., 2023; Jones et al., 2019). The volcanic signal is unmistakable.

But here's the problem: direct volcanic carbon dioxide emissions almost certainly weren't enough. Estimates of magmatic carbon dioxide concentrations in large igneous province basalts vary considerably—from about 1,080 to 7,471 ppm depending on the province (Black & Aiuppa, 2023). Even generous estimates of eruption volumes struggle to get above a few thousand gigatons of carbon from magmatic degassing alone. And magmatic carbon dioxide has the wrong isotopic signature—it's too heavy to explain the observed CIE without additional contributions from lighter sources.

The volcanic trigger hypothesis therefore increasingly invokes indirect mechanisms: carbon released not from the magma itself, but from the sediments it intruded. When hot basaltic sills penetrate organic-rich shales and coals, they cook the surrounding rock, liberating methane and carbon dioxide through a process called contact metamorphism. The carbon in these sediments is isotopically light, and the volumes can be substantial.

The drilling results from Expedition 396 confirmed that this mechanism operated during the PETM. The researchers found that hydrothermal vent complexes formed at sill margins, with approximately 95% of these vents terminating at the Paleocene-Eocene boundary (Jones et al., 2019). Critically, many of these vents erupted in shallow water settings—shallow enough that methane could escape directly to the atmosphere without being oxidized in the water column (Berndt et al., 2023). Stable isotope stratigraphy and biostratigraphic markers tie the infilling of these structures directly to PETM onset, with vent formation occurring only a few millennia before the carbon isotope excursion began.

## The Methane Question

For two decades after the PETM was first characterized, methane hydrate dissociation dominated explanations for the event. The hypothesis was elegant: bottom water warming destabilized vast deposits of methane ice frozen in continental margin sediments, releasing isotopically light carbon that explained both the magnitude and rapidity of the CIE. Early modeling showed that dissociation of roughly 1.12 × 10¹⁸ grams of methane hydrate could produce the observed -2.5‰ excursion while also explaining carbonate dissolution patterns on the seafloor (Gerald R. Dickens et al., 1997).

The hypothesis had attractive features. Methane hydrates represent a genuinely enormous carbon reservoir—estimates for the modern ocean run to thousands of gigatons. Their stability depends on temperature and pressure, making them vulnerable to warming. And their isotopic signature is extremely light (δ¹³C around -60‰), meaning relatively modest releases could generate large CIE signals.

But problems accumulated. First, we don't actually know how much methane hydrate existed in Paleocene oceans. Modern estimates vary enormously, and the warmer Paleocene ocean may have had smaller reservoirs in different locations than today (Dickens, 2011). Second, the thermal dynamics don't quite work for a rapid, singular event. Heat conducts slowly through marine sediments—temperature anomalies at the sediment-water interface require 10,000-26,000 years to penetrate to the depths where hydrates would have been stable (Zeebe, 2013; Archer, 2007). This means hydrate dissociation would have produced a prolonged tail of methane release, not a sharp spike.

Third, and perhaps most damaging, there's strong evidence that warming preceded the main carbon isotope excursion. High-resolution records show that sea surface temperatures increased by approximately 2°C roughly 2,000 years before the CIE onset (Frieling et al., 2019). This temporal sequence inverts the simple methane hypothesis: if warming came first, hydrate dissociation was a consequence of climate change, not its primary cause. The warming must have had another origin.

This doesn't mean methane hydrates weren't involved. The pre-onset warming provides exactly the kind of trigger that could destabilize hydrate deposits, creating a positive feedback that amplified initial volcanic forcing (Frieling et al., 2019; Dickens, 2011). Independent geochemical evidence supports enhanced methane cycling during the PETM: barium burial rates increased threefold across shelf, slope, and deep ocean settings, consistent with widespread anaerobic methane oxidation (Frieling et al., 2019). The hydrates likely participated, but as an amplifier rather than an initiator.

Recent modeling suggests the actual carbon release occurred in at least two distinct stages. The first stage, lasting roughly 3,000 years, involved release of 900-1,100 Pg C with isotopic signatures of -22 to -30‰—consistent with volcanic or thermogenic sources. The second stage, lasting about 1,000 years, released an additional 900-1,400 Pg C with more depleted signatures of -50 to -60‰, consistent with methane from hydrates or biogenic sources (Carozza et al., 2011). Intriguingly, the modeling showed that Stage 1 "cannot be associated with any known PETM hypothesis" using conventional assumptions, suggesting we may still be missing something fundamental about the initial trigger.

## A Cascade of Thresholds

The emerging picture of the PETM is one of cascading processes, each crossing thresholds that activated new carbon sources or amplified existing feedbacks. The volcanic trigger hypothesis provides a plausible initiator: NAIP volcanism released carbon dioxide both directly from magmas and indirectly through contact metamorphism of organic sediments. This initial warming then destabilized additional carbon reservoirs—methane hydrates, permafrost, perhaps organic carbon in warming soils—creating positive feedbacks that amplified the perturbation beyond what volcanism alone could achieve.

This cascade model helps explain one of the persistent puzzles of PETM research: the apparent mismatch between volcanic signals and carbon isotope stratigraphy. Mercury enrichments are most prevalent before CIE onset and during the recovery phase, with the largest isotopic shifts occurring when volcanic signals are weakest (Jones et al., 2019; Kender et al., 2021). This pattern makes sense if volcanic carbon dioxide triggered secondary releases from isotopically lighter reservoirs: the volcanoes fired first, then their effects rippled through the Earth system.

The cascade also explains the duration of the event. Heat diffusing through marine sediments would have continued destabilizing hydrate deposits for tens of thousands of years after initial warming ceased (Zeebe, 2013). Enhanced weathering of newly erupted volcanic materials, while ultimately drawing down carbon dioxide, may have initially released nutrients that stimulated marine productivity and organic carbon burial. The system didn't simply switch into a new state and hold there—it continued evolving, with different feedbacks dominating at different times.

Evidence for this dynamic behavior comes from ecological records. Dinoflagellate assemblages from the New Jersey shelf show substantial variability throughout the PETM, in stark contrast to the stable communities characterizing the preceding Late Paleocene (Sluijs & Brinkhuis, 2009). The PETM wasn't a static warm interval but a "dynamic climate state" with oscillating conditions. A particularly striking feature is the increase in freshwater indicators approximately 10,000 years after CIE onset—evidence for hydrological intensification that lagged the initial carbon perturbation by a geologically significant interval.

The ocean biogeochemical responses were equally dynamic. Marginal marine settings experienced widespread deoxygenation, with some regions developing fully euxinic (sulfidic) conditions where both oxygen was depleted and hydrogen sulfide accumulated in the water column (Sluijs et al., 2014; Mariani et al., 2024). Biomarkers from photosynthetic green sulfur bacteria—organisms that require both light and sulfide—demonstrate that these anoxic conditions occasionally penetrated into the sunlit surface waters, a state called photic zone euxinia that's extraordinarily rare in Earth history. Continental freshwater systems may have suffered even more severely, with lacustrine records from China documenting warming of approximately 11°C and evidence for toxic cyanobacterial blooms and mass mortality events (Chen et al., 2023).

## The Recovery

What ultimately ended the PETM? The answer lies in Earth's long-term thermostat: silicate weathering. When carbon dioxide levels rise, temperatures increase, which intensifies the hydrological cycle. More rain falls on continents, accelerating the chemical breakdown of silicate minerals. The dissolved products wash into oceans, where they combine with dissolved carbon dioxide to form carbonate minerals that settle to the seafloor. Over geological timescales, this negative feedback draws down atmospheric carbon dioxide and cools the planet.

Multiple lines of evidence confirm that weathering intensified dramatically during and after the PETM. Lithium isotope records show a 3-4‰ negative excursion in seawater values, indicating that erosion rates increased 2-3 fold while weathering intensity rose by 50-60% (Pogge von Strandmann et al., 2021). Osmium isotopes independently document a greater than 10% increase in radiogenic (continental-derived) osmium, consistent with enhanced weathering fluxes reaching the ocean (Ravizza et al., 2000). Terrestrial records from China show even larger effects, with regional silicate weathering intensity approximately doubling during the PETM (Chen et al., 2023).

The North Atlantic volcanics may have played an ironic dual role: both triggering the warming and helping end it. Volcanic ash is highly reactive and weathers rapidly, particularly basaltic material. Sediments from the North Sea contain up to 36% zeolites and 90% smectite clays derived from volcanic material (Stokke et al., 2021). This newly deposited reactive substrate would have enhanced weathering rates in regions proximal to NAIP eruptions. The volcanoes that destabilized the climate may also have provided the raw materials for its recovery.

The total recovery took roughly 100,000-170,000 years (Piedrahita et al., 2025)—fast by geological standards, but unimaginably slow by human ones. The lesson here is sobering. Even with dramatically enhanced weathering, the Earth system required order-of-magnitude longer than human civilization has existed to restore climate equilibrium. Negative feedbacks exist and they work, but not on timescales relevant to human planning.

## What We Still Don't Know

Three decades of intensive research have transformed our understanding of the PETM, yet fundamental uncertainties persist. The relative contributions of different carbon sources—direct volcanic degassing, thermogenic methane from contact metamorphism, seafloor hydrate dissociation, organic matter oxidation—remain contested (Kender et al., 2021; Dickens, 2011). Estimates of total carbon release span a factor of three, from roughly 3,000 to 10,000 gigatons. The duration of the onset phase—critical for determining carbon release rates and climate sensitivity—is estimated anywhere from 3,000 to 20,000 years depending on methodology and location (Kirtland Turner et al., 2017; Li et al., 2022).

Part of the problem is equifinality: multiple combinations of processes could produce observationally similar outcomes. If you don't know precisely how much methane hydrate existed, or exactly how organic-rich the sediments were that sills intruded, you can't definitively partition the carbon budget among different sources. The isotopic constraints help, but they're not unique—different mixtures of sources with different isotopic signatures can produce the same net signal.

More fundamentally, we face the challenge of reconstructing ancient Earth system states with imperfect proxies. We don't know precisely what Paleocene atmospheric carbon dioxide levels were before the PETM began, which affects our estimates of climate sensitivity. We don't have detailed maps of pre-event methane hydrate distributions. We can't directly measure the rate at which heat penetrated marine sediments 56 million years ago. Every reconstruction involves assumptions, and those assumptions cascade through our interpretations.

There's also a persistent model-data mismatch that hasn't been fully resolved. Climate models driven by carbon dioxide increases consistent with carbon cycle constraints struggle to reproduce the full 5-8°C of PETM warming (Richard E. Zeebe et al., 2009). Either pre-event carbon dioxide levels were lower than assumed (making the system more sensitive), or non-carbon dioxide feedbacks (water vapor, clouds, methane) were more important than models capture, or something else is missing entirely. The inability of models to fully explain PETM warming raises questions about their reliability for projecting future warming under ongoing carbon emissions.

## The Contemporary Resonance

The PETM represents the closest geological analog to what humans are doing to the climate, but with a crucial caveat: we're doing it faster. The PETM carbon release occurred over at least a few thousand years. Modern anthropogenic emissions are on track to release comparable amounts of carbon within a few centuries. We don't have a direct geological precedent for perturbations at this rate.

This matters because rate affects outcome. Faster carbon release allows less time for the ocean to buffer atmospheric carbon dioxide levels, leading to higher peak concentrations and more severe acidification. It allows less time for ecosystems to adapt or migrate. It potentially activates threshold responses that slower perturbations might not trigger. The PETM shows us what happens when large amounts of carbon enter the atmosphere-ocean system quickly; we're running the experiment faster.

The feedback mechanisms documented in the PETM are particularly relevant. Methane hydrate destabilization, permafrost carbon release, enhanced organic matter oxidation in warming soils—these are all feedbacks that could operate in a warming modern world. The PETM demonstrates that once activated, such feedbacks can sustain elevated temperatures for geological durations even after the initial trigger ceases. The ocean acidification, deoxygenation, and ecosystem disruptions documented 56 million years ago could recur.

Yet the PETM also demonstrates recovery. The Earth system has mechanisms—primarily silicate weathering—that can restore equilibrium after massive carbon perturbations. Life survived and ultimately diversified. The event was catastrophic by any human-relevant timescale, but it wasn't terminal. This is cold comfort for anyone hoping to maintain current ecosystems and coastal cities, but it does suggest that Earth itself will persist.

What we don't know is where the thresholds lie. How much warming triggers hydrate dissociation? How much carbon release is needed to flip the ocean into a persistently deoxygenated state? The PETM crossed these thresholds under conditions somewhat different from today's, and the crossing was difficult to predict even with the benefit of hindsight. We lack the detailed mechanistic understanding needed to know precisely where those boundaries are in the modern system.

---

The hydrothermal vents drilled in 2021 tell a more complicated story than the one we started with. The PETM wasn't simply volcanoes or methane—it was volcanoes triggering methane, methane amplifying warming, warming driving acidification and anoxia, weathering eventually drawing down the excess carbon. It was a cascade, a system pushed past multiple thresholds into an altered state that persisted for nearly 200,000 years before negative feedbacks could restore equilibrium.

Understanding this cascade matters because we're currently pushing the same system. Not with volcanism, but with fossil carbon—the compressed remains of ancient photosynthesis, isotopically light, capable of driving the same kind of excursion our instruments record in 56-million-year-old sediments. The PETM doesn't tell us exactly what will happen, because we're doing it faster and starting from different conditions. But it tells us what's possible. And it tells us that recovery, when it comes, operates on timescales utterly alien to human experience.
